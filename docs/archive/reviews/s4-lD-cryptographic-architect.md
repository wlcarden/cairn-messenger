# §4 — Cryptographic Architect Lens

## Summary

§4 is, on the whole, more honest than typical security-product orientation prose: it names trust roots (line 246), distinguishes wire-level FS from at-rest decryptability (line 271), and avoids absolute language ("verified through chain of attestations" rather than "secure"). The principal cryptographic concern is not over-claiming primitives — the primitives are correctly named — but over-claiming what _combinations_ of primitives achieve against the specific adversaries §3 names. Several §4 statements describe the _attack chain_ a primitive interrupts using language that subtly elides the steps the primitive does _not_ close, or that load-bears on conditions §4 does not surface (challenge storage location, honest-majority of reviewers and witnesses, OIDC-provider integrity, user availability during the 48-hour window).

The pattern is consistent: §4 makes integration-level claims of the form "X defeats Y," where the §5 substrate supports the weaker claim "X bounds Y conditional on Z₁…Zₙ." For a cryptographic reviewer, this is the failure mode that erodes trust between brief and spec — the cryptographic claims are technically supportable but rhetorically out of step with what the substrate guarantees. Narrowing five specific phrases would close the gap without weakening the brief's persuasive structure.

## Critical findings

### F1: "Defeat impersonation attacks" overstates D0005 against a continuous-control adversary

- **Evidence:** Line 254 says recovery is mitigated by "pre-shared challenges and a 48-hour cooling-off window per D0005 — that defeat impersonation attacks." D0005 itself rejects each mechanism individually on the grounds that one defeats impersonation, the other defeats real-time pressure (D0005 lines 37–39: "delay-and-confirm only… does nothing against same-day voice-cloning impersonation"). The combination raises the cost; it does not "defeat" a state-actor adversary who can hold the user under continuous control for 48 hours AND extract the per-peer challenge under coercion. §5.3 line 414 acknowledges this surface as "a residual leak rather than an absence of leakage." §5.3 line 418 calls it "layered resistance," not defeat.
- **Impact:** A reader holding the threat tier in mind — state actors, Pegasus operators, forensic-extraction-capable adversaries — will note that "defeat" is the wrong verb for a layered defense whose layers each fall under conditions §3 explicitly models. This is the exact rhetorical pattern that erodes a cryptographic reviewer's confidence: a strong claim in §4 that softens to "bounded resistance" by §5.3.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "defeat impersonation attacks" with the language §5.3 actually uses — "raise the cost of impersonation attacks substantially, with explicit residual surfaces named in §5.3" or "bound impersonation attacks except under continuous-control adversaries." The phrasing in §4.3 line 285 ("defeats the attack chain") needs the same narrowing.

### F2: §4.3 line 285's "attack chain" elides the on-device challenge storage option

- **Evidence:** Line 285 says D0005 "defeats the attack chain that compelled unlock would otherwise open: unlock → peer enumeration → impersonation." Three problems with this framing against §5.3:
  1. Peer enumeration from a compelled-unlock device succeeds via contact list and recovery-app state; §5.2 line 382 only excludes peer relationships from the _public_ trust graph, not from on-device state. The attack chain's middle step is not broken.
  2. D0005 line 18 and §5.3 line 404 permit challenges to be stored in "a hardware-backed password manager" — which, if it lives on the seized device, means unlock _does_ yield the challenges. The chain unlock → peer enumeration → impersonation succeeds in this configuration.
  3. What D0005 actually defeats is the _naive_ impersonation chain absent off-device challenge storage AND absent continuous user control. §4.3 should not assert full attack-chain defeat.
- **Impact:** A cryptographic reviewer reading this against §5.3 will identify the gap immediately. The brief's credibility on recovery security depends on the recovery section's honesty about layered, conditional defense; §4.3 line 285 frames it as deterministic.
- **Recommendation:** Narrow to "raises the cost of the unlock → peer enumeration → impersonation chain, with residual surfaces in §3.3 and §5.3." Or, if the brief wants a stronger statement, explicitly condition it: "defeats the unlock → impersonation chain when challenges are stored off-device and the user can issue a cancellation within 48 hours."

### F3: "The user does not have to trust the developer's word" replaces developer trust with reviewer-pool + witness-pool + OIDC trust without naming the substitution

- **Evidence:** Line 287: "The user does not have to trust the developer's word that a release is genuine; the user (or anyone) can check the public record." This implies developer trust is _eliminated_. Per §5.5, what is actually achieved is that developer trust is _replaced with_ an honest-majority assumption over the 5+ reviewer pool (§5.5 line 484: 3-of-5 threshold), an honest-majority assumption over the witness pool that cosigns Sigsum log state (§5.5 line 490 explicitly acknowledges shared witness pool as a correlation risk affecting both trust-graph and release-binary integrity simultaneously), AND trust in the OIDC provider's resistance to legal compulsion (§5.5 line 488: "v1 uses a U.S.-based OIDC provider in pilot… with the explicit acknowledgment that this places U.S. legal process in the effective trust surface").
- **Impact:** A reviewer who reads §5.5 after §4.3 will register the gap: §4 says developer trust is replaced by public verifiability; §5.5 says developer trust is replaced by a different distributed-trust assumption that includes named, jurisdictionally-bound parties. The §5.5 framing is the cryptographically defensible one; §4.3's framing is the marketing-cleaner one. For an audience including funders evaluating residual trust, the gap matters.
- **Recommendation:** Rephrase to name the substitution: "the user trusts the honest majority of an enumerated reviewer and witness pool plus the OIDC provider's jurisdictional posture, rather than the developer's single signing identity. The trust is distributed and publicly auditable, not eliminated." This matches the framing §3.4 trust-roots and §5.5 use.

## Significant findings

### F4: §4.2 line 271 names forward secrecy but omits post-compromise security, a different property with different coverage

- **Evidence:** Line 271: "On-wire message content is forward-secret via SimpleX's double-ratchet derivative (and Briar's BTP/BSP in v1.5). At-rest message history on the device remains decryptable under unlock." §5.4 line 438 specifies SimpleX provides "forward secrecy _and post-compromise security_… past traffic cannot be decrypted if a current session key is compromised, _and_ the protocol self-heals via the asymmetric ratchet step after compromise." §4.2 names only the FS half. PCS is the property that bounds the _forward_ damage of a session-key compromise; FS bounds the _backward_ damage. These cover different threat scenarios.
- **Impact:** For the state-actor / Pegasus tier, PCS is arguably the more operationally relevant property (after a temporary endpoint compromise, the conversation self-heals). Omitting it from §4 underspecifies the protection the integrated product offers. A cryptographic reviewer notices because the absence is conspicuous — naming FS without PCS is a tell that the writer either forgot PCS or chose not to claim it.
- **Recommendation:** "On-wire message content is forward-secret _and post-compromise-secure_ via SimpleX's double-ratchet derivative." The §5.4 substrate supports this without further work.

### F5: "Bounded compromise through tier separation" frames a temporal property as a structural one

- **Evidence:** Line 269: "No single credential gives an adversary access to everything… Compromise at one tier bounds rather than collapses the user's cryptographic position." Against §5.1 line 305 and line 311, the master _is_ present on the device at provisioning and during the rotation/recovery flow — including rotation initiated under coercion. The "bounded" property is bounded-_window_ exposure, which §5.1 line 305 explicitly names. The §4.2 framing reads as "structural separation prevents single-credential compromise," which is true outside the reconstruction windows but not during them. §4.2 closes with "bounded-window exposure, not zero exposure," which partially closes the gap — but the lead sentence describes tier separation as if it were the bounding mechanism, when in fact tier separation plus _operational discipline about when reconstruction happens_ is the bounding mechanism.
- **Impact:** A reviewer reading the lead claim ("no single credential gives an adversary access to everything") and then reaching §5.1 line 311 ("rotation under coercion is itself a vulnerable moment… the master is reconstructed in active memory on the device under coercion") will register the gap. The honest framing is in the principle's closing sentence; the lead overstates it.
- **Recommendation:** Restructure the paragraph to lead with the bounded-window framing and present tier separation as the mechanism that makes the window bounded rather than as the property itself. E.g., "Tier separation makes compromise _bounded in scope and time_ rather than total: outside the brief reconstruction windows at provisioning and recovery, the master is not on the device to extract."

### F6: Sigsum "anchoring" claims need precision about commitment-only logging

- **Evidence:** Line 253: "the cryptographic trust graph (Section 5.2) that propagates attestations, revocations, introductions, and key rotations across the user's social network, anchored in Sigsum commitments." The phrase "anchored in Sigsum commitments" is technically correct but underspecifies what Sigsum provides: per §5.2 line 372, Sigsum holds _commitments_ (hashes) of operations, not operation content. The log proves existence and order, not content; equivocation detection is on the prior-hash chain over commitments. §4 does not say "operations are propagated through Sigsum" — but a reader who skips §5.2 might infer that Sigsum is the propagation channel, when in fact propagation is via SimpleX user-to-user traffic (§5.2 line 368), and Sigsum is the audit anchor.
- **Impact:** Minor in isolation; cumulative with F1–F3 in that it contributes to an impression that §4 elides the operational reality of how the integrated system actually works. A cryptographic reviewer evaluating witness-pool risk in §5.5 line 490 will want to see §4 frame Sigsum precisely as a _commitment_ anchor, not a propagation substrate.
- **Recommendation:** Either inline "anchored in commitment-only entries in Sigsum (per §5.2)" or rely on Section 5.2 to clarify and remove "anchored in Sigsum commitments" from §4.1 line 253 to avoid the under-specification. The former is preferred since it surfaces the architectural commitment.

## Minor findings

### F7: "The release security in Section 5.5 only works because the operational discipline in Section 5.6 makes users likely to actually verify" overstates the conditionality

- **Evidence:** Line 289. §5.5's release-security stack provides _post-hoc auditability_ even for users who do not verify at install time — the log is permanent, reviewers and witnesses keep watching, anomalies surface at the population level. A user who installs without verifying still benefits from the population-level detection property. §4.3's framing implies the stack provides zero value without user verification, which is the targeted-attack property only.
- **Impact:** Minor; the composition argument's logical shape is correct (these layers reinforce each other), but the specific phrasing understates the value of release security for non-verifying users.
- **Recommendation:** "The targeted-attack property of release security in Section 5.5 only works when users actually verify; the population-level audit property holds regardless."

### F8: SimpleX "no persistent user identity at the protocol level" needs the multi-device caveat from §5.4

- **Evidence:** Line 250: "SimpleX is the v1 messaging substrate — identifier-less, queue-based, with no persistent user identity at the protocol level." §5.4 line 442 caveats this: "A user who connects multiple devices to the same set of queues creates correlatable activity at those queues regardless of how the identifiers are constructed." v1 is single-device per D0007, so the caveat does not apply at v1 — but the brief is making architectural commitments that read forward to v2 multi-device, and the §4 phrasing as a flat property invites misreading at v2.
- **Impact:** Minimal at v1; potentially significant once multi-device returns. Not worth retitling the paragraph for, but worth a clause.
- **Recommendation:** "no persistent user identity at the protocol level _in the v1 single-device configuration_." Defers the multi-device complication to the v2 architecture work where it lives.

## Patterns

Two patterns recur across F1, F2, F3, F4, and F6:

1. **Verb inflation at the §4 layer.** Statements that §5 carefully phrases as "raises the cost of," "bounds," "complicates," or "layered resistance" become "defeats," "eliminates," or "the user does not have to trust" at the §4 layer. This is the inverse of the calibration §4.2 line 273 commits to ("Calibrated language replaces absolute-sounding claims"). The brief's own principle indicts its own §4 prose. Resolution is mechanical: scan §4 for verbs of complete elimination and swap them for the §5 vocabulary that already exists.

2. **Trust-substitution framed as trust-elimination.** F3 is the clearest case; F1 and F2 are softer versions of the same pattern. The integrated product replaces _one_ trust placement (e.g., developer signing identity, master-key custody) with _a distributed set_ of trust placements (reviewer pool, witness pool, OIDC provider, recovery peers, pre-shared challenge storage). The cryptographic and operational arguments for this substitution are strong — but §4 sometimes frames the substitution as elimination, which is both less defensible and less interesting than the actual architectural commitment. Reframing as "this trust placement is replaced by _that_ distributed-and-auditable one" is more honest _and_ more compelling to the cryptographic audience.

A third, weaker pattern (F4, F6, F8): **load-bearing properties named in §5 are omitted from §4's compressed restatement**, where the omission changes what threat coverage the §4 description suggests. PCS, commitment-only logging, multi-device correlatability — each is in §5 but not in §4. Some omissions are appropriate compression; the named cases are not, because they change the threat-model framing for a reader who reads §4 in isolation.
