# Architecture Simplification — Operational Maintenance Cost Lens

## Summary

The Cairn v1 design absorbs an architectural surface that was sized for a funded foundation operating at quarterly cadence, then operates that surface from a solo-developer volunteer baseline at "as available" cadence (D0008). The mismatch concentrates ongoing maintenance load on the single developer in three ways: (a) audit re-coordination scope that compounds each cycle (the pre-pilot audit's four-component scope per §8.5 / D0011 plus the second cycle commitment in §8.5's "Continuous review"); (b) upstream-roadmap tracking obligations that fire on the upstream's calendar, not Cairn's (Tor pluggable transports per §5.4, SimpleX/Briar protocol versioning, GrapheneOS/Pixel firmware drift per §9.2); (c) partner-coordination operations beyond §8.6's outreach baseline (reviewer-pool rotation per §8.2 at 18-month cadence; Sigsum witness-pool engagement; foundation governance per §8.4).

The Phase D arithmetic in §10.4 names this honestly — the foundation pays every contributor except the engineer-operator who runs it — but the upstream architectural commitments that produce that load are made in §5 without any operational-cost budget attached. The findings below identify commitments whose ongoing per-cycle cost is disproportionate to their v1 value at the volunteer baseline, with specific attention to the audit scope expansion enabled by D0011 and the upstream-dependency surface created by §5.4's pluggable-transport tracking, §5.5's reviewer-pool rotation, and §5.7's bilingual build chain.

## Critical findings (commitments whose ongoing cost > v1 value)

### F1: Pre-pilot cryptographic audit scope (D0011 / §8.5) — four components for v1 value of one

- **Commitment:** §8.5 / D0011: pre-pilot audit scope covers (1) COSE_Sign1 envelope verification, (2) Shamir SSS reconstruction with memory hygiene, (3) trust-graph operation envelope nine-field schema and signature chain including issuer-cert-hash binding, (4) recovery-flow cryptographic operations. Budget $15-30K subsidized; 1-2 person-weeks senior auditor time.
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** Each audit cycle requires (a) developer prep — producing the audit packet, scoping discussions, response-and-remediation work — estimated at 2-4 weeks of developer time per cycle from the §10.4 unenumerated operational load; (b) subsidy-program application cycles of 3-9 months per §10.2 with no guarantee of close; (c) §8.5's "Continuous review post-launch" commits to a second audit "approximately 18-24 months after the first," meaning the cycle repeats indefinitely on a 18-24 month period, and v1.5 additions (Briar, reproducible builds, local caching) expand the next scope rather than retire any of the v1 scope.
- **v1 value:** The cryptographic-correctness assurance v1 actually needs at pilot scale (10-15 users per §6.3) is dominated by component (1) COSE_Sign1 envelope and (4) recovery-flow crypto — the two surfaces where Cairn does original construction that no upstream audit covers. Component (2) Shamir over GF(256) is a well-understood primitive whose memory hygiene is the actual delta (and that hygiene is a Rust-core property auditable independently of Shamir math). Component (3) is largely a schema check.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Excess.** The §8.5 "Audit explicitly does not duplicate upstream-project audits" framing applies to SimpleX/Briar/Sigstore/Sigsum but not to the audit scope's own internal decomposition. The four-component scope expands the audit packet, the response surface, and the per-cycle prep cost without proportional v1-value gain.
- **Recommendation:** **Simplify.** Narrow pre-pilot audit scope to COSE_Sign1 envelope construction and recovery-flow cryptographic operations explicitly, with Shamir reconstruction-and-memory-hygiene split out as a Rust-core-properties check the developer can demonstrate through `cargo test` + the property-based test framework rather than as a separate audit item. The trust-graph nine-field schema is closed-form and check-able from the source review reviewers already do per §5.5; it does not need separate cryptographic audit treatment at v1 scale. D0011's contingency clause ("If pre-pilot audit funding does not close, the project either reduces pre-pilot scope further (e.g., capability-token construction only)...") already names a narrower scope as defensible; the brief should adopt that narrower scope as the v1 default rather than as the contingency.

### F2: Reviewer-pool rotation at 18-month cadence (§8.2) — partner-coordination burden compounds per cycle

- **Commitment:** §8.2: "The project targets an 18-month rotation cadence with overlap between outgoing and incoming reviewers so attestation continuity is not disrupted." 5+ pool, 3-of-5 threshold per §5.5. Geographic and institutional diversity recruitment criteria.
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) Recruitment outreach for replacement reviewer(s) on a 18-month wall-clock cycle — partner-coordination work at the §8.6 cost scale; (b) onboarding to the reviewer toolkit (semi-annual LTS refresh per §8.2 at volunteer baseline, per-release at honoraria baseline); (c) overlap-period coordination so attestation continuity is not disrupted; (d) Q5 NGO-partner-outreach dependency means recruitment is gated on relationships outside the project's control. §9.1's reviewer-and-witness-pool-erosion risk is explicitly named as a longer-term risk.
- **v1 value:** v1 releases at median 4-6 month cadence per D0008. Across an 18-month window, that's 3-4 releases. The rotation cadence delivers margin against single-reviewer compromise and reviewer fatigue, but at v1 release volume the rotation cycle imposes a recruitment-and-onboarding obligation that fires roughly as often as new-pool recruitment did initially.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Excess at v1 scale.** The rotation cadence is calibrated to a foundation operating quarterly releases with honoraria; at the volunteer-attestation baseline the rotation cycle's compounded coordination cost exceeds the marginal security gain over an unrotated pool of the same composition. Reviewer fatigue at 3-4 releases/cycle is materially different from reviewer fatigue at 6 releases/cycle.
- **Recommendation:** **Defer or accept asymmetry.** Defer the rotation commitment to Phase C honoraria-funded operations, when quarterly cadence is feasible and the rotation cycle's coordination cost is funded as honoraria-budgeted partner coordination rather than absorbed by the developer. At the volunteer baseline, the commitment should be "reviewers may rotate at their own initiative; the project does not impose a rotation cycle." This trades a security-property posture against operational sustainability and §9.1's solo-developer SPOF risk; the trade is in the right direction given §10.4's named Phase D sustainability cliff.

### F3: Pluggable-transport tracking (§5.4) — upstream-roadmap dependency with no exit

- **Commitment:** §5.4: "Pluggable transports (obfs4, meek, webtunnel, snowflake, whichever the Tor Project's current guidance indicates)... are an ongoing engineering commitment, not a one-time decision: transport choices appropriate at v1 release may be blocked by v2 because DPI evasion is a continuously-being-solved problem rather than a solved one. The project commits to tracking Tor Project transport guidance and prioritizing transport-update releases when active blocking is observed in pilot or partner jurisdictions."
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) Continuous tracking of Tor Project transport-recommendation evolution — measured in hours-per-week of awareness work, not weeks-per-quarter of integration work; (b) emergency-release-path execution per §8.2 (2-of-5 attestation, with 3-of-5 follow-up) when active blocking is observed in pilot or partner jurisdictions — this is a partner-driven trigger the developer does not control; (c) reviewer-pool engagement on emergency-cadence releases imposes its own partner-coordination cost beyond the §8.6 baseline. The trigger frequency is not under project control — it fires on adversary-jurisdiction operational tempo.
- **v1 value:** Pilot is 10-15 users in 1-2 local groups (§6.3) typically known to the developer. The probability that pilot-jurisdiction DPI environments materially evolve over a 6-month pilot is non-zero but bounded; transport-tracking value at pilot scale is dominated by the initial transport selection at v1 release, not by ongoing cadence.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Excess for v1 pilot phase; appropriate post-broader-release.** The commitment shape ("ongoing engineering commitment, not a one-time decision") is correct for v2+ when the user base spans multiple DPI-active jurisdictions; for v1 pilot it imposes a tracking obligation whose realized trigger probability is low and whose missed-trigger cost is bounded to "pilot users in DPI jurisdictions are offline until an updated transport propagates" — a real cost but one the pilot can be designed around rather than continuously defended against.
- **Recommendation:** **Defer the continuous-tracking commitment to broader-release Phase C/D.** For v1 pilot, the commitment is: "transport selection at release; transport updates as Tor Project releases recommended changes (not as DPI environments evolve in real time)." Reframe §5.4's "ongoing engineering commitment" as a Phase D operational item rather than a v1 commitment, and acknowledge in §9.2 that v1 pilot users in DPI jurisdictions may be offline during transport-update gaps as a v1 limitation rather than as a commitment-failure.

### F4: Project-operated SimpleX crash-reporting queue (§5.7, §6.1) — infrastructure operation contradicts §4.2 minimal-project-operated principle

- **Commitment:** §5.7 / §6.1: opt-in crash reporting delivered through SimpleX to a "Cairn-team-controlled SimpleX queue" with consent flow at provisioning and intake/triage infrastructure in v1. §6.1 explicitly names this as "the one acknowledged exception to the §4.2 minimal-project-operated-infrastructure principle."
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) Queue operation — SimpleX queue maintenance, monitoring, availability against the SimpleX network's own changes; (b) intake-and-triage of crash reports as they arrive — this is real ops work at any non-zero report volume, and crash reports are by their nature unpredictable in arrival pattern; (c) consent-flow maintenance as the on-wire schema or the consent text evolves; (d) §4.2 exception means this is a place the project carries operational liability the rest of the architecture is designed to avoid.
- **v1 value:** At 10-15 pilot users with developer-led facilitation (§6.3), crash reports are operationally redundant with direct facilitator-user communication. The facilitator (the developer) already has direct contact channels with every pilot user; "encrypted crash reports delivered through SimpleX to a queue the developer triages" is a more elaborate version of "the pilot user tells the developer the app crashed."
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Excess at v1 scale.** The infrastructure shape is correct for broader-release scale where direct facilitator-user channels do not exist; at pilot scale it duplicates an existing channel at meaningful ongoing operational cost while creating a §4.2 exception the project named explicitly because the exception is uncomfortable.
- **Recommendation:** **Defer to v1.5/v2 broader-release.** v1 crash reporting reverts to "pilot users report crashes through their direct channel to the facilitator (the developer); crash report format is unstructured." Architectural slot for structured opt-in encrypted crash reporting via project-operated queue moves to v1.5 (where Briar integration and reproducible builds are already changing the infrastructure surface) or v2 (broader release). The §4.2 principle stays clean; the developer's pilot-phase operational load drops by the entire crash-infrastructure stack.

## Significant findings

### F5: Sigsum integration for trust-graph operations (§5.2) — log participation as ongoing dependency

- **Commitment:** §5.2: trust-graph operations submitted to Sigsum at issuance; witness-cosigned; NGO partners and academic auditors as Sigsum witnesses. §3.4 acknowledges shared-witness-pool concentration.
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) Sigsum log-state monitoring — if a witness pool member departs or stops cosigning, the project notices and addresses it (the §9.1 reviewer-and-witness-pool-erosion risk applies here); (b) witness recruitment is partner-coordination work overlapping with reviewer-pool recruitment per §8.6; (c) if the project participates in operating Sigsum log infrastructure rather than only consuming it (§10.4 line item: "log-operator participation (if the project operates its own Sigsum log, the higher end; if the project participates in public logs, the lower end)"), the operational cost is named but the operational scope is not specified.
- **v1 value:** Sigsum provides the externality property (commitments are public, witnesses are independent) that justifies the trust-graph architecture's "no central authority" claim. At v1 pilot scale of 10-15 users, the trust-graph operation volume is small; the witness pool is doing detection-against-equivocation work whose value depends on the size of the user base, not on the volume of operations.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Proportionate but front-loaded.** The witness-pool recruitment cost is the same regardless of v1 user volume; the per-cycle ongoing cost (monitoring, addressing erosion) is real but bounded.
- **Recommendation:** **Accept with cost-visibility.** Surface the witness-pool-management ongoing cost in §10.4 explicitly (it is currently bundled into "infrastructure" rather than into partner-coordination operations) so the operational scope is named rather than implicit. Defer the §3.4-acknowledged separation of witness pools (trust-graph witnesses vs. release-log witnesses) to v1.5+ rather than treating it as a v1 commitment — at v1 the shared-witness-pool correlation is documented as a known limitation.

### F6: Property-based migration test framework (§5.7) — testing burden the developer absorbs

- **Commitment:** §5.7: "The Cairn-specific store is SQLite with explicit schema versioning and migration tests using property-based round-trip verification through each migration step." §8.5 / §10.1 commit to property-based testing for trust-graph CRDT and operation envelope, fuzz testing for COSE/CBOR parsers, Shamir reconstruction, capability-token verifier; known-answer tests; differential testing against SimpleX reference.
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) Test framework maintenance against Rust ecosystem evolution (`proptest` / `quickcheck` API changes, fuzzing-tool churn); (b) per-release the test suite must execute against the current schema-migration set, growing as schema versions accumulate; (c) flaky-test triage absorbs developer time per-cycle in proportion to test surface area; (d) the differential-testing-against-SimpleX-reference commitment requires tracking SimpleX upstream changes — overlapping with the §5.4 upstream-tracking surface.
- **v1 value:** Property-based testing is materially more valuable than example-based testing for the schemas Cairn defines (CBOR-encoded envelopes, trust-graph operations). The cryptographic value of this testing approach is real and v1-load-bearing — it's the testing layer that makes the §10.1 self-audit-and-tooling commitment plausibly sufficient between external audits.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Proportionate but with under-acknowledged maintenance scope.** The framework's setup cost is named in §10.1 implicitly; the ongoing cost is not budgeted anywhere.
- **Recommendation:** **Accept commitment; name the ongoing-maintenance cost explicitly.** Add to §10.4 unenumerated operational load: "test infrastructure maintenance (property-based test suite evolution, fuzzing harness maintenance, differential-test-against-SimpleX upstream tracking)" so the Phase D scope acknowledges it. No architectural change; the simplification is to honest cost-naming, not to scope reduction.

### F7: UnifiedPush distributor selection + battery-aware polling (§5.4, §6.1) — UX-surface ongoing maintenance

- **Commitment:** §5.4 / §6.1: UnifiedPush as the push-architecture choice; v1 ships push off by default; client polls at user-configurable intervals (default 15 minutes); battery-aware polling-loop implementation; UnifiedPush distributor selection UX; opt-in flow during provisioning.
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) UnifiedPush distributor ecosystem evolution — distributors come and go, distributor protocol evolves; (b) battery-aware polling implementation must adapt to Android battery-management changes per Android version; (c) per-release UX surface for distributor selection means UI maintenance against UnifiedPush ecosystem; (d) the v1.5 "may revisit the default" framing (§5.4) means the push posture itself is an open question that depends on pilot feedback.
- **v1 value:** v1 ships push OFF by default — the UnifiedPush integration is exposed as user-configurable but is not on the operational critical path for v1 pilot users who accept the polling default. The active-push opt-in surface is needed at pilot scale only for users whose operational rhythm requires near-real-time delivery (§9.2 "Push notifications are off by default in v1").
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Excess at v1.** The full UnifiedPush distributor-selection UX plus battery-aware polling implementation is calibrated for a broader-release product where users routinely opt in; at v1 pilot scale where the default is off and the facilitator (the developer) is present for every provisioning conversation, distributor-selection UX is duplicative work.
- **Recommendation:** **Simplify v1 to polling-only.** Defer UnifiedPush distributor-selection UX to v1.5 where pilot feedback either confirms or refutes the polling-default tradeoff. v1 ships polling-only; the architectural slot for UnifiedPush is preserved per §6.4 forward-compatibility. Pilot users requiring active push are managed by the facilitator (the developer) on a per-user basis through the in-person provisioning ceremony, not through a generic distributor-selection UI. Architectural commitment to UnifiedPush as the eventual protocol is unchanged.

### F8: Bilingual Rust + Kotlin maintenance via UniFFI (§5.7, D0003) — per-cycle integration testing cost

- **Commitment:** D0003 / §5.7: Rust core + Kotlin UI with UniFFI binding generation. "UniFFI binding maintenance, integration testing at the Rust/Kotlin boundary, and Rust↔Kotlin reproducible-build coordination are explicit ongoing components of the v1 working set."
- **Ongoing per-cycle cost:** (a) UniFFI version tracking — upstream changes can break binding generation; (b) Rust toolchain pin tracking against UniFFI's supported set; (c) Kotlin/Android-Gradle-Plugin version tracking against UniFFI's supported set; (d) integration-test surface at the Rust/Kotlin boundary; (e) reproducible-build coordination across two language toolchains (v1.5 commitment) doubles the toolchain-version-pinning work; (f) language-skill maintenance — bilingual development means the developer's cognitive cost per task is higher than single-language work.
- **v1 value:** D0003 frames Rust-core as a security choice (Rust's memory model gives explicit control over secret-material lifetime); §5.7 names the specific security properties (`zeroize`, `secrecy`, `subtle`, typestate). The v1 value is real and load-bearing for the §3.3 endpoint-surface threat model.
- **Cost / value verdict:** **Proportionate at v1 but cliff at v1.5.** The bilingual cost is justified at v1 scope by the security properties; the cliff is when v1.5 adds reproducible-build coordination across both toolchains. The combined per-cycle cost at v1.5 is materially higher than v1 and is currently not budgeted in §10.3.
- **Recommendation:** **Accept commitment; surface the v1.5 cliff explicitly.** Add to §10.3 Phase C contingency: "If reproducible-build pipeline cost exceeds the v1.5 budget, single-language artifact reproducibility (Rust core only; Kotlin UI as non-reproducible) is the fallback rather than abandoning reproducible builds entirely." This is a smaller scope cut than the alternative of language-stack revisitation but names the maintenance-cliff defensively.

## Minor findings

### F9: Foundation incorporation per D0010 — legal-consultation cost is one-time but Phase D overhead is per-cycle

§10.4 names "$10,000-30,000 per year for board governance, accounting, fiscal infrastructure, regulatory compliance, jurisdiction-specific tax filings." This is correctly scoped as Phase D and is named honestly. The v1 finding here is only that §6.1 implicit work (none) and §8.4 path-to-foundation work (substantial in Phase B/C) should not be conflated. No simplification recommendation; the cost is appropriately deferred.

### F10: Multi-channel distribution (§5.5: F-Droid, Accrescent, Tor onion, offline images) — release-management coordination per release

§5.5 commits to four distribution channels at v1; §6.1 narrows to "F-Droid, Accrescent, and direct download from a project-controlled domain." The Tor onion service is named for §5.5's "Distribution and supply-chain surface" defense and appears in §6.2 deferred to v1.5+ (implicitly, since §6.1 omits it). The simplification recommendation: explicitly confirm §6.1's three-channel v1 scope and defer Tor onion service distribution + offline signed images to v1.5/v2 with the §3.3 supply-chain-surface implication acknowledged as a v1 limitation.

### F11: Long-lived APK signing key (§5.5) — key-management ongoing cost

The hardware-security-token-held long-lived APK key requires per-release ceremony, periodic hardware-token rotation against Android's expectations, and incident-response readiness against the compromise scenario named in §5.5 (APK Signature Scheme v3 key rotation). The per-cycle cost is real but bounded. v1 value justifies the commitment (signature continuity is non-negotiable for Android update mechanics). No simplification; this is fundamental.

## Patterns

**Pattern 1: Architectural commitments calibrated to foundation-operated quarterly cadence, operated at volunteer "as-available" cadence.** F2 (reviewer rotation), F3 (pluggable-transport tracking), F4 (crash-reporting queue), F7 (UnifiedPush UX) all share this shape — the architecture's per-cycle obligation fires on a calendar the solo developer does not set, against a release cadence (D0008's median 4-6 months) that has no honoraria-funded slack to absorb the obligation. Recommendation pattern: defer the continuous-operation commitment to Phase C (honoraria-funded reviewer pool, UX engineer per §10.3) and reframe v1 obligations as point-in-time at release rather than continuous.

**Pattern 2: Audit scope expansion enabled by D0011's two-stage approach without per-stage scope discipline.** F1 (pre-pilot audit four-component scope) plus §8.5's "Continuous review post-launch... a second external cryptographic audit approximately 18-24 months after the first" creates a compounding audit-coordination cost that is not budgeted in Phase D. D0011 already names a narrower scope as the contingency-mode default; the simplification is to make the contingency-mode scope the v1 baseline and treat the expanded scope as the well-funded-case option.

**Pattern 3: Upstream-dependency surface ongoing cost is acknowledged in §9.1/9.2 but not budgeted in §10.4.** F3 (Tor pluggable transports), F5 (Sigsum witness pool), F6 (SimpleX differential testing), F8 (UniFFI / Rust / Kotlin / AGP version coordination) all impose ongoing tracking obligations against upstream calendars. §10.4's "unenumerated operational load" mentions partner coordination and audit coordination but does not enumerate upstream-tracking. Simplification recommendation: add upstream-tracking as a named §10.4 line item so the Phase D sustainability cliff arithmetic is honest about it. Architectural commitments do not need to change; the cost-visibility does.

**Pattern 4: §4.2 minimal-project-operated-infrastructure principle has exactly one stated exception (§6.1 crash-reporting queue) but is implicitly violated by several other commitments at scale.** Sigsum log operation (§10.4's "if the project operates its own Sigsum log"), reviewer toolkit hosting (§8.2), v1.5 Tor onion service (§5.5), and the project-operated SimpleX queue per F4 each represent project-operated infrastructure the §4.2 principle disfavors. The simplification is to honor §4.2 strictly at v1 by deferring the project-operated SimpleX queue per F4 and confirming Sigsum-witness-only (not Sigsum-log-operator) posture at v1, with v1.5+ revisiting only if pilot operational evidence justifies the §4.2 exception.

## 150-word summary

The Cairn v1 architecture is calibrated to foundation-operated quarterly cadence but executed at volunteer "as-available" cadence (D0008), concentrating per-cycle maintenance load on the solo developer. Four critical findings: F1 — pre-pilot audit's four-component scope (D0011/§8.5) should narrow to COSE_Sign1 envelope and recovery-flow crypto only, with Shamir hygiene treated as a Rust-core property check; F2 — 18-month reviewer-pool rotation (§8.2) is excess at v1's 3-4-release cadence and should defer to honoraria-funded Phase C; F3 — §5.4's "ongoing engineering commitment" to Tor pluggable-transport tracking imposes continuous obligation on adversary-jurisdiction tempo and should defer to broader release; F4 — project-operated SimpleX crash-reporting queue duplicates the facilitator-user channel at pilot scale while creating the only stated §4.2 exception, and should defer. Pattern: §10.4 should enumerate upstream-tracking and witness-pool-management as named operational line items so the Phase D sustainability cliff arithmetic is honest about the per-cycle coordination cost the architecture currently absorbs implicitly.
