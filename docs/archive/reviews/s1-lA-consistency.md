# §1 — Cross-Section Consistency Lens

## Summary

§1 (lines 18–34) generally mirrors the deeper-section calibration well — phase-gate register, audience-honesty framing, residual-surface acknowledgment, conditional-vs-unilateral split. The consistency drifts cluster in three categories: (1) **factual compression errors** where §1 restates a quantitative parameter in a form that contradicts the deeper section (e.g., "5-of-N reviewer attestation threshold" in §1 line 26 conflicts with the "5+ pool / 3-of-5 threshold" specified in §5.5 line 506, §8.2 line 787, and §1 itself elsewhere); (2) **load-bearing qualifiers omitted** where §1 names a commitment but drops a conditional the deeper section makes structural (Briar's upstream-maintenance dependency per §7.1, v1.5 foundation incorporation as "materially in progress" not "complete" per §7.1, reviewer-pool recruitment as conditional on Q5 outreach but not Q3 funding per §8.2's volunteer-attestation baseline); (3) **non-promise list under-coverage** where §1's "What this brief is and is not" omits four §10.8 disclaimers (specific fiscal sponsor / audit firm / jurisdiction; figures-gross-of-fiscal-sponsor-fees; maintainer-compensation arrangement; BYOD hardware availability across jurisdictions) that a sophisticated funder would expect §1 to surface.

The most consequential drift is F1 below: a first-time reader who skims §1 alone will internalize a "5-of-N" attestation model and discover only at §5.5 that the actual parameter is "3-of-5" — a number that changes the security-properties story materially (compromise of 2 reviewers vs 3 is the difference §5.5 line 506 stakes the design on).

## Critical findings

### F1: §1 says "5-of-N reviewer attestation threshold" — §5.5 and §8.2 say "5+ pool, 3-of-5 threshold"

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26: "release security that substitutes a distributed trust set — a 5-of-N reviewer attestation threshold, witness cosignatures, OIDC provider integrity — for the developer's single signing identity." §5.5 line 506: "The reviewer pool ships with at least five recruited reviewers, with a threshold of three attestations required for a release to be considered properly verified. The 5+ pool size provides margin against single-reviewer compromise, attrition, and unavailability; the 3-of-5 threshold mirrors the Shamir parameter in 5.3 and means the compromise of two reviewers is insufficient to issue a false-but-quorum-attested release." §8.2 line 787: "Releases ship when 3-of-5 reviewer attestations form (the architectural threshold from Section 5.5)." §1 itself names the correct parameter once at line 28: "the 5+ membership and 3-of-5 attestation threshold targets specified in §5.5" — but that line is buried inside the v1 scope paragraph rather than in the architecture-commitments paragraph where §1 first introduces the parameter.
- **Impact:** First-time reader at the architecture-orientation level (§1 line 26) takes away "5-of-N" — a quorum-of-the-whole-pool framing that implies the pool itself is the threshold. The actual design (3-of-5 with 5+ recruited) is more permissive about pool size and more specific about the compromise budget (two-reviewer compromise insufficient). The technical-reviewer audience §1 names as a primary reader will notice the mismatch at §5.5 and discount §1's calibration accordingly.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "5-of-N reviewer attestation threshold" in §1 line 26 with "a 3-of-5 reviewer attestation threshold over a 5+ recruited pool" to match §5.5 line 506. Alternatively: "a multi-reviewer attestation threshold (3-of-5 over a 5+ pool per §5.5)."

### F2: §1 conflates reviewer-pool recruitment with Q3 funding; §8.2 specifies that the volunteer-attestation baseline is the pool's Phase A operating model

- **Evidence:** §1 line 30: "Reviewer-pool recruitment, partner-organization arrangements, and witness-pool composition are conditional on Q3 (funding for honoraria) and Q5 (NGO partner outreach) per Section 8.6." §8.2 line 791: "For the v1 self-funded-MVP baseline, the reviewer pool operates on a volunteer-attestation basis — reviewers contribute time because the project's mission aligns with their own work, with public acknowledgment in release notes as the recognition mechanism. Honoraria become the operational model once partnership or grant funding closes (Q3)." §10.1 line 1084: "Reviewer-pool recruitment outreach (Section 8.2 volunteer-attestation baseline): conversations, candidate evaluation, onboarding through the documented reviewer toolkit" — listed as a Phase A item, explicitly _not_ funding-gated. Q10 line 162: "Begins after Q3 (funding) provides enough certainty to offer reviewer honoraria, and after Q5 (NGO partner outreach) opens the partnership conversation" — Q10 itself drifts in the same direction §1 does, but §8.2 / §10.1 are the load-bearing specs.
- **Impact:** §1 implies the reviewer pool cannot form without funding. §8.2 / §10.1 specifically deny this: the volunteer-attestation pool is the Phase A operating reality, and §7.1 v1 ship-condition (d) — "first-quorum reviewer attestation per Section 8.2 / 5.5 forms" — is contingent on the volunteer pool forming, not on honoraria funding closing. A funder reading §1 misreads the funding criticality of pool formation; a partner-organization reader misreads what the project is asking for at Q5 outreach (volunteer reviewer commitment, not paid reviewer slot).
- **Recommendation:** Reframe §1 line 30 as: "Reviewer-pool recruitment is conditional on Q5 (NGO partner outreach) per §8.2 (volunteer-attestation baseline at Phase A); the volunteer-to-honoraria transition is conditional on Q3 (funding closes). Partner-organization arrangements and witness-pool composition are conditional on Q5." Separates the recruitment gate (Q5) from the compensation gate (Q3).

### F3: §1 omits Briar's upstream-maintenance conditional that §7.1 and §6.2 make load-bearing

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26: "communications (SimpleX in v1; Briar joins in v1.5)" — unconditional. §1 line 28: "Subsequent releases (v1.5 architecture completeness; v1.6 deferred UX...)" — no Briar-conditional. §7.1 line 709: "Adds Briar as the highest-sensitivity tier (subject to Briar's continued upstream maintenance and release cadence — a dependency the brief does not control)." §6.2 line 623: "Briar integration as the highest-sensitivity tier (per [D0004]). Includes the per-conversation extra-private mode toggle that depends on Briar landing. Conditional on Briar's continued upstream maintenance." §5.4 line 466: integration relies on Briar's protocol "as published by the Briar project and named as a trust root in Section 3.4."
- **Impact:** §1's "Briar joins in v1.5" reads as a commitment the project controls. §7.1 / §6.2 / §5.4 make clear it depends on the Briar project's continued operation — a dependency parallel to GrapheneOS's continued availability that §9.2 line 944 surfaces as a real risk. A funder evaluating the roadmap's robustness reads §1 as if v1.5's highest-sensitivity tier is contingent only on Cairn's engineering capacity; the deeper sections name it as additionally contingent on an upstream project Cairn does not control.
- **Recommendation:** Qualify §1 line 26 as "communications (SimpleX in v1; Briar joins in v1.5, subject to upstream maintenance per §7.1)" — preserves the orientation-level register while pointing readers at the load-bearing conditional.

### F4: §1's v1.5 ship-condition compresses "foundation incorporation materially in progress" to "foundation incorporation"

- **Evidence:** §1 line 28: "v1.5 broader-than-pilot release is structurally gated on Phase C funding (pre-beta full audit per D0011; foundation incorporation per [D0010]; reviewer-honoraria operating model per Section 8.2)." §7.1 line 709: "(d) foundation incorporation per §8.4 / D0010 is materially in progress, such that the structural mitigations broader-release users receive (formalized Safe Harbor per D0012, board-bound governance, formal partner advisory authority per D0009) can be operative when broader release lands." §8.4 line 817: "incorporate as a non-profit foundation approximately 18-24 months post-v1 launch."
- **Impact:** §1's compressed "foundation incorporation" reads as a binary precondition (incorporation complete before v1.5 ships). §7.1 specifies a softer threshold ("materially in progress" with operative structural mitigations). Given §8.4's 18-24-month timeline for incorporation and §10.3's framing of v1.5 as the broader-release milestone, the §1 binary-reading implies v1.5 cannot ship for at least 18-24 months post-v1 even if all engineering and audit gates close — a timeline §7.1 does not actually require.
- **Recommendation:** §1 line 28: "...foundation incorporation materially in progress per [D0010]..." — single qualifier that preserves orientation register while matching §7.1's actual specification.

### F5: §1 lists OIDC provider as a substituted-trust component; §3.4, §5.5, §4.3 name it as a residual trust placement adding U.S. legal process

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26: "(4) release security that substitutes a distributed trust set — a 5-of-N reviewer attestation threshold, witness cosignatures, OIDC provider integrity — for the developer's single signing identity." §5.5 line 510: "The Sigstore identity that signs each release is bound to an OIDC identity... The OIDC provider — chosen at project start and named in Section 3.4 trust roots — can issue OIDC tokens for the developer's identity under legal process in its jurisdiction or under coercion of its personnel... v1 uses a U.S.-based OIDC provider in pilot... with the explicit acknowledgment that this places U.S. legal process in the effective trust surface." §4.3 line 309: "§5.5:488 explicitly names U.S. legal process in the v1 effective trust surface; Q24." §3.4 line 214: identifies the OIDC provider as a trust root explicitly. §4.1 line 275: "Sigstore (signing infrastructure including OIDC chain; §5.5:488 places U.S. legal process in the effective trust surface via the v1 OIDC provider, Q24)."
- **Impact:** §1's "OIDC provider integrity" framing positions the provider as part of the _defense_ (a substituted trust set). The deeper sections frame it as a _trust placement that adds jurisdictional exposure_ — a residual surface, not a security property. A reader who anchors on §1 forms an inverted picture: §5.5 / §4.3 / §3.4 are saying the OIDC provider is a cost the multi-party stack accepts, not a layer of protection it adds. Partner organizations in non-U.S. jurisdictions reading §1 would not see the U.S. legal-process exposure §5.5 names explicitly.
- **Recommendation:** Reframe §1 line 26 commitment (4) as: "release security that substitutes a distributed trust set — multi-reviewer attestation (3-of-5 over a 5+ pool), witness cosignatures, transparency-logged signing events — for the developer's single signing identity, with the OIDC provider's jurisdiction (U.S.-based in v1 per §5.5) named as an explicit trust placement rather than a removed one (§3.4, §4.3)."

## Significant findings

### F6: §1's release-security commitment omits the long-lived APK signing key that §5.5 names as a single-credential surface

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26 framing: "release security that substitutes a distributed trust set... for the developer's single signing identity." §5.5 line 502: "Two signing layers: long-lived APK key and per-release Sigstore attestation... The project must therefore hold a long-lived Android signing identity for APK signature continuity (required for updates to install over prior versions). That key is protected as carefully as the threat model demands — held in a hardware security token, with rotation supported via APK Signature Scheme v3 key rotation if a future compromise requires it." §5.5 line 520: "Long-lived APK key compromise (the harder scenario): the response uses APK Signature Scheme v3 key rotation. All installed clients must process the rotation in an update before subsequent updates can be signed by the new key. This is a multi-release recovery process." §9.3 line 962: lists long-lived APK signing key as a trust-root compromise scenario.
- **Impact:** §1's "substitutes... for the developer's single signing identity" framing implies the developer's single signing identity has been fully replaced. §5.5 makes clear the long-lived APK key persists as a single-credential surface with the heaviest-recovery scenario in the brief (multi-release rotation). A technical reviewer comparing §1 against §5.5 finds the §1 framing materially incomplete.
- **Recommendation:** §1 line 26: "release security that substitutes a distributed trust set... for the developer's single per-release signing identity, retaining the long-lived APK signing key as the residual single-credential surface per §5.5."

### F7: §1's "What this brief is and is not" omits four §10.8 non-promises a sophisticated funder would expect

- **Evidence:** §1 line 32 non-promise list: specific dollar amounts; specific timelines tied to funding events; that the project reaches Phase C or D; that named subsidy programs are open at cited rates; that audience populations not served at v1 will be served at v1.x or v2; that v2 iOS and v3 mesh-radio extensions materialize on any specific timeline. §10.8 line 1239 explicitly enumerates: specific dollar amounts (✓ §1 covers); specific timelines tied to funding events (✓); reaches Phase C or D (✓); specific maintainer compensation arrangement (✗ §1 misses); specific fiscal sponsor, audit firm, or jurisdiction (✗ §1 misses); subsidy programs open at cited rates (✓); Phase B-D figures net of fiscal-sponsor fees (✗ §1 misses); BYOD hardware availability across jurisdictions (✗ §1 misses); §9.1-committed self-funding runway (✓ §1 acknowledges separately).
- **Impact:** §1 promises to mirror §10.8's non-promise list. Four named §10.8 non-promises do not appear in §1. The maintainer-compensation and net-of-fees omissions are particularly load-bearing: §1's Phase D figure "(~$90–250K/year excluding maintainer compensation)" is the same gross-of-fiscal-sponsor-fees figure §10.4 uses, and a funder reading §1 alone reasonably infers the figure is net to the project. §10.8 explicitly disclaims this.
- **Recommendation:** Add to §1 line 32 non-promise list: "specific fiscal sponsor (Q15), audit firm (Q7), or jurisdiction of incorporation"; "that Phase B-D figures are net to the project (figures stated gross of fiscal-sponsor fees per §10.2 and §10.4)"; "that BYOD hardware availability holds across candidate-user jurisdictions"; and "a specific maintainer compensation arrangement" (the Phase D aspiration is identified; the funding configuration that supports it has not closed).

### F8: §1's social-recovery commitment omits the v1 online-Sigsum dependency that §6.1 / §9.2 / §4.1 name as a real audience limit

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26 commitment (3): "social recovery without centralized trustees via Shamir-among-peers with pre-shared peer challenges and a 48-hour delay-and-confirm window..." §4.1 line 272: "v1 recovery requires online connectivity to Sigsum (v1.5 adds offline-tolerant caching)." §6.1 line 597: "v1 recovery requires online Sigsum connectivity (v1.5 adds offline-tolerant caching)." §9.2 line 934: "Recovery requires online connectivity to Sigsum in v1. The v1 trust-graph evaluation queries Sigsum directly (per [D0004]); a user offline at the moment of recovery cannot evaluate new attestations they have not yet seen. v1.5 adds local caching that mitigates the most common case but does not address full-offline recovery."
- **Impact:** §1 names recovery as one of four architectural commitments but does not surface the v1 online-Sigsum dependency. The dependency is a real audience limit (§9.2 frames it as a product limitation explicitly). A reader who acts on §1's recovery framing without §6.1 / §9.2 context misestimates the v1 audience: users in network-constrained jurisdictions (a population overlapping with the threat tier §2 describes — the same Pegasus-target regions that experience internet shutdowns per §9.2 line 926) may not have viable v1 recovery even with a viable peer network.
- **Recommendation:** §1 line 26 commitment (3) addition: "...the unlock → peer enumeration → impersonation chain (per [D0005]); v1 recovery requires online Sigsum connectivity per §6.1 and §9.2, with offline-tolerant caching deferred to v1.5."

### F9: §1's "v1 implementation is solo" is correct but omits §6.1's specialist-roles absorption that §8.1 / §9.1 name load-bearing

- **Evidence:** §1 line 30: "v1 implementation is solo." §6.1 line 591: "Solo-developer absorption of §8.1 specialist roles. Section 8.1 identifies three specialist roles (part-time cryptographic consulting; UX-focused engineer; documentation and community-management role) as funding-gated additions, with §5.6 UX alone estimated at 30–50% of v1 implementation effort. v1 absorbs these specialist scopes into the solo developer's working set at the volunteer baseline." §9.1 line 904: "Solo-developer single-point-of-failure. The v1 implementation is the work of one developer... This is the most concentrated risk in the project's structure." §8.1 line 775: rolling cryptographic consulting is the "aspirational addition when partnership or grant funding closes (Q3). Section 9.1 acknowledges the residual engineering risk this concentrates during the self-audit period."
- **Impact:** §1's three-word framing "v1 implementation is solo" is technically accurate but does not surface that "solo" means absorbing three specialist roles (cryptographic, UX, documentation) into one developer. §6.1 line 591 makes the absorption explicit because §8.1 / §9.1 stake real engineering and SPOF risk on the framing. A funder evaluating "is this fundable at the Phase A volunteer baseline?" needs to see that "solo" means more than headcount.
- **Recommendation:** §1 line 30: "v1 implementation is solo, with the §8.1 specialist roles (cryptographic consulting, UX engineering, documentation) absorbed into the developer's working set at the volunteer baseline per §6.1 — the Phase Aspect §9.1 names as the project's most concentrated risk."

### F10: §1's v1 ship-conditions omit engineering scope completion (§7.1 condition (a))

- **Evidence:** §1 line 28: "v1 is a closed pilot, not a public release in the usual sense: pilot deployment is gated on the pre-pilot cryptographic-primitives audit per Section 8.5 and [D0011], on the partner-mediated consent and exit protocol per [D0013], and on first-quorum reviewer attestation forming per Section 8.2." §7.1 line 707: "v1 ships when: (a) the engineering scope per §6.1 completes (~9–12 person-months of focused effort at the volunteer baseline per D0008); (b) the pre-pilot cryptographic-primitives audit per Section 8.5 / D0011 closes (Phase B funding event); (c) the partner-mediated pilot consent and exit protocol per D0013 is in place; (d) first-quorum reviewer attestation per Section 8.2 / 5.5 forms."
- **Impact:** §1 names three of four v1 ship-conditions and omits engineering scope completion. The omission is forgivable in compression (a reader assumes the engineering must complete) but §7.1 lists it first because it carries the Phase A volunteer-baseline cadence dependency — the engineering scope may not complete on a schedule the project controls. A funder reading §1's three conditions infers a faster v1-launch path than §7.1 actually specifies.
- **Recommendation:** §1 line 28: "v1 is a closed pilot, not a public release in the usual sense: pilot deployment is gated on engineering scope completion (~9-12 person-months at Phase A volunteer-baseline cadence per D0008), on the pre-pilot cryptographic-primitives audit..."

### F11: §1 omits the v1 external dependency surface §4.1 names as load-bearing

- **Evidence:** §1 line 26 architecture layers: "endpoint... transport... communications..." plus four cross-cutting commitments. §4.1 line 275: "§4.1's three layers integrate substantially more upstream-maintained infrastructure than the layer enumeration above surfaces. Specifically v1 depends on: Tor (protocol + pluggable transports, maintained by the Tor Project); SimpleX (messaging protocol + reference implementation, maintained by SimpleX Chat); GrapheneOS (OS + hardware-hardening, maintained by the GrapheneOS project); Sigstore (signing infrastructure including OIDC chain...); Rekor (Sigstore's transparency log infrastructure, single-operator log model); Sigsum (witness-cosigned log infrastructure...); UnifiedPush (push protocol, distributor availability dependency); F-Droid and Accrescent (distribution channels with their own inclusion criteria and operational continuity)... The integration is the product; the integration is also the dependency surface."
- **Impact:** §1's layered architecture framing matches §4.1's surface enumeration but does not surface §4.1's load-bearing "integration is the dependency surface" honesty. The honesty matters at the orientation level because the comparator landscape (§2.3) treats integration approaches with skepticism precisely because of this dependency-surface trade. A reviewer landing at §4.1 finds it; a §1-only reader takes away "Cairn integrates SimpleX/Briar/Tor/Sigstore/Sigsum" without internalizing that integration substitutes architectural risk for upstream-dependency risk.
- **Recommendation:** Add a sentence to §1 line 26 architectural-commitments paragraph: "The integration substitutes architectural construction for upstream-dependency surface; §4.1 enumerates the v1 external dependencies (Tor, SimpleX, GrapheneOS, Sigstore, Rekor, Sigsum, UnifiedPush, F-Droid, Accrescent) and acknowledges that the integration is also the dependency surface."

## Patterns

Three patterns recur across the findings:

**Pattern 1 — Compression that drops a load-bearing conditional.** F3 (Briar upstream maintenance), F4 (foundation incorporation "materially in progress"), F8 (online Sigsum dependency), F9 (specialist-roles absorption), F10 (engineering scope completion). §1's compression is generally well-calibrated but consistently drops the conditionals the deeper sections preserve. The pattern's impact is uniform: §1 reads as a slightly more committed posture than the deeper sections actually specify. Recommendation across all five: when compressing a roadmap or operational commitment, retain the single most-load-bearing qualifier (Briar's upstream dependency; incorporation as in-progress not complete; v1 online Sigsum; specialist-roles absorption; engineering scope completion as the Phase A volunteer-baseline contingency).

**Pattern 2 — Quantitative restatement errors.** F1 ("5-of-N" vs "3-of-5 over 5+") is the principal instance. §1 contains correct restatements elsewhere (engineering scope "9-12 person-months" matches §6.1; foundation timing "18-24 months" matches §8.4; Phase A/B/C/D dollar bands match §10). The reviewer-attestation parameter is the one that drifted — the parameter §5.5 line 506 stakes the security argument on. Recommendation: audit every §1 quantitative claim against the deeper-section source; the architectural-commitments paragraph (line 26) is the most concentrated drift surface because it compresses §§4-5 across four bullets.

**Pattern 3 — Non-promise list under-coverage relative to §10.8 plus marketing-register drift on residual surfaces.** F5 (OIDC provider framed as "integrity"), F6 (long-lived APK key omission), F7 (four §10.8 non-promises missing in §1), F16 ("undefended" vs "not defended by the tier model"). §1's residual-surface framing is generally honest (it explicitly names the reconstruction-window exposure, names the source-vs-binary v1 supply-chain gap, names the Phase D sustainability question). The drifts above are where §1 either reframes a residual as a defensive layer (OIDC as "integrity"), omits a residual the deeper section names (long-lived APK key as single-credential surface), or uses slightly more absolute language than the deeper section permits ("undefended"). Recommendation: cross-check §1's residual-surfaces enumeration against §3.3, §5.5, §9.3 to verify that every residual §1 names appears in the same calibration as the deeper section, and that no residual the deeper section names is silently dropped from §1.
