## Summary

PROBLEM: The brief's 180–420 hrs/yr Phase D operational-time estimate (§10.4) is meaningfully incomplete; an honest accounting of the omitted categories adds another ~350–1,300 hrs/yr. At a 15–20 hrs/wk volunteer baseline (~780–1,040 hrs/yr available), the realistic Phase D recurring-coordination floor consumes most or all of the developer's capacity before any engineering work happens. The maintainer will not see this on month one of v1; they will see it at month 18–30, and at that point the deferred decisions (F6 foundation deferral, F4 reviewer-pool deferral) have removed the institutional handoff path that comparable projects relied on to survive that window.

EVIDENCE: §10.4 enumerates upstream tracking (30–80), reviewer/witness pool coordination (80–200), test-infra maintenance (30–60), partner coordination (40–80), summing to 180–420. The brief itself names but does not budget: CVE response cycles (§9.4 references runbooks-not-yet-written; §5.5 names compromise plans), grant-application cycles (§10.6 sequences them but does not estimate hours), pilot-user direct support (§6.3 commits "in-app support channel" plus 3-month and 6-month partner debriefs; no hour estimate), trust-roots health reporting at "80–320 hrs/cycle biennially" (§9.4 — this single line item exceeds the §10.4 ceiling at biennial cadence), Q5/Q10/Q14/Q15 partner-recruitment work compounding across six distinct partner role categories (§8.6), and dependency-update/security-tracking work for the Rust crate tree, GrapheneOS firmware, Sigstore client, Tor pluggable transports, and Briar (when v1.5 lands). The architecture-simplification review's P4 ("400–1,000 hrs/yr recurring solo coordination") was the pre-F4/F6 estimate; the post-F4/F6 estimate of 180–420 reflects the F4 (reviewer-pool deferral) and F8a (mesh-radio removal) cuts but does not similarly re-baseline the categories above.

IMPACT: At Phase D, the project either (a) absorbs uncompensated developer time beyond the §9.1-acknowledged-but-undisclosed self-funding runway, hitting the D0009 sudden-unavailability contingency on a predictable trajectory (which §10.7 acknowledges as the long-horizon failure mode), or (b) ships at degraded operational quality with documentation drift, slipped audit cadence, and reactive partner coordination. Both outcomes match the failure trajectories of comparable solo-or-near-solo projects at this threat tier.

RECOMMENDATION: Before writing v1 code, do the four things in "What you would change before writing v1 code" below. They are calibrated to where the brief has actual control: closing the runway-disclosure gap (Q20), specifying the CVE-response runbook, specifying the APK signing-key custody/transfer mechanism, and rewriting §10.4 to enumerate the categories the current line items omit.

The brief is unusually honest about several structural risks (§10.4 sustainability-horizon-is-finite framing; §10.7 enumerated failure modes; §10.8 disclaimer list; D0009's explicit "not fully addressed" subsection acknowledging coercion-induced false continuation). That honesty is the foundation for the work below. None of the findings below contradict the brief's own acknowledged risks; they identify where the acknowledgment has not yet been turned into operational specification.

## Year-three trajectory mapping

Calendar mapping from brief publication (2026-05-28) under volunteer-baseline cadence per D0008 plus the realistic operational gates the brief itself names.

**Months 0–6:** Brief publication → Q5 partner outreach begins → fiscal-sponsor onboarding pursued in parallel (§10.2 names this as 2–6 months at SFC/OCF/CS&S; NLnet grantee-sponsorship lower-friction). Phase A engineering begins. §9.1 frames "approximately 6 months from brief completion to funded development" — but this is the brief's own estimate, which §10.7 names as Q20-unresolved-runway-dependent.

**Months 6–18:** Phase B first-dollar threshold pursuit. OTF Secure Audit cycle is ~6 months (§10.2); NLnet NGI Zero Trust is ~3 months. Earliest realistic close of Phase B funding: month 9 if NLnet hits, month 12+ if OTF. Phase A engineering continues — §6.1 says 9–12 person-months of focused effort at volunteer baseline. At 15–20 hrs/wk effective volunteer time (the brief does not state this, but it is the implicit "as available" cadence per D0008), 9–12 person-months of focused effort is calendar 18–32 months. The brief acknowledges this implicitly when D0008 says v1.5 ships "10–18 months post-v1 launch" under volunteer cadence, but does not equivalently translate v1 itself.

**Months 18–32 (year 1.5 to year 2.7):** Pre-pilot audit ($15–30K subsidized per D0011) runs once Phase B funds close. Pilot consent protocol (D0013) lands. Engineering scope completes. **v1 ships.** This is the optimistic case where funding closes on the first OTF cycle (month 12), engineering completes on the 24-month bound, and the audit runs cleanly. The pessimistic case slips to month 36.

**Months 30–48 (year 2.5 to year 4):** v1 pilot runs for 6 months (§6.3 fixed). During the pilot: 10–15 users at 1–3 hrs/month support each (not budgeted in §10.4); partner-organization 3-month and 6-month debriefs; pilot crash/feedback flowing through the D0013 partner channel for triage; v1.5 engineering begins in parallel (~6–9 person-months per §7.1, calendar 12–18 months at volunteer cadence per D0008); D0016 trigger evaluation must complete during this window; Phase C funding ($60–220K pre-beta audit + optional honoraria + conditional foundation incorporation) must be pursued.

**The year-three cliff lands here.** At month 36 (approximately mid-2029 under the brief's optimistic schedule, mid-2030 realistically), the developer is concurrently:

- Running the v1 pilot's final 3 months (user support, partner debriefs, feedback collection).
- Implementing v1.5 engineering scope (Briar integration, reproducible builds, trust-graph caching).
- Pursuing Phase C funding (≥1 OTF cycle = 6 months, plus reporting load on any Phase B grant still active).
- Performing the D0016 trigger evaluation (which requires legal consultation if any criterion appears near-active).
- Producing the trust-roots health report (§9.4 biennial; 80–320 hrs/cycle — this cycle lands at year 2 or year 4 depending on start date).
- Maintaining D0009 monthly check-ins and successor documentation.
- Witness-pool renewal (Q5 cadence — multi-year arrangements per §8.6 require renewal).
- Operating any active grant's quarterly reporting (§10.9).
- Coordinating the pre-beta full audit ($60–220K; ~4–6 weeks of audit-firm time but 80–200 hrs of project-side coordination per D0011-cycle analysis).

This is the window in which comparable projects at this threat tier either (a) had already incorporated institutional structure (Briar Project CIC, Cwtch under OpenPriv, Signal Foundation by year 3), or (b) folded. The brief's D0016 deferral commits the project to facing this window without the institutional handoff path the comparable projects relied on.

**Compounding by year 3:** v1 has shipped, generated CVEs (any non-trivial cryptographic product at this scale gets 1–3 CVEs in the first 18 months per the audit-firm-public-disclosure record), and accumulated documentation drift across the architecture changes that v1.5 introduces. Each prior release has an open CVE window until users have patched; the maintainer is coordinating patch distribution across F-Droid + Accrescent + direct download + the partner-mediated D0013 channel. Partner organizations have established Q5 cycle arrangements requiring renewal. The §9.4 health report covers a longer accumulated trust-roots surface (Sigsum protocol evolution since v1; F-Droid policy changes; Pixel hardware availability shifts; OIDC provider posture changes; SimpleX and Briar protocol revisions). None of this is exotic; it's what every comparable project has experienced.

**The brief sees the cliff in §10.4** ("Phase D sustainability horizon without maintainer compensation is finite, not indefinite") **but does not project where on the calendar it lands.** That projection is what the runway figure (Q20) would deliver. Without Q20 resolution, the brief models year 3 as one decision point among many; in operational reality, year 3 is the decision point at which the deferred structural decisions (D0016 trigger, foundation evaluation, maintainer-compensation pursuit) compound simultaneously.

## Hidden-overhead accounting

§10.4 enumerates four Phase D recurring-coordination categories summing to 180–420 hrs/yr. Below is what the enumeration omits, with hours estimates derived from the audit-firm and OSS-foundation literature plus comparable-project public budgets. **The hours below are additive to the brief's 180–420 figure; they are not replacements for it.**

PROBLEM: **CVE response cycle is not enumerated.**
EVIDENCE: §9.4 commits to incident-response runbooks "once `docs/runbooks/` is established (likely concurrent with v1 alpha)" — meaning the runbooks do not exist at brief publication. §5.5 names Sigstore-identity and APK-key compromise plans but treats them as architectural specifications rather than operational time commitments. §8.2 names the emergency-release path mechanics but not the response-cycle envelope (intake → triage → patch → coordinated release → user notification → post-mortem).
IMPACT: A first CVE during pilot lands as ~40–160 hrs of dominant developer attention over 3–6 weeks. The audit-firm public-disclosure record for comparable products (Signal: average ~2–4 CVEs/yr post-launch; Briar: 1–2/yr; SimpleX: 1–2/yr; GrapheneOS: ongoing); assume **1–3 CVEs/yr** at Phase D, **80–480 hrs/yr** total.
RECOMMENDATION: Add CVE response as a §10.4 line item with the cited range. Specify the runbook and partner-notification-tree as a v1 prerequisite (not a v1.5 nice-to-have).

PROBLEM: **Grant-application and reporting cycle is not enumerated in §10.4 even though §10.6 sequences it heavily.**
EVIDENCE: §10.6 commits the project to parallel grant applications across audit subsidies (Phase B), direct grants (Phase C/D), and multi-year operational grants. §10.9 commits to quarterly progress reports during active grant periods. The §10.5 funding-source roster names ≥6 grant programs; sustained Phase D operations imply ≥2 active grants at any time. OSS-foundation public budgets (Software Freedom Conservancy fiscal-sponsorship reporting; OpenCollective sponsored-project financials) indicate **20–40 hrs per substantive grant application** + **10–20 hrs/quarter per active grant for reporting**.
IMPACT: 2–3 grant applications/yr (~60–120 hrs) + 2 active grants × 4 quarters × 10–20 hrs (~80–160 hrs) = **140–280 hrs/yr** sustained at Phase D.
RECOMMENDATION: Add as §10.4 line item. This is uncompensated developer-funder-coordination labor that maintainer compensation, if it ever lands, does not absorb because grant applications are how that compensation is obtained.

PROBLEM: **Pilot user direct support is not budgeted.**
EVIDENCE: §6.3 commits a dedicated in-app support channel, partner debriefs at 3-month and 6-month marks, and developer-facilitator continued involvement post-provisioning. §5.6 names "structural support for ongoing security operations" as user-facing operational cost. §6.3 does not estimate facilitator/support time per pilot user.
IMPACT: Comparable projects' helpdesk records: Briar (pre-Mailbox era) ~30–60 minutes/user/month average; Signal pre-Username era ~10–20 minutes/user/month; threat-tier-calibrated support is heavier than consumer-product support because users at the threat tier ask questions whose answers are operationally consequential. At 10–15 users × 1–3 hrs/month over 6-month pilot: **120–540 hrs over pilot period** = **240–1,080 hrs/yr equivalent during the pilot window.** Reduces post-pilot if v1.5+ broader release shifts support to partner facilitator networks (per §8.6 intent), but partner-mediated support is partner-coordination load, not removed load.
RECOMMENDATION: §10.4 should distinguish pilot-window operational load from steady-state Phase D load. The pilot window is where solo capacity is most stressed; it overlaps the v1.5 engineering window (per §7.1's "v1.5 ships when pilot generates feedback informing v1.5 design").

PROBLEM: **Trust-roots health report (§9.4) commitment exceeds the §10.4 ceiling at biennial cadence.**
EVIDENCE: §9.4: "biennial at the v1 self-funded-MVP baseline (acknowledging the solo-developer SPOF and the multi-day-per-cycle production cost)." The architecture-simplification review's Lens-D F8 estimated 80–320 hrs/cycle. At biennial cadence: **40–160 hrs/yr amortized.** At the upper bound, this single line item is 38% of the §10.4 ceiling.
IMPACT: §10.4's 180–420 either silently absorbs this (in which case the ceiling is wrong) or omits it (in which case the line item exists and is unbudgeted). Either way the §10.4 arithmetic does not balance.
RECOMMENDATION: §10.4 should either add the health report as an explicit line item with the 40–160 hrs/yr estimate, or §9.4 should acknowledge that the biennial cadence is conditional on capacity rather than a unilateral commitment.

PROBLEM: **Q5/Q10/Q14/Q15 partner-recruitment work compounds across six partner role categories.**
EVIDENCE: §8.6 names six role categories (technical reviewers, Sigsum witnesses, pilot facilitation, threat intelligence, localization, user training). Q5 covers reviewer + facilitator + witness + threat-intel + localization recruitment; Q14 covers partner advisory authority (now permanent under D0016); Q15 covers fiscal-sponsor selection (now permanent under D0016). Each partner conversation is a multi-meeting arrangement; partner organizations have their own evaluation cycles; some will decline.
IMPACT: At v1 phase (before partner relationships exist), recruitment work is the dominant non-engineering load. F4's reviewer-pool deferral did not eliminate this work — it shifted it to "proceeds at v1 through Q5 outreach in parallel" (D0015 explicit). The work is still in the v1 working set, just with a different consequence-for-failure framing. Conservative estimate: **80–200 hrs/yr at v1 + Phase B**, declining to **40–100 hrs/yr at Phase D** as renewal-only.
RECOMMENDATION: Pre-v1 partner-recruitment work should be a §10.1 Phase A line item; current §10.1 lists "Partner-organization outreach (Section 8.6): conversations leading toward pilot facilitation, threat intelligence, and localization arrangements. No funding required for conversations themselves" — true that no funding is required, false that no time is required.

PROBLEM: **Dependency-security tracking is named in §9.3 but not budgeted.**
EVIDENCE: §9.3 names cargo-vet, cargo-crev, "periodic dependency review during the documentation cadence." The Rust core's transitive dependency tree at the scope §6.1 describes (Sigsum client + Tor on Android + Shamir + COSE + Ed25519 + storage layer + UniFFI) is realistically 200–400 crates. Comparable projects' security-disclosure traffic on transitive deps (RustSec database, GitHub Dependabot security advisories): **20–60 advisories/yr** at this dependency scale, with **5–15 requiring actual evaluation**.
IMPACT: 30–60 hrs/yr at steady state for dependency triage, version-bump testing, security-advisory evaluation. Not catastrophic but unbudgeted.
RECOMMENDATION: Add as §10.4 line item.

PROBLEM: **Documentation-drift maintenance across architecture changes is unbudgeted.**
EVIDENCE: The brief is 1,774 lines (`docs/design-brief.md`). It cross-references 16 D-docs, an open-questions tracker, an architecture-diagrams file, and reviews/. Every architectural change ripples across these documents — D0004's scope cuts required 50+ surface revisions per D0004's own consequences list; the F4/F6 changes the brief absorbed in 2026-05-28 required updates to 18 brief sections, 7 D-docs, and 11 open questions per the architecture-simplification-review action plan. **Glossary drift is already visible in the current draft**: line 1568 still says "Cairn anticipates using a fiscal sponsor for grant intake during the 18–24 month pre-incorporation window per D0010" — a sentence the D0016 deferral superseded in the same session that updated §8.4. A maintainer rereading the brief will see the contradiction within hours; a partner organization's legal advisor will see it on first read.
IMPACT: Per v1.5/v1.6/v2/v3 release cycle, documentation-drift maintenance is **30–80 hrs/release**. At median 4–6 month cadence per D0008: **60–240 hrs/yr** at Phase D.
RECOMMENDATION: Either commit to documentation-drift maintenance as a §10.4 line item with the explicit hours, or commit to a documentation-rewrite cycle synchronized with release cadence. The brief currently treats documentation as a Phase A finished output rather than an ongoing operational commitment.

**Honest-accounting sum.** The omissions above total **390–2,160 hrs/yr** at Phase D steady state, additive to the brief's 180–420. Combined: **570–2,580 hrs/yr.** At 15–20 hrs/wk volunteer baseline (780–1,040 hrs/yr available), the engineering capacity at Phase D is **structurally negative at the upper bound** and **constrained to ~200–470 hrs/yr at the lower bound** before any v1.5/v1.6/v2 engineering work, CVE response engineering, or partner-conversation engineering. The brief's "Phase D sustainability horizon ... is finite, not indefinite" framing (§10.4) is consistent with this; the brief does not currently project where on the calendar the horizon ends, and the runway-disclosure gap (Q20) is what would let funders see it.

## First-CVE walkthrough

The realistic disclosure-to-patched-users sequence at v1 pilot scale, traced under the brief's documented response infrastructure.

**Day 0 — Disclosure intake.** A security researcher (Citizen Lab, Amnesty Security Lab, EFF, or academic — the §8.5 coordinated-disclosure relationship targets) emails `security@cairn-project.example` (the PGP-encrypted contact §8.5 commits). The researcher reports a vulnerability in the COSE_Sign1 envelope construction discovered post-pre-pilot-audit. The disclosure is high-severity (the audit caught some envelope flaws but missed this one).

**Day 0–3 — Triage.** The developer reads the disclosure (the PGP-encrypted contact is monitored by the developer; D0009 monthly check-in is unaffected by short windows). Triage requires: confirming the vulnerability against the live codebase; assessing scope of pilot-user exposure; deciding whether the disclosure requires the emergency-release path (§8.2: 2-of-5 attestation threshold, "documented security justification"). Triage at this depth on a solo developer is realistically 8–16 hrs.

**Day 3–10 — Patch development.** The vulnerability is in the trust-graph operation envelope handling — a Rust-core change requiring envelope validation tightening, UniFFI binding update, and Kotlin UI verification. The patch is small (~100–300 lines) but security-critical. Per §8.3, security-critical changes require external-reviewer-pool review under normal circumstances, with emergency-path reduced threshold (2-of-5 at commit time, 3-of-5 in follow-up). At v1, **the recruited reviewer pool does not exist** (deferred to v1.5 per D0015). The §8.3 emergency-path wording does not address what happens at v1 when the pool itself has not formed: "At v1, emergency releases ship under the developer-source-review baseline with the same security justification documented; emergency releases at v1 do not require a quorum threshold because the v1 stack does not require a pool." This is operationally clean — but means the v1 emergency-release path has no external-review backstop. Patch development: 16–40 hrs depending on complexity.

**Day 10–14 — Release engineering.** The developer signs the patched APK with the long-lived APK signing key (hardware-token-held, §5.5). The developer authenticates with the OIDC provider to obtain the Sigstore identity signing. The patched binary is logged to Rekor and Sigsum. Witness cosignatures are requested from the witness pool (§5.5 — witness pool is a v1 commitment, recruitment conditional on Q5). **At v1 pilot, the witness pool may or may not have formed at recruitment-cadence.** If witnesses are slow to cosign, the release blocks; emergency-release path commits to "2-of-5 reviewer attestation" but the v1 emergency path uses developer source review baseline, so witness cosignatures are the only multi-party signal. Witness coordination: 8–24 hrs depending on partner-organization-side response time.

**Day 14–21 — Distribution and notification.** The patched APK ships to F-Droid (primary; F-Droid has its own community-rebuild cycle that ranges from days to weeks for security-relevant changes), Accrescent (faster), and direct download (fastest). **Pilot users are notified through the D0013 partner-mediated channel** — partner organization receives the advisory, distributes to the 10–15 pilot users, mediates their response. Coordinated disclosure to all 10–15 users via the partner takes 3–7 days depending on partner-side capacity. Direct-download users patch first; F-Droid users patch later; the channel mismatch produces verification confusion that the maintainer fields.

**Day 21–35 — Coordinated public disclosure.** The 90-day default disclosure timeline (§8.5) is shortened in the high-severity case. The developer publishes the security advisory (CHANGELOG distinguishes security-relevant changes per §8.2). The Sigsum-anchored release log preserves the audit trail. Researcher acknowledgment per §8.5. The §9.4 trust-roots health report is updated if the disclosure affects a trust root.

**Day 35–60 — Post-mortem and follow-up.** Per §8.5: post-mortem informs runbook updates. Per §8.2: emergency releases require 3-of-5 follow-up in next release within 4 weeks — at v1, this is documented developer source review at the next median 4–6 month release cadence per D0008. Per §9.4: the disclosure event itself becomes an entry the next biennial trust-roots health report covers.

**Total developer time: 60–160 hrs over 4–8 weeks of dominant attention.** During this window: no v1 engineering, no v1.5 engineering (if we are in the year-3 cliff window), no partner outreach, no grant-application work. The dead-man's-switch monthly check-in (§D0009) still has to be performed during the CVE response.

**What the brief does not specify:**

- **The CVE response runbook itself.** §9.4: "The plans live in `docs/runbooks/` once that directory is established (likely concurrent with v1 alpha)." This means at the moment of first CVE, the runbook may not exist. The runbook is what makes a 60–160 hr response possible instead of 200–400 hrs of ad-hoc coordination.
- **The partner-notification tree.** Which partner organization is the D0013 mediating partner? Who is on call if that partner is unavailable? §8.6 names Q5 as the resolution path but at v1 ship time the partner may have been arranged only weeks before.
- **The signing-key custody during CVE response if the developer is unavailable.** The APK signing key is hardware-token-held by the developer. If the developer is on vacation, sick, or traveling, the CVE patch cannot be signed. There is no committed multi-party access procedure (§5.5: "ideally with multi-party access procedures" — aspirational language).
- **The interaction with the D0009 60-day window.** If a CVE drops during the 60-day pre-advisory window (developer becomes incapacitated, partner has not yet published the advisory), no one can sign the patch. Pilot users have an unpatched vulnerability and no path to receive the fix because the partner advisory authority does not hold signing material.

**Compare to comparable projects:**

- Briar's first major CVE response (2020 — a peer-discovery vulnerability): handled by Briar Project CIC three-person team plus published runbook with explicit response timeline; ~6 weeks from disclosure to patched users; total team time ~120 hrs across three people.
- Signal Foundation's typical disclosure cycle: 2–4 weeks from disclosure to patched users; supported by dedicated security-response staff (≥1 FTE at any time).
- GrapheneOS's response cadence: typically <2 weeks from disclosure to patched users; multiple maintainers with documented escalation chain.

Cairn's volunteer-baseline solo-developer response model commits to absorbing what Briar Project CIC needed three people to do, in the same 4–8 week window. The brief does not commit to a specific response time SLA, which is itself the honest framing — but pilot users at the §3 threat tier need to understand that the response window may be 8 weeks at v1 rather than the 2–4 weeks comparable products achieve.

## Dead-man's-switch and foundation-trigger realism

PROBLEM: **D0009 60-day window is too long for the threat tier the developer is exposed to.**
EVIDENCE: D0009 commits the partner advisory authority to publish "if the developer's monthly check-in misses by 60 days (two consecutive missed cycles)." The brief itself (§9.1, D0009 context) frames the developer as a target at the §3 threat tier. The §3 threat tier includes border seizure (hours), short-term detention (days), and asset seizure that includes signing infrastructure (immediate). A 60-day window is calibrated to "developer became unreachable for sustained period" cases (illness, accident); it is poorly calibrated to "developer detained at border, held for a week, returned without device" or "developer arrested, signing key seized, fate uncertain." Comparable trajectories: Wikileaks-style organizational continuity in detention cases; the Tor Project's planning around staff-detention scenarios.
IMPACT: During the 60-day window, no patches can be issued (developer is the only signer). A CVE during the window has no response path. Pilot users are silently abandoned for up to 60 days before the partner advisory authority publishes anything; they have no mechanism to know whether the project is in stable maintenance or in unannounced crisis.
RECOMMENDATION: Reduce the missed-check-in trigger to 30 days for the partner advisory authority's _first contact_ with the developer; reserve the 60-day window for _advisory publication_ if the first contact fails. This adds a check-in path that catches the 14–45 day "something is wrong" cases without immediately escalating to public advisory.

PROBLEM: **D0009's "coercion-induced false continuation" acknowledgment is correct that the architecture cannot solve this, but the operational consequence is unspecified.**
EVIDENCE: D0009 explicit: "Coercion that compels the developer to issue false continuation statements through the dead-man's-switch mechanism — the advisory authority does not have a way to detect coercion-under-duress within the developer's own check-ins." The brief honestly frames this as residual.
IMPACT: A developer under coercion can be compelled to sign monthly check-in messages indefinitely; the partner advisory authority sees normal operations and does not act. The threat-tier-relevant scenario is that the developer's signing identity is being used by an adversary to ship a malicious build, and the dead-man's-switch is being used by the same adversary to suppress the advisory mechanism that would warn users.
RECOMMENDATION: A duress-canary mechanism in the check-in message structure. Briar's threat model discusses canaries for related cases; warrant canaries are widely deployed. The check-in message could include a structured affirmation that the developer is acting freely; absence of the affirmation triggers the partner advisory regardless of timing. This is the Tor Project's warrant-canary pattern adapted to the dead-man's-switch flow. Implementation cost: 1–2 hrs/month additional developer time, partner-side check-in-parsing infrastructure ~20 hrs one-time.

PROBLEM: **D0009 does not specify physical custody of the APK signing key under successor handover.**
EVIDENCE: §5.5: "the APK key is protected with the strongest mechanism the deployment can sustain (hardware-token-held, ideally with multi-party access procedures)." D0009 successor-handover documentation: "signing-identity-recovery procedure" (referenced but not specified in D0009). The "ideally" qualifier in §5.5 is aspirational language; the brief does not commit to multi-party access procedures as a v1 prerequisite.
IMPACT: Under the D0009 sudden-unavailability contingency, the partner advisory authority publishes an advisory. The advisory cannot include "and here is a patched release" because the partner does not hold the APK signing token. The signing-identity-recovery procedure for the APK key, per §5.5, is APK Signature Scheme v3 key rotation, which requires the prior key to sign the rotation block — **and the prior key is in the developer's hardware token, which is either inaccessible or seized.** A successor cannot rotate the APK key; pilot users would face a hard migration to a different installation under a new signing identity, requiring clean reinstall.
RECOMMENDATION: Multi-party access procedures should be a v1 prerequisite, not an "ideally." The minimum operational specification: APK signing token in a safe-deposit-box-equivalent with access controlled by N-of-M trustees (partner advisory authority + one or two named individuals). The successor-handover documentation must include the trustee identities and access procedure. Implementation cost: one-time setup of trustee arrangement; ongoing trustee renewal as Q14 cycle.

PROBLEM: **D0016 trigger criteria are detection-late.**
EVIDENCE: The five criteria activate after operational stress has already materialized: Criterion 1 fires after pilot evidence specifically identifies a structural-mitigation need (i.e., after a partner already names it); Criterion 4 fires after a personal-liability scenario has occurred; Criterion 5 fires when three structural mitigations are _simultaneously_ named as needing formalization (i.e., after stress is compounding). Criteria 2 (funding scale) and 3 (funder requirement) are more forward-looking but require funding scale or funder requirement that depends on Q3 resolution.
IMPACT: A maintainer evaluating triggers at v1.5 broader-release planning will see "criteria not activated; continue under deferral" in most realistic scenarios — even if a partner conversation a quarter later would have surfaced criterion 1. The trigger evaluation is point-in-time at v1.5 broader-release planning; the brief does not commit to ongoing re-evaluation at v1.6, v2, v3 planning cycles.
RECOMMENDATION: D0016 should commit to trigger re-evaluation at every subsequent broader-release planning cycle (v1.6, v2, v3) with the same criteria, not only at v1.5. The brief currently says (D0016 Reversibility section) "The deferral is not one-time; it is the ongoing operational state until a trigger evaluation activates incorporation work." But the specific re-evaluation timing is implicit; making it explicit at each release-planning cycle is the operational specification that matches the framing.

## Sequencing critique

PROBLEM: **The brief sequences engineering before partner outreach when the comparable-project record sequences them concurrently or partner-first.**
EVIDENCE: §10.6 step 1: "Partner-organization outreach precedes formal grant applications." Step 2: pre-incorporation legal consultation (Phase B). Step 3: subsidy programs. Step 4: direct grants. §10.1 Phase A includes both engineering and partner outreach. ## Contributor-attraction barriers

A useful second contributor — someone whose joining would meaningfully reduce solo-developer SPOF — faces specific barriers under the brief's current architecture. Identifying them is part of the sustainability problem.

PROBLEM: **The technical onboarding floor for security-critical contributions is extremely high.**
EVIDENCE: A contributor's first meaningful PR must understand: Rust (D0003), UniFFI bindings, Kotlin Android UI conventions, the COSE_Sign1 envelope construction (D0006), the nine-field signed-operation schema, Shamir Secret Sharing over GF(256), GrapheneOS device specifics, the trust-graph operation semantics (D0006), the recovery flow (D0005), reproducible builds (v1.5), Sigstore identity-based signing, the §3 threat model, the §5 architecture detail, and the §8.3 commit-signing requirement (Sigstore-anchored signatures for any security-critical commit). §8.3: "Maintainer review is sufficient for non-security changes. Cryptographic changes additionally require sign-off from the rolling cryptographic consultant (when that role is filled per 8.1)." A contributor who wants to contribute anything security-critical has a substantial pre-contribution learning curve; a contributor who can only contribute non-security changes has limited surface (UI polish, documentation, localization — and §8.6 frames localization as partner-organization-mediated translator work, not OSS contribution).
IMPACT: The realistic contributor population is (a) people already deeply familiar with this exact stack (very small population), or (b) people willing to spend 50–100 hours learning the stack before their first PR (very small population). Neither is the realistic pool that produces sustained contribution.
RECOMMENDATION: Identify three concrete "first-PR-friendly" surfaces and document them as such. Candidates: test infrastructure (property-based test additions to the trust-graph CRDT; fuzz test corpus expansion); documentation (user-facing onboarding documentation for the facilitator-handbook); reviewer toolkit (Docker/Nix environment improvements per §8.2). Each surface should have a documented first-PR template with expected scope. This lowers the contribution barrier without diluting security-critical review discipline.

PROBLEM: **The single-maintainer code-review bottleneck is structural.**
EVIDENCE: §8.3: "Maintainer review is sufficient for non-security changes." §8.3: "External-reviewer-pool review is required for security-critical changes — the same pool from Section 5.5, or a subset designated for code review specifically." At v1, the recruited reviewer pool does not exist (deferred per D0015). The maintainer is the entire review path for v1; the reviewer pool at v1.5+ is conditional on recruitment outcomes the brief acknowledges as uncertain.
IMPACT: A contributor submits a PR; the maintainer reviews; if the maintainer is on vacation, sick, or in CVE-response mode, the PR queues. Contributors who experience >2 week review windows tend to drift away; the brief's volunteer-baseline review timing implies review windows could be 4–8 weeks during high-load periods.
RECOMMENDATION: Identify "review delegation" candidates among reviewer-pool recruits — reviewers who can do code review on non-security-critical PRs as well as release attestation. §8.3's "subset designated for code review specifically" is the right mechanism; the brief should commit to a specific subset at v1.5 ship rather than leaving it as architectural intent.

PROBLEM: **The decision-velocity bottleneck is structural.**
EVIDENCE: Every architectural change goes through one person. D0001–D0016 were all single-author decisions; the architecture-simplification review process was the closest the brief has come to multi-party architectural deliberation, and that process produced 19 findings to which the developer responded individually.
IMPACT: A second contributor who wants to make architectural-level contributions does not have a path; they can suggest decisions, but the maintainer holds the architectural lock. At v1, this is appropriate (the maintainer is the sole owner of architectural responsibility). At v1.5+, sustained contribution requires architectural-decision delegation that the brief does not specify.
RECOMMENDATION: Specify how architectural decisions transition to multi-party governance when the project has multiple contributors. Under D0016 deferral, the partner advisory authority does not have architectural authority; under foundation-operating posture (if D0016 triggers), the board does. The brief currently has no transition specification for the deferral-permanent case.

PROBLEM: **The contribution-attraction question itself does not appear in the open-questions tracker.**
EVIDENCE: Q1–Q26 cover partner outreach, audit firms, OIDC providers, fiscal sponsors, jurisdictions, localization, non-peer recovery, and many other operational questions. None ask "how do we attract a useful second contributor."
IMPACT: The question is silent in the brief's risk register; it surfaces only implicitly through §9.1's solo-developer SPOF framing. The brief frames team scaling (§8.1) as funding-conditional rather than as a contributor-attraction-and-retention problem.
RECOMMENDATION: Add as Q27 in open-questions. The question is operational and ongoing; treating it as funding-gated misses that comparable projects (Briar, Cwtch) attracted contributors via mission alignment as much as compensation.

## Failure-mode catalog

Five failure-mode shapes with names, trajectories, intervention points, and what the brief should specify.

**FAILURE MODE 1: Year-three solo-developer burnout cascade.**
TRAJECTORY: Months 0–24: developer absorbs Phase A engineering at volunteer baseline (15–20 hrs/wk). Pilot launches; pilot user support absorbs additional 5–10 hrs/wk. Months 24–36: v1.5 engineering begins concurrently with pilot operations + Phase C funding pursuit + D0016 trigger evaluation. Documentation drift accumulates. Q5 partner relationships require renewal cycle. Cumulative recurring-coordination load reaches the upper bound of the honest §10.4 accounting (570–2,580 hrs/yr). Developer's effective engineering capacity drops to near-zero. Months 36–48: project either secures multi-year operational funding (§10.5 names this category as structurally narrower than audit funding) or enters the §10.7 "long-horizon failure mode" trajectory.
INTERVENTION POINT: **Month 18.** The runway-disclosure resolution (Q20) and the F4/F6 simplification commitments need re-evaluation against actual Phase A elapsed time. If Phase A is at month 18 with engineering 50% complete and partner outreach unstarted, the project is on the burnout cascade trajectory.
WHAT THE BRIEF SHOULD SPECIFY: A month-18 check-in commitment to publish actual Phase A elapsed-vs-projected metrics; honest re-assessment of v1 scope against actual engineering velocity; D0016 trigger re-evaluation at month 18 rather than waiting for v1.5 broader-release planning.

**FAILURE MODE 2: First-CVE during D0009 60-day window.**
TRAJECTORY: Developer becomes unavailable (illness, detention, asset seizure — any §3 threat-tier scenario applied to developer). 60-day dead-man's-switch window starts. CVE is reported by Citizen Lab researcher at day 15. Pilot users have an unpatched high-severity vulnerability; the partner advisory authority does not have signing material; no patch can be issued. At day 60, partner advisory authority publishes the sudden-unavailability advisory; pilot users learn simultaneously that the project is in crisis and that there is an unpatched CVE. Coordinated disclosure is impossible at this point.
INTERVENTION POINT: **D0009 trigger reduction to 30 days for first contact, 60 days for public advisory.** Multi-party APK signing-key custody specification per §5.5 "ideally" upgraded to commitment.
WHAT THE BRIEF SHOULD SPECIFY: Per the recommendation in the dead-man's-switch section above. Additionally: the §9.4 incident-response runbook should be a v1 alpha prerequisite, not "concurrent with v1 alpha" (which means runbooks may not exist at first CVE).

**FAILURE MODE 3: Phase B funding does not close in time for pilot launch.**
TRAJECTORY: Brief published; partner outreach begins (Q5); fiscal-sponsor onboarding pursued (2–6 months per §10.2); first OTF Secure Audit cycle takes 6 months; first NLnet NGI Zero Trust cycle takes 3 months. Both decline. Cure53 mission-rate engagement is pursued; mission-rate qualification requires Cure53's discretionary decision. At month 18, the project has Phase A engineering 60% complete and no Phase B funding. Developer self-funding runway (Q20 undisclosed) approaches exhaustion. Project enters §10.7 "all Phase B funding fails for extended period" failure mode.
INTERVENTION POINT: **Resolve Q20 runway disclosure before Phase B pursuit.** The runway figure determines whether the project can sustain Phase A through multiple Phase B funding cycles or whether the first cycle is structural.
WHAT THE BRIEF SHOULD SPECIFY: Either commit to the runway figure publicly (the §9.1-stated commitment that §10.8 currently acknowledges as unmet) or commit to disclosing it under grant-agreement confidentiality to Phase B funders. The current "honest gap acknowledgment" framing does not let funders see the timeline risk they are evaluating.

**FAILURE MODE 4: Partner organization quietly de-prioritizes Cairn after Q5 conversations.**
TRAJECTORY: Developer holds Q5 conversations with Front Line Defenders, Tactical Tech, Access Now, Citizen Lab. Initial conversations are positive (the §3 threat tier is aligned with their work; the §2.2 audience is overlapping; the project has technical credibility). Months pass without a specific arrangement crystallizing. Partner organizations have their own program priorities; some absorb Cairn conversations as "interesting but not actionable this cycle"; others commit to facilitator-network engagement conditional on funding the partner organization itself has not secured. The §8.6 partnership picture remains intent rather than agreement. The partner advisory authority for D0009 cannot be specifically named.
INTERVENTION POINT: **First Q5 conversation outcome should produce a concrete commitment with named resourcing.** If conversations produce only "we'd be interested" without specific time/dollar commitments, partner relationships are not actually progressing.
WHAT THE BRIEF SHOULD SPECIFY: The brief's communication cadence with partner organizations should catch the "quiet de-prioritization" signal. Specifically: §8.6 partner-conversation outcomes should be tracked in a documented partner-status register that distinguishes "active engagement" from "interested-but-not-resourced" from "declined" from "stalled." This avoids the failure mode where the developer treats positive initial conversations as commitments and discovers at year 2 that none of them are operational.

**FAILURE MODE 5: D0016 trigger never activates and partners-cannot-engage-without-Safe-Harbor surfaces silently at v1.5 broader-release.**
TRAJECTORY: v1 pilot completes successfully at month 30. Pilot users report positively. Partner organizations review the pilot evidence. At v1.5 broader-release planning, the developer asks the facilitator-network candidate organizations to commit to broader-deployment facilitation. The candidate organizations decline citing legal-exposure concerns under the F6-deferred published-preference Safe Harbor. The developer evaluates D0016 trigger criteria; criterion 1 (partner-organization structural-mitigation need) appears active; criterion 5 (structural-mitigation cascade) appears partially active; criterion 3 (funder requirement) is not active because broader-release funding is not yet pursued. Only one criterion fully activates; the trigger does not fire (two required). The developer continues under deferral; partner organizations continue to decline; broader-release does not occur.
INTERVENTION POINT: **Pre-v1 Q5 outreach should specifically test whether partners will engage at broader-release scale under published-preference Safe Harbor.** This is the partner-conversation evidence D0016 trigger criterion 1 is meant to capture, but it can be captured before v1 ships rather than at v1.5 broader-release planning.

Three things would make me bet with rather than against you:
