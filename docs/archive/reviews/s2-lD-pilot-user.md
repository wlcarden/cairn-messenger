# §2 — Pilot User Candidate Lens

## Summary

§2.2 describes an idealized version of me — one with an institutional affiliation (NGO, news desk, research lab), an intact peer network of five trustable contacts, geographic proximity to a "trained facilitator," and the operational latitude to maintain "operational discipline" as a constant background process. The actual journalists, lawyers, organizers, and dissidents in the threat tier §2.1 describes are more often isolated than networked, more often working under family or employer device-search conditions than in spaces of their own, and more often onboarded under time pressure than in a sit-down ceremony. The brief talks about us in the third person plural — "users at this threat tier," "the population" — and the language is theoretical enough that I read it without recognizing any specific person I know.

The more serious gap is what §2.2 does not name. Sex workers under criminalization, domestic-abuse survivors leaving traffickers or state-protected partners, undocumented organizers, religious minorities under family surveillance, queer people in states where queerness is criminalized, low-income tenant organizers, prisoners' families communicating across monitored channels — all face the §2.1 threat tier (state-or-near-state adversaries with both legal and extralegal reach) and all are excluded by the §2.2 audience construction. The architecture's assumption that a peer recovery network exists and is "viable" is a class assumption the brief never inspects.

## Critical findings

### F1: The "trusted peer network of 5" assumption excludes the most acute users

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 52: "users without a peer network capable of holding recovery shares responsibly" are explicitly named as out of scope. Line 62 conditions v1 audience membership on "(b) operate in trust networks where social recovery is viable." §2.3 line 95 elevates "social-recovery flow that works without centralized trustees" to a defining property of the project.
- **Impact:** A journalist whose source network is itself the target population they are reporting on cannot recruit those people as recovery peers — the relationship is asymmetric and time-bounded, and a recovery share would compromise the source. A domestic-abuse survivor leaving a controlling partner has typically had their existing peer network systematically severed by that partner — peer isolation is a defining feature of the abuse pattern, not an incidental gap. A queer person in a jurisdiction where queerness is criminalized may have a peer network composed entirely of people who, if compromised, would face the same prosecution — the recovery quorum is itself a prosecution graph. An undocumented organizer's network is structurally fluid (people deported, in detention, unreachable) on timescales shorter than a recovery share's useful life. The brief treats "no recovery peers available" as a configuration outside the audience rather than as the default condition for a large fraction of the §2.1 threat tier. This produces an architecture that systematically excludes the highest-risk subgroups.
- **Recommendation:** Either (a) acknowledge in §2.2 that the v1 architecture has a structural ceiling on which subgroups of the §2.1 threat tier it can serve, and name the excluded subgroups explicitly (sex workers, abuse survivors, isolated dissidents, undocumented organizers); or (b) commit to a v1.x non-peer recovery path (printed paper shares held by self, time-locked self-recovery, single-trustee with attorney-client privilege, etc.) and surface it in §2.2's roadmap rather than leaving the gap implicit. Pick one. The current text reads as if the developer has not considered that the missing-peer-network case is not a configuration choice but the operating condition of the most acute users.

### F2: "Onboarded in person by a trained facilitator" assumes geography I do not have

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 52: "onboarded in person by a trained facilitator (the developer in the v1 pilot)." Line 62: v1 audience requires that users "can be onboarded in person by a facilitator." §2.1 line 44 names "journalists in countries where press freedom is contested, organizers in active dissent" as the upper-end threat tier.
- **Impact:** A journalist in Hungary, Mexico, the Philippines, India, or any of the contested-press jurisdictions §2.1 invokes is not geographically reachable by a developer running a pilot. A human-rights lawyer in Egypt or Belarus is not flying to meet the facilitator and the facilitator is not flying to them. The "trained facilitator" requirement collapses §2.2's stated audience down to "people the developer can drive to" — which §2.2 admits at line 62 ("groups the developer has direct relationships with"). But the brief does not then revise its description of the audience. The disconnect between "the population the v1 architecture is designed against" (§2.2 line 54) and "people the developer can meet for coffee" (the actual pilot) is left for the reader to notice. From my position, this reads as the developer describing a target audience that has no operational path to becoming a user.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should separate two questions cleanly: (1) what threat tier the architecture is designed for, and (2) who the v1 pilot can actually onboard. Currently both are bundled as "the v1 audience." A user reading the brief should be able to see at a glance: "the architecture is designed for X; the v1 pilot serves Y, a small subset of X; the path to expanding from Y to X is Z." The roadmap section (lines 64–69) gestures at this but only in terms of platform expansion (USB, iOS, mesh), not facilitation model. The implicit assumption that "remote onboarding" is solved by "v2 adds USB" does not survive contact with my operational reality: I do not have a facilitator at all, on any platform, at any version.

### F3: "Operational discipline the design assumes a support network provides" is undefined and class-coded

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 71: "the operational discipline the design assumes a support network provides." §2.1 line 42: "users who must think about it constantly — what to send, what to delete, when to verify a new contact, how to recover if compromised." §5.6 (line 463–465 context) refers to "operational discipline" as a UX surface.
- **Impact:** "Operational discipline" is doing a lot of work in this brief without being defined. From the user side, it describes (a) cognitive overhead I have to carry in addition to my job, my family, my survival; (b) a set of unspecified behaviors that, if I get any of them wrong, may get me or someone I love killed; (c) something the brief assumes my "support network" will help me sustain. None of (a), (b), or (c) match my reality. I do not have a support network with bandwidth to help me sustain anything. The journalists §2.1 names are typically working alone or in pairs, often as freelancers without staff support, often in addition to a second income job. The phrase "support network provides" reads as if written by someone with an institutional affiliation describing other people who have institutional affiliations. The high-end threat tier this product targets has, in my experience, less institutional support, not more — the institutionally supported journalists are the ones working for The Guardian, who get OPSEC training from desk staff; the journalists at risk are the freelancers and the local-language reporters working without that scaffolding.
- **Recommendation:** Either define "operational discipline" concretely (a list of behaviors, with time/cognitive cost estimates) so I can evaluate whether I can sustain it, or replace the phrase with what is actually being asked of the user. The current phrasing lets the developer not look at what they are asking for. If sustaining the discipline genuinely requires a support network, §2.2 should say "users without a support network are not served by v1" the same way it says "users without GrapheneOS-Pixel are not served by v1." The honesty §2.2 claims for itself at line 50 ("Naming the audience honestly is part of the architectural commitment") is undermined by leaving the support-network requirement implicit.

## Significant findings

### F4: Implicit English-language and tech-literacy assumptions

- **Evidence:** §2.2 enumerates audience subgroups by professional category (journalists, NGO staff, organizers, defense subcontractors, civil-society researchers). The threat-tier §2.1 references — "commercial mercenary spyware deployment, forensic extraction at border crossings" (line 56) — are documented against populations across multiple continents and many primary languages. The brief is written in English and assumes English-speaking professional categories.
- **Impact:** A Tigrinya-speaking dissident in the Eritrean diaspora, a Dari-speaking journalist in Afghanistan, a Uyghur diaspora organizer — these are exactly the §2.1 threat tier and do not appear in §2.2's enumeration except by implication. The brief never names language localization as an audience question. The §5.6 UX surface "surfaces these properties to non-expert users through familiar messaging idioms" (§2.3 line 97) assumes idioms are familiar across languages, which is a UX research claim that may not survive.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should name language scope honestly. "v1 ships in English; the audience is therefore limited to English-comprehending users in the threat tier" is an honest statement. Acknowledging localization as a v1.5 or v2 commitment, with the implication that primary audiences in non-English-dominant jurisdictions are excluded from v1, would let me see whether I am even in scope.

### F5: Device-search-by-family/employer threat is not the developer's threat model

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 52 names "users in environments where security-conscious-device-choice is itself a tell beyond what they can absorb" as out of scope. §2.1 line 42 names "what to send, what to delete" as part of the discipline.
- **Impact:** "Security-conscious-device-choice is a tell" is the most acute case for many users in this threat tier — a woman whose husband searches her phone weekly, an adult child in a controlling religious family, a worker whose employer mandates MDM on personal devices, an undocumented worker whose phone may be seized in a workplace raid. Acknowledging this case as out of scope is honest but inadequate: the duress-wipe pattern referenced in §2.2 line 66 (v1.5 feature) explicitly addresses exactly this case, which means the developer has been thinking about it, but §2.2 still names it as out of audience. The audience-out and roadmap-in framings contradict each other.
- **Recommendation:** Reconcile §2.2 line 52 ("security-conscious device choice is itself a tell — out of scope") with §2.2 line 66 (v1.5 ships the duress pattern, which serves exactly that case). Either the audience is in scope from v1.5 onward and §2.2 should name them, or duress-wipe is for a different population and §2.2 should clarify which.

### F6: "Peer compromise as the proximate path to user compromise" is described as a v1 feature, but the audience this serves is narrower than stated

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 58: "The Recovery network surface in Section 3.3 directly addresses the failure mode that dominates this population: peer compromise as the proximate path to user compromise."
- **Impact:** This sentence describes my threat correctly but the architectural response (social recovery with 5+ peers, 3-of-5 quorum) is a defense against peer-graph compromise that scales with the size and stability of the peer graph. A user with a fragile, drifting, or partially compromised peer graph — which describes most of the §2.1 threat tier in my experience — is told their failure mode is being addressed by an architecture that requires the failure mode not to be present. This is a circularity the brief does not acknowledge.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should either describe the conditions under which social recovery actually defends against peer compromise (peer graph size, stability over time, independence of peers from the user's primary adversary), or describe what the user is supposed to do when those conditions are absent. "We address peer compromise via a recovery model that requires uncompromised peers" is not a defense for users whose peer graph is the attack surface.

## Minor findings

### F7: "Trained facilitator" is undefined

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 52. "Trained" is not specified.
- **Impact:** I do not know what training I would need to become a facilitator for my peer group, or what training the facilitator I am being onboarded by has. This matters because §2.2 makes the facilitator a load-bearing trust element.
- **Recommendation:** Specify or reference where facilitator training is defined (Section 8 or Section 6.3, presumably). A forward reference would suffice.

### F8: Civil-society researcher subgroup conflates two distinct populations

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 60: "Civil-society researchers. Studying targeted surveillance, documenting incident chains, analyzing forensic-extraction artifacts."
- **Impact:** Citizen Lab researchers (named example) and the activists whose phones Citizen Lab analyzes face overlapping but distinct threats. The architecture appropriate for the researchers (who have institutional support, mature OPSEC, peer networks in academia) is not necessarily appropriate for the activists. Folding them together obscures whether the v1 architecture serves both or only the researcher subgroup.
- **Recommendation:** Separate "researchers studying targeted surveillance" from "subjects of targeted surveillance who are themselves the analyzed population." The threat is similar; the support context is not.

### F9: "Dual-use technology workers and defense subcontractors" do not look like the rest of this audience

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 59: "Dual-use technology workers and defense subcontractors. Working on materials or systems that adversaries treat as targets."
- **Impact:** This subgroup has employer-mandated device policies, classified-program access discussions, and threat models that are explicitly closer to the "national-security tooling" tier §2.1 line 36 places above this audience. Including them reads as audience-stretching to make the user pool look larger. The brief itself notes at line 59 that they are "constrained by employer-imposed device policies in ways that complicate the GrapheneOS-Pixel requirement," which is to say they cannot use the v1 product.
- **Recommendation:** Either explain why this subgroup is included given that the architecture admittedly cannot serve them, or remove them from the audience enumeration.

### F10: "Honest" and "honestly" appear three times in §2.2

- **Evidence:** §2.2 lines 50, 52, 71 (and §2.3 line 98 echoes "An honest threat model").
- **Impact:** Repeating that the brief is honest while leaving major audience boundaries (language, support network, geography, peer graph viability) implicit reads to me as protesting too much. The honesty claim should be earned in the structure, not repeated as a property.
- **Recommendation:** Cut the rhetorical claim and let the structural choices speak. If §2.2 names every excluded subgroup explicitly, "honest" becomes redundant.

## Patterns

**Pattern 1: The audience is described as a population the developer studies rather than people the developer knows.** Throughout §2.2, the subject is "the user" or "users at this threat tier" or "the population." There is no concrete person, no named scenario, no specific journalist or organizer or lawyer whose operating conditions are described. The brief reads as if drawn from the public reporting of organizations that work with these populations (Tactical Tech, Front Line Defenders, Access Now — all named at line 57) rather than from direct relationships with users. §2.2 line 62 admits the pilot is "drawn from groups the developer has direct relationships with," but the rest of §2.2 is written at a remove. The result is an audience description that those of us inside the threat tier do not recognize as ourselves.

**Pattern 2: The architecture's prerequisites are described as boundary conditions, but they are actually the operational profile of users with the most resources.** GrapheneOS-Pixel, in-person onboarding, 5-peer recovery network, capacity to sustain "operational discipline" — each is named as a v1 prerequisite, but the population that meets all four is the institutionally-supported, geographically-proximate-to-the-developer, network-stable subset of the §2.1 threat tier. This subset is small and is not where the threat tier's most acute cases live. The brief never inspects this gap.

**Pattern 3: Populations the threat tier centrally includes are absent from §2.2's enumeration.** Sex workers, abuse survivors, undocumented organizers, queer people in criminalizing jurisdictions, religious minorities under family surveillance, low-income tenant organizers, prisoners' families. Each faces an adversary with both legal and extralegal reach (§2.1's definition of the threat tier). None appears in §2.2. The audience is constructed from professional categories with institutional backing rather than from the threat model's actual perimeter. From inside, this reads as the brief addressing the populations the developer has heard of through advocacy channels and missing the populations who do not have advocacy channels at all.

**Pattern 4: Time, fluency, and cognitive bandwidth are treated as unconstrained.** "Operational discipline" assumes I can pay attention to it. "Verify a new contact" assumes I have time to verify. "What to delete" assumes I have the cognitive surplus to make the deletion decision well. The brief never asks what fraction of cognitive bandwidth users at this threat tier actually have available. For many of us the answer is: almost none, because we are using all of it to survive the threat. A design brief that does not engage with this is, from my position, designing for a fantasy version of me with more capacity than I have.
