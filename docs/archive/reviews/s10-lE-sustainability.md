# §10 — Sustainability vs Maintainer-Comp Aspiration Lens

## Summary

§10 stages an honest phase-gated model that separates volunteer commitments from grant-funded ones, and its Phase D paragraph at brief line 1117 explicitly names maintainer compensation as aspirational rather than budgeted. The problem is structural: Phase D's $90-100K floor and $250K ceiling fund _everything except the human who runs it all_ — recurring audits, reviewer honoraria, foundation overhead, infrastructure, and localization are line items; release engineering, security response, board interface, partner coordination, reviewer-pool management, foundation administration, dead-man's-switch operation, and translator orchestration are not. The brief asks a single uncompensated developer to absorb that operational load _indefinitely_ on top of the volunteer baseline cadence already documented in D0008.

The brief does not name this as a sustainability cliff. §9.1's "long-horizon sustainability risk" (line 861) frames the risk as foundation/v2/v3 dependency on grants, not as "Phase D is structurally non-sustaining absent maintainer comp." §10.7's funding-risk enumeration (lines 1153-1160) covers subsidy-program failure, reviewer-honoraria failure, and incorporation funding failure — but does not enumerate "no funder closes a multi-year maintainer-comp grant" as a Phase D failure mode, even though §10.6's funding strategy (lines 1141-1149) and §10.5's source roster (lines 1119-1140) contain no source category that routinely funds maintainer salaries. The aspirational framing functionally describes the project's most likely state for the indefinite duration of Phase D, but is presented as a future grace rather than as a structural risk requiring a horizon and an honest contingency.

## Critical findings

### F1: Phase D budgets the foundation's _organizational_ costs while excluding the labor that operates the foundation, creating a sustainability cliff the brief does not name

- **Evidence:** Brief line 1117 frames maintainer comp as "the project's aspiration rather than its plan" and excludes it from the floor/ceiling at line 1114-1115. The Phase D line-item list at lines 1106-1110 includes _recurring audit cycle_ ($40-100K/year), _reviewer honoraria_ ($40-100K/year), _foundation overhead_ ($10-30K/year), _infrastructure_ ($1-3K/year), and _localization honoraria_ ($5-20K/year) — but no line for the work that orchestrates audits, manages the reviewer pool, runs board governance interface, coordinates partner relationships, manages release engineering, executes the dead-man's-switch monthly check-in (D0009 specifies 2-3 hours/month), maintains the trust-roots health report (§9.4 line 988, biennial at volunteer baseline), processes incident reports, and administers the foundation's actual day-to-day operations. §8.1 line 720 commits that "v1 implementation is solo," and D0008 establishes 4-6 month volunteer release cadence as expected slippage — but §10.4 implicitly transitions to steady-state without identifying when, or whether, this baseline ever ends. The "without maintainer compensation" qualifier on the totals (line 1112) is technically honest but operationally describes a foundation paying every contributor in its workflow except its primary engineer-and-administrator.
- **Impact:** Funders evaluating Phase D at the floor figure ($90-100K/year) see an institutionally-funded entity paying audit subsidies, reviewer honoraria, and accountant fees. They do not see that the same entity's sole engineering and operational capacity is uncompensated. Two failure modes converge: (a) the developer becomes uncompensated labor cross-subsidizing a foundation that pays everyone else, which §10.4 does not acknowledge as the unusual posture it is; (b) over a multi-year horizon, the developer's self-funding floor (§9.1 line 857 acknowledges this is finite) is exhausted, and Phase D collapses into D0009's sudden-unavailability contingency or §9.4's sunset — outcomes the brief reserves for catastrophe, not for the predictable consequence of indefinite uncompensated operations. The project then sunsets not because the architecture failed but because the funding model never funded the engineer.
- **Recommendation:** Add an explicit subsection (proposed §10.4.1 or a paragraph in §10.7) named "Phase D sustainability horizon without maintainer compensation." Name a numerical horizon (e.g., "the developer's volunteer-baseline contribution to Phase D operations is sustainable for N years under self-funded posture; beyond N, Phase D either secures multi-year maintainer-comp funding or sunsets per §9.4"). State plainly that the volunteer-time component of Phase D is not a permanent operating model. Either commit to the volunteer-time horizon as a public timeline funders can act against, or qualify the Phase D ceiling at line 1115 as "ceiling _conditional on indefinite developer volunteer time, which the project does not unilaterally commit to_."

### F2: §10's funding strategy and source roster contain no category that routinely funds maintainer salaries; Phase D's maintainer-time is sourced from nowhere named

- **Evidence:** §10.5 (lines 1119-1140) lists self/personal, subsidy programs (OTF Secure Audit, Cure53 mission rates, Mozilla Open Source Audit Awards, NLnet NGI Zero Trust), direct grant programs (OTF main, Ford, OSF, MOSS, European democracy funds, NLnet main, Knight), partner co-funding, individual donations, and hardware partnerships. The subsidy programs are explicitly audit-funding. The direct grants are "broader Phase B/C/D funding" but the line items they fund (per §10.3/10.4) are audits, reviewer honoraria, foundation overhead, localization — not maintainer FTE. §10.6 line 1148 mentions "direct grants typically support multi-year operational funding" but does not specify maintainer comp as a budget category these grants are sought for. By contrast, §10.4 line 1117 names "$150-250K/year per FTE" as the figure a funded maintainer line would require — the brief states the cost but does not identify the program structure that would close that line. None of the named programs is a routine source of multi-year engineering salary funding for open-source security projects at the threat tier; OTF main grants, Ford, OSF can include personnel costs, but a $150-250K/year multi-year personnel commitment is a different ask than a one-time audit subsidy, and the brief does not distinguish.
- **Impact:** A funder reading §10.5-10.6 cannot locate where Phase D's maintainer-time is supposed to come from. The brief presents Phase D as a coherent steady-state (line 1102: "what the project costs annually once the foundation is operating") but the cost category that dominates real-world security-engineering project budgets — engineering salary — is absent both from the budget and from the funding strategy that closes the budget. The asymmetry between "we budget audit subsidies because OTF Secure Audit funds them" and "we do not budget maintainer comp because no program funds it" is not acknowledged. The result is a strategy that, if executed flawlessly, funds the foundation but does not fund the engineer who operates it.
- **Recommendation:** In §10.5, add a "Multi-year operational grants for engineering capacity" category that names the actual program structures relevant (e.g., Sloan Foundation's open-source program, Chan Zuckerberg Initiative's Essential Open Source Software for Science where applicable, Internet Society Foundation organizational grants, sustained engineering grants from European NGI consortia at the project-not-task tier). State the pursuit difficulty honestly: these grants are larger, slower (12-24 month cycles), more competitive, and rarely cover a full engineering FTE at security-engineering market rates. In §10.6, add a sixth sequencing step naming "engineering-capacity grant pursuit" as a distinct, lower-probability long-horizon activity separate from audit subsidy and direct-grant pursuit. If no such category exists at scale for the project's threat tier, name that absence as the structural finding it is rather than leaving Phase D's maintainer-time implicit.

### F3: §9.1's project risks do not enumerate "no maintainer-comp grant closes; developer cannot sustain Phase D indefinitely" as a long-horizon failure mode

- **Evidence:** §9.1 (lines 849-869) names solo-developer SPOF (line 853), funding non-materialization in two horizons (lines 855-861), pilot non-validation (line 863), architectural mistakes (line 865), reviewer/witness pool erosion (line 867), and brief-to-funded-development timeline slippage (line 869). The "long-horizon sustainability risk" paragraph (line 861) names "foundation incorporation, v2 and v3 development, and partner-organization infrastructure" as the items dependent on multi-year funding — but does _not_ name "ongoing maintainer time at Phase D scale" as an item in that category. The risks acknowledge that "reviewer-pool erosion risk increases during grant-cycle transitions where honoraria continuity is uncertain" (line 861) — but the same logic applied to the maintainer is absent. The §10 funding-risk subsection (lines 1153-1160) covers the parallel territory and similarly does not enumerate the maintainer-comp gap as a Phase D failure mode.
- **Impact:** The brief takes reviewer pool sustainability seriously enough to surface the volunteer-to-honoraria-to-volunteer trajectory (§8.2 line 740) as a recruitment-disclosure requirement, but does not take the developer's equivalent trajectory seriously enough to surface it as a project risk. §9.1 line 853 establishes "at v1 scale, the developer is the project"; §10.4 implicitly assumes the same developer continues at Phase D scale indefinitely. The gap is that v1 SPOF is acknowledged but Phase D SPOF — same developer, larger workload, longer horizon, same lack of compensation — is not. A program officer doing due diligence on a Phase C or Phase D ask would notice that the multi-year sustainability story relies on a single uncompensated person, and the brief's own risk register does not surface this. The honest-disclosure posture §8 commits to (e.g., the volunteer-attestation surfacing in 8.2) breaks down at the maintainer line.
- **Recommendation:** Add a §9.1 bullet: "Maintainer-comp non-materialization at Phase D scale. The Phase D operational model assumes ongoing developer time at a workload that volunteer baseline (D0008) cannot sustain indefinitely. If no multi-year engineering-capacity grant closes within a stated horizon post-Phase-C transition, the project either reverts to extended volunteer-baseline cadence (with explicit operational consequences for foundation administration, partner coordination, and audit-cycle execution) or initiates the §9.4 sunset process. The conditions distinguishing these paths are named in the §10 sustainability-horizon language [F1 recommendation]." Cross-link the §9.1 bullet to §10's sustainability horizon and to D0008's volunteer-cadence framing.

## Significant findings

### F4: The "150-250K/year per FTE" figure (line 1117) under-states the actual labor scope, treating the maintainer as one engineer rather than as engineer + operations + governance interface

- **Evidence:** Line 1117 frames the comp figure as "a funded maintainer line at security-engineering market rates" — single engineer, single rate. The Phase D operational scope per §10.4 line items plus the unenumerated operational load (board interface §8.4 line 798; partner coordination §8.6; reviewer-pool management §8.2; release engineering §8.2; audit coordination §8.5; dead-man's-switch operation D0009; trust-roots health report §9.4 line 988; localization coordination §10.4 line 1110; incident response §9.4 line 990) corresponds to at least 1.0 engineering FTE _plus_ 0.3-0.5 of operations/program-management FTE for a foundation-governed security product. The brief at §8.1 line 722-727 anticipates "Part-time cryptographic consulting," "UX-focused engineer," and "Documentation and community-management role" as funded additions — but these are framed as Phase B-C aspirations, not as the Phase D steady-state team composition. The Phase D maintainer-comp aspiration is described as a single FTE; the workload described across §8 implies more.
- **Impact:** A funder reading "$150-250K/year per FTE would close the maintainer-comp gap" sees a manageable single-engineer ask. The actual minimum-viable Phase D team composition implied by §8's operational commitments is closer to 1.5-2.0 FTE total ($225-500K/year fully loaded). The understatement makes the maintainer-comp gap look smaller than it is, and a funder closing a single-FTE grant would still leave the foundation operationally under-resourced. The brief inadvertently undersells the size of the funding gap by framing comp as engineering-only.
- **Recommendation:** Revise line 1117 to specify "engineering FTE _plus_ a fractional operations/program-management role" with separate range estimates. Cross-reference §8.1's anticipated roles (cryptographic consulting, UX engineer, documentation/community-management) and clarify which of those roles persist into Phase D steady-state versus which are Phase B/C-bound implementation roles that conclude when v1.5 ships. State the Phase D minimum-viable team composition explicitly.

### F5: Foundation overhead ($10-30K/year, line 1108) likely understates real costs for a security-critical product foundation

- **Evidence:** Line 1108 budgets "$10,000-30,000 per year for board governance, accounting, fiscal infrastructure, regulatory compliance, jurisdiction-specific tax filings." The §8.4 board composition (line 798) anticipates a board including a cryptographic technical advisor, a partner-organization representative, a community representative with operational threat-tier experience, "an executive director or equivalent operational role (compensated when funded)," and a treasurer/audit-committee chair. The board composition implies governance costs: board meeting logistics, director liability insurance (D&O for a security non-profit operating in encryption-product distribution carries non-trivial premiums), board secretary work, regulatory filings across jurisdictions if the foundation accepts international donations (per D0010's tax-treaty considerations), accounting for grant-restricted vs unrestricted funds, annual audit of financial statements (separate from the cryptographic audit; required by many funders above certain grant sizes), and counsel retainer for ongoing regulatory questions (US 501(c)(3) Form 990, EU equivalent filings, encryption-product export regulation per D0010 line 46). The $10-30K range is plausible for a small grantmaking shell entity; it is low for a foundation that operates a security-critical product, holds custody of pilot-user encrypted contact rosters (per D0009 line 28), maintains signing identity infrastructure with documented compromise plans, and administers researcher safe-harbor commitments (D0012, §8.5 line 823).
- **Impact:** If foundation overhead is closer to $30-60K/year — closer to the operating cost of a comparable small security-tooling non-profit with board, accounting, D&O, audit, and counsel retainer — the Phase D floor moves from ~$90-100K to ~$110-130K and the ceiling from $250K to $280-300K. The understatement is not catastrophic but compounds with F4 (under-stated team scope) to systematically size Phase D below its realistic floor. A funder relying on the brief's figures to size a multi-year operating grant would under-fund the operation.
- **Recommendation:** Disaggregate the $10-30K range into components (board operations; D&O insurance; accounting + annual financial audit; regulatory filings; counsel retainer; jurisdiction-specific compliance). Either acknowledge the figures are pre-consultation estimates (consistent with D0010's posture on jurisdiction analysis) or revise the range to reflect comparable security non-profit operational costs. Add a note that foundation overhead at the upper end depends on jurisdiction (per D0010); the current "$10-30K range depends on incorporation jurisdiction per D0010" treatment is correct in form but the range itself may be low.

### F6: The §10.4 framing treats Phase D maintainer-comp as "aspirational" without acknowledging the unusual ethical posture of an organization that pays honoraria but not its primary engineer

- **Evidence:** §10.4 line 1107 budgets reviewer honoraria at $40-100K/year "fair relative to security-engineering market rates for the equivalent work" (per §8.2 line 740). §10.4 lines 1110 budgets localization honoraria for "native-speaker security-trainer translators." §10.4 line 1117 excludes maintainer comp from the same budget on aspirational grounds. The brief's own framing (§8.2 line 740) cites fairness-to-market-rates as the principle behind honoraria; the same principle applied to the developer would require maintainer-comp budgeting at the rates §10.4 line 1117 acknowledges. The asymmetry — honoraria for everyone else at fair market rates, volunteer time for the developer indefinitely — is not named as the unusual posture it is. §8.2 line 740 acknowledges the analogous volunteer-to-honoraria transition for reviewers as "the project's intent not to extract indefinite volunteer labor," and commits to surfacing the funding state to the reviewer pool transparently. The equivalent commitment for the developer (surfacing to whom? funders? successors? the developer themselves?) is not articulated.
- **Impact:** The brief's honest-disclosure posture (visible elsewhere, e.g., the F-series in §8/9 reviews) breaks down at this specific seam. Funders evaluating Phase D see an aspirational note about maintainer comp but not the ethical inversion: the foundation will pay its volunteer reviewers, its translators, its accountants, its board's executive director (per §8.4 line 798), and its auditors — and will not pay the engineer who built the product and operates the foundation. This is unusual for open-source security non-profits (most that reach foundation scale fund at least one engineering FTE before paying contributor honoraria) and the brief does not acknowledge it as unusual. A program officer noticing the inversion may read the brief's aspirational framing as understatement of the project's actual financial fragility.
- **Recommendation:** In the line 1117 paragraph, explicitly name the asymmetry: "The Phase D model funds reviewer honoraria, translator honoraria, foundation operating costs, and auditor engagements while leaving maintainer time at volunteer baseline. This is unusual for an open-source security non-profit at this operational scale, and the project acknowledges it as such; the aspiration to fund maintainer time at market rates is the project's commitment to not perpetuate this posture indefinitely." Pair this with the F1 sustainability horizon so the asymmetry is bounded in time rather than presented as the steady state.

## Minor findings

### F7: The §10.4 audit amortization figure assumes biennial cadence under §8.5 line 815 but does not name the cadence's volunteer-baseline contingency

- **Evidence:** Line 1106 amortizes the recurring audit to $40-100K/year on a "second external cryptographic audit approximately 18-24 months after the first" cadence (per §8.5 line 815). §8.5 line 815 frames this as the post-launch commitment, but the parallel reviewer-honoraria text (§8.2 line 740) frames cadence as "depends on grant scope." If audit funding cycles are not synchronized with the 18-24 month cadence, the actual audit cadence stretches and amortization changes accordingly. The brief does not name this dependency in the Phase D figure.
- **Impact:** Minor — the amortization range is plausible across realistic cadence variation, but the figure is presented as a steady-state line item without naming the cadence dependency on grant timing.
- **Recommendation:** Add a parenthetical to line 1106: "amortization assumes the 18-24 month cadence per §8.5 line 815; actual cadence depends on subsidy-program timing per §10.6 and may stretch the inter-audit interval under volunteer-baseline funding posture."

### F8: §10.7 "Funding risks" enumerates reviewer-honoraria failure (line 1158) but not the structurally identical maintainer-volunteer trajectory

- **Evidence:** Line 1158 names the reviewer-honoraria failure mode and the volunteer-attestation reversion path with surfaced transparency. The maintainer-comp non-materialization equivalent is absent. The brief acknowledges the reviewer trajectory because it commits to honoraria as the operational model; it does not acknowledge the maintainer trajectory because it never committed to maintainer comp in the first place. The asymmetry is structural.
- **Impact:** The §10.7 enumeration looks complete on reviewer-side risk but is silent on the larger and more concentrated risk on the developer side. Reads as the brief enumerating risks for the parties it pays while not enumerating the risk concentrated in the unpaid party.
- **Recommendation:** Add a §10.7 bullet symmetric to the reviewer-honoraria bullet, naming the maintainer-volunteer trajectory's structural risks (foundation administration degradation, audit-cycle slippage, partner-coordination gaps, dead-man's-switch operational continuity, release-engineering bandwidth) and the contingency the project commits to (per F1/F3 recommendations).

### F9: §10.8 "What this section does not promise" mentions maintainer comp only as an undelivered specific (line 1169), missing the opportunity to be explicit about the sustainability cliff

- **Evidence:** Line 1169 reads: "A specific maintainer compensation arrangement. The Phase D aspiration is identified; the funding configuration that supports it has not closed." This is a true non-promise but reads as "we will not commit to a specific dollar figure for maintainer comp," not as "we cannot guarantee Phase D sustains indefinitely under the maintainer-comp absence." The honest framing the §10.8 list aspires to (line 1162-1172) is undercut by the soft phrasing on the most material non-promise.
- **Impact:** Funders reading §10.8 see the same disclosure posture as the §10.5 funding-source list — a non-promise of specifics that does not actually surface the sustainability cliff. The §10.8 list is otherwise candid (e.g., line 1168: "the project reaches Phase C or D... depends on... funding decisions the project does not control"); this line stops short of the same candor on the most consequential question.
- **Recommendation:** Revise line 1169 to: "A specific maintainer compensation arrangement, _or that Phase D operations sustain past N years without one_. The Phase D aspiration is identified; the funding configuration that supports it has not closed, and the project does not commit Phase D to indefinite volunteer maintainer time. The sustainability horizon and contingency paths are stated in §10.4.1 [or wherever F1's horizon language lands]." This makes the §10.8 non-promise match the actual structural ask of the funding model.

## Patterns

**P1: Honest disclosure is rigorous everywhere except at the maintainer line.** §10 and §8 collectively make several honest-disclosure moves — volunteer-attestation surfacing in §8.2 line 740, audit-budget bound dependency in §8.5 line 813, jurisdictional analysis depth caveat in D0010, "what this section does not promise" in §10.8, the §9.1 enumeration of project risks. Each of these surfaces an uncomfortable truth the brief committed to surfacing. The maintainer-comp asymmetry is the equivalent uncomfortable truth that does not get the same treatment: the framing at line 1117 is technically accurate but functionally pre-empts the conversation by labeling the gap "aspirational" rather than "the structural sustainability cliff Phase D rests on." The brief's honest-disclosure posture is asymmetric — it surfaces honest disclosures most rigorously where the disclosure is about other parties (reviewers, partner organizations, auditors, jurisdictions), and softens where the disclosure concerns the project's own financial fragility at the maintainer line.

**P2: Steady-state framing without horizon.** §10.4 is the only phase described as steady-state ("what the project costs annually once the foundation is operating and the v1 broader release has shipped," line 1102). Steady-state framing implies time-invariance — Phase D is what the project costs _per year_, every year. The brief does not bound Phase D in time. But the implicit assumption (uncompensated developer continues indefinitely) cannot be time-invariant — it is bounded by the developer's self-funding floor (§9.1 line 857 acknowledges this is finite), by burnout (§10.1 line 1053 names this for Phase A but not Phase D), and by the developer's life circumstances (D0009 names the catastrophic scenarios but not the gradual ones). The phase-gate model is rigorous for A→B→C transitions but treats D as a destination rather than as another transition awaiting its own funding event. A complete phase-gated model would name Phase D as bounded — either bounded by maintainer-comp funding closing (Phase D→E with funded steady-state), or bounded by sunset.

**P3: Cost-side rigor without strategy-side rigor on the same item.** §10.4 line 1117 names the maintainer-comp _cost_ ($150-250K/year per FTE) with appropriate specificity. §10.5/10.6 do not name the _funding strategy_ that closes that cost with the same specificity. The brief is precise about what maintainer comp would cost; vague about how it would be paid. This is the inverse of the audit-funding treatment (§10.6 step 3 line 1147), where the strategy is precise (named subsidy programs, parallel applications, named cycles) and the cost matches (D0011's specific subsidy-tier vs. market-rate bands). The asymmetry between specificity on the cost and vagueness on the strategy for the same line item is itself a signal that maintainer comp does not have a real funding path in the brief's own model.
