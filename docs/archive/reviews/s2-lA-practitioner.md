# §2 — Civil-Society Security Practitioner Lens

## Summary

§2 reads as written by someone who has read the casework literature carefully (Citizen Lab, Amnesty, Front Line Defenders, Access Now are correctly named) but not staffed an emergency Helpline rotation. The threat-tier description in §2.1 is accurate at the strategic level; the audience taxonomy in §2.2 is where the brief loses contact with operating reality. The five listed audience subgroups are treated as a single intersection ("v1 architecture targets the intersection of these communities" — line 62) when their threat models, device situations, support relationships, and consent-to-be-trained capacities differ in ways that determine whether v1 can actually onboard them. Several distinctions practitioners draw daily — between a target who is _currently_ under active surveillance and one who is preparing for an anticipated escalation, between a journalist with an outlet's digital-security team and an organizer with no institutional support, between a user whose adversary is co-located (family, employer, partner) and one whose adversary is remote — are absent.

The most consequential gap is structural: §2 contains no language about who in the user's life the user is _hiding the tool itself from_, and the GrapheneOS-Pixel-plus-in-person-ceremony posture is itself a tell in exactly the populations §2.2 names. §2.1's "operational discipline" framing, while technically defensible, drifts toward language practitioners would flag as user-blaming if a partner organization circulated it as recruiting material.

## Critical findings

### F1: The five audience subgroups have incompatible threat models and are treated as a single intersection

- **Evidence:** §2.2 lines 56–60 list five distinct communities (journalists in contested-press environments; NGO field staff; organizers in active dissent; dual-use tech workers; civil-society researchers), then line 62 collapses them: "The v1 architecture targets the intersection of these communities where users (a) have access to or can be supplied with GrapheneOS-Pixel hardware, (b) operate in trust networks where social recovery is viable, and (c) can be onboarded in person by a facilitator."
- **Impact:** From casework: a journalist at a mid-sized outlet typically has institutional IT support, a corporate-issued device they cannot replace, and a threat model dominated by source protection (where the _source_, not the journalist, is the recovery-peer-equivalent risk). An organizer in active dissent typically has no institutional support, a personal device they own, and a threat model dominated by peer compromise via arrest cascades. A humanitarian field worker has an employer-mandated device with MDM, a threat model that includes their employer's risk tolerance, and travel patterns that put them through hostile borders on a predictable cadence. A dual-use tech worker has employer-imposed device policy that explicitly forbids the GrapheneOS posture. A civil-society researcher (Citizen Lab, Amnesty Security Lab) is closer to the developer's own profile and is the _least_ representative of the populations the others serve. These groups do not have a viable intersection on (a)+(b)+(c) simultaneously; the v1 audience as architected is closest to the researcher subgroup and one slice of the organizer subgroup. Naming all five gives §2 the appearance of broad civil-society applicability while v1 actually serves a narrow band.
- **Recommendation:** Replace the five-bullet list-and-intersect framing with a single explicit v1 user archetype (the one the developer actually has direct relationships with, per line 62's pilot reality) and a separate paragraph naming the four other groups as v1.5/v2 targets contingent on specific architectural extensions (employer-device support, source-protection workflows, MDM coexistence). The current framing inflates v1's reach in a way practitioners reviewing the brief will discount, and pilot users will be more accurately served by being told who v1 is and is not for.

### F2: No device-custody-transfer scenario; co-located adversary patterns absent

- **Evidence:** §2 describes the device as belonging to a user against external adversaries. The custody language assumes the user holds their own device under their own control except during defined seizure events (border, detention — addressed in §3.2 but not §2). §2.2 line 52: "users without a peer network capable of holding recovery shares responsibly" is the only acknowledgment that the user's intimate network can be adversarial, and it is phrased as an exclusion criterion rather than a designed-for case.
- **Impact:** In Helpline and Front Line Defenders casework, an enormous share of mobile-device compromise of civil-society targets — particularly women journalists, women human-rights defenders, and LGBTQ+ organizers in jurisdictions where their identity is criminalized — involves a co-located adversary: an intimate partner, family member, employer's IT department, or roommate-informant who has physical access to the device daily and social access to the user's recovery network. The GrapheneOS-Pixel posture is _itself_ a visible signal to this adversary class ("why is your phone different from everyone else's"), the in-person provisioning ceremony with a facilitator is itself a meeting the co-located adversary can ask about, and the recovery-peer designation conversation per D0005 happens in front of (or is later compelled to be disclosed to) the co-located adversary. None of this is in §2, and the audience in §2.2 includes populations (organizers, field staff) where this pattern is the dominant compromise vector documented in the operational reports §2 cites approvingly.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should add an explicit subsection — "Who this tool is and is not for when the adversary is in the room" — that names co-located adversary as a casework-dominant pattern, acknowledges that v1's posture (visible distinct device, facilitator meeting, recovery-peer ceremony) is high-friction in this scenario, and states honestly that v1 is appropriate for users whose primary adversary is remote and not appropriate as a primary tool for users whose primary adversary shares physical space with them. Without this, the brief is recruiting users into a product that may make their situation worse, which is the failure mode trainers most want to avoid endorsing.

### F3: "Operational discipline" framing drifts into user-burden language that partner organizations will not endorse

- **Evidence:** §2.1 line 42: "Operational discipline absent from the design. The tools are built for users who do not need to think about the threat. The threat tier this product addresses includes users who must think about it constantly — what to send, what to delete, when to verify a new contact, how to recover if compromised — and existing tools provide little structural support for these operations beyond the cryptographic primitive layer." §2.2 line 71: "the operational discipline the design assumes a support network provides." §2.2 line 50: "Naming the audience honestly is part of the architectural commitment."
- **Impact:** The framing is technically correct but practitioners reading it hear something the author may not intend. "Users who must think about it constantly" is, in casework, often a user who is already in trauma response from prior surveillance exposure, prior arrest of a colleague, or prior loss of a source — and asking them to _additionally_ sustain "operational discipline" is the kind of language that triggers, in some Front Line Defenders staff specifically, an objection that the security industry asks users to do what the industry should be doing for them. The audience subgroups in §2.2 disproportionately include people the practitioner community would describe as already-overwhelmed. Cairn's design choice (operational discipline as architectural commitment rather than usability burden the design hides) is a legitimate one; the brief's _language_ around it makes the choice sound like a feature when it is a cost. Partner organizations whose endorsement v1 needs for pilot facilitation will be sensitive to language that sounds like "if compromise happens it's because the user did not sustain the discipline."
- **Recommendation:** Reframe §2.1's third bullet and §2.2's "operational discipline" references so the cost is named as the project's cost (the project commits to building structural support that reduces the discipline burden where it can; the discipline that remains is acknowledged as a real cost users absorb in exchange for the threat tier) rather than as the audience's cost ("the discipline the design assumes a support network provides"). The current phrasing positions the user as accountable for sustaining the architecture; the relationship should be reversed.

## Significant findings

### F4: Pilot recruitment from "groups the developer has direct relationships with" elides the consent-and-power dynamics practitioners watch for

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 62: "The pilot deployment specifically draws from groups the developer has direct relationships with so the facilitator role can be sustained at pilot scale."
- **Impact:** Recruiting research-or-pilot subjects from one's own social and political network is a familiar pattern in civil-society tech and one that experienced trainers approach carefully. The pilot population is being asked to (a) accept a device the developer purchased and configured, (b) accept identity provisioning the developer mediates, (c) designate recovery peers in the developer's presence, and (d) trust the developer's operational discipline for their device supply chain (§3.3 line 168 acknowledges "the developer becomes the supply chain for the pilot"). When the population is already in social relationship with the developer, the refusal of any of these steps carries social cost. Front Line Defenders' own protection-work guidance specifically flags this dynamic; the brief does not. There is no language in §2 about how the pilot handles a user who wants to _leave_ the pilot, refuse a feature, or report that the tool made their situation worse without that report carrying friction with their social relationship to the developer.
- **Recommendation:** Add a short paragraph (in §2.2 or referenced from it) on pilot consent and exit: how informed consent is obtained when the recruiter is also the facilitator and a member of the social network; how a pilot user reports negative outcomes through a channel that is not the developer; what happens if a pilot user wants to stop using the tool mid-pilot. This is standard practice in academic IRB-reviewed protective-tech studies and partner organizations may require it explicitly before facilitating.

### F5: Audience definition omits the user's relationship with their existing tools and people

- **Evidence:** §2.2 describes audience by adversary threat tier and platform constraint. Nothing about which tools the audience currently relies on (Signal for everyday work, WhatsApp for family, Telegram channels for movement coordination, encrypted email for sources, organization-mandated platforms for paid work) or which of those relationships Cairn is or is not expected to replace.
- **Impact:** From the practitioner side: civil-society users in 2026 typically run 3–6 messaging tools concurrently because different counterparties use different tools, and security training conversations are dominated not by "should you adopt X" but by "which conversations move to X and how do you keep the old tool running for everyone else." A tool that demands a separate dedicated GrapheneOS-Pixel device implicitly demands the user carry two phones, which is a known significant signal in many threat environments §2.2 cites. §2 never addresses two-phone reality. The closest acknowledgment is §2.2 line 52's "users in environments where security-conscious-device-choice is itself a tell beyond what they can absorb" — which is honest but incomplete, since the two-phone pattern is itself a tell distinct from device choice.
- **Recommendation:** Add to §2.2 a paragraph that names the carrying-two-phones operational reality, names which counterparty relationships v1 is designed to host (presumably the user's most sensitive contacts and the recovery-peer network), and names which relationships v1 explicitly does not try to displace (paid-work, family-of-origin contacts who will not migrate, sources who refuse to install another tool). This shapes pilot recruitment honestly and shapes user expectations correctly.

### F6: Threat-tier-to-onboarding mismatch is not acknowledged

- **Evidence:** §2.1 line 36 places the audience at "state-or-near-state-capable" adversary tier. §2.2 line 52 specifies onboarding as "in person by a trained facilitator (the developer in the v1 pilot)." D0005 line 60 specifies the in-person provisioning ceremony must include peer-challenge establishment.
- **Impact:** In casework, the population at the highest threat tier is the population for whom _meeting an unknown facilitator in person to set up a new security tool_ is itself a meaningful risk event — observed meetings, follow-on inquiries to attendees, in some cases pressure on the facilitator after the fact. The brief treats in-person provisioning as a security property (which it is, cryptographically) without acknowledging that for the highest-threat-tier user it is also a casework risk event the user may not be able to absorb. Practitioners running secure-handoff workshops in hostile environments routinely do compartment shifts (separate locations, rotated facilitators, time-of-day variation) precisely because the workshop itself attracts surveillance interest. §2 does not address this.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should add a sentence acknowledging that the in-person provisioning posture is itself an operational exposure for the highest-threat-tier audience, and that the pilot's audience is therefore the _subset_ of the threat tier for whom the provisioning event is operationally absorbable. This is consistent with the project's existing honesty framing and shrinks the audience claim correctly.

### F7: The "trust network capable of holding recovery shares responsibly" assumption maps poorly onto several listed populations

- **Evidence:** §2.2 line 52: "users without a peer network capable of holding recovery shares responsibly" is named as out-of-scope. Line 62 says v1 targets "the intersection of these communities where users... (b) operate in trust networks where social recovery is viable." §2.2 line 58 also positions organizers' recovery network around the failure mode that peer compromise is "the proximate path to user compromise."
- **Impact:** Across the listed populations, the "peer network capable of holding recovery shares responsibly" assumption holds well only for established journalists at institutional outlets and for civil-society researchers (the populations with stable colleague relationships and clear identity verification). For organizers in active dissent — where movement composition turns over rapidly, where members exit after arrests, where adversary-controlled or adversary-curious participants are documented to enter networks — the recovery peer pool is precisely the surface §3.3 line 164 names as "high-value target." For humanitarian field staff, the most trusted peers are often other field staff in the same operational area, whose simultaneous compromise (single border-crossing checkpoint, single hotel) is a real pattern. Without acknowledging these subgroup-specific recovery-pool challenges in §2, the audience claim implies a uniform "trust network" property that does not exist uniformly across the named communities.
- **Recommendation:** §2.2 should briefly differentiate the recovery-peer-feasibility assumption across the named subgroups: for whom it is straightforward, for whom it requires operational care, for whom it may not be viable at v1. This is a real architectural constraint on who can use v1 and is more honest than naming all five populations as v1 audiences with a generic feasibility caveat.

## Minor findings

### F8: "Authoritarian contexts" is the only national-political-system descriptor used

- **Evidence:** §2.1 line 36: "organizers in authoritarian contexts." §2.2 line 56–60 uses neutral phrasing for other populations.
- **Impact:** In practitioner usage, much of the casework that motivates this product happens in jurisdictions that are not customarily described as "authoritarian": EU member states using Pegasus against journalists (Greece, Hungary, Poland, Spain), the US targeting environmental and Indigenous-rights organizers, the UK applying RIPA §49 (referenced in D0002) to compelled decryption against ordinary defendants. The "authoritarian" framing risks reproducing the audience's own complaint that Western security tooling treats threat as something that happens elsewhere. The rest of §2 mostly avoids this trap; the one usage stands out.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "authoritarian contexts" with a description that does not bound by regime type — e.g. "organizers whose coordination activity is itself the subject of state interest" (which §2.2 line 58 already uses, more accurately).

### F9: §2.1's reference to "users whose intellectual property is itself a target" sits uneasily with the civil-society framing

- **Evidence:** §2.1 line 44: "journalists in countries where press freedom is contested, organizers in active dissent, dual-use technology workers whose intellectual property is itself a target."
- **Impact:** In civil-society practitioner language, "intellectual property" is the framing used by employers and trade-secret litigation; the population the brief means here (defense subcontractors, dual-use workers) is real but their threat model centers on national-security adjacency and employer-coerced compliance rather than IP-as-target. The phrasing reads as the brief importing a corporate-security frame into a civil-society section, which is jarring and may make civil-society partners reading the brief wonder whose product this is.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "whose intellectual property is itself a target" with a description that matches the threat (e.g. "whose work is adjacent to national-security material their employers cannot fully protect them on"). Keep the dual-use subgroup; reframe their threat.

### F10: The brief never names _language_ as an audience-defining constraint

- **Evidence:** §2.2 audience subgroups are defined by role and threat, not by language. Section 7 mentions localization as a v1.5+ item (per the broader brief context).
- **Impact:** v1 ships in English-only by default unless otherwise stated. The populations §2.2 names that practitioners actually serve operate in dozens of languages where security-tool localization is the operational gate — a tool that exists only in English serves a different subset of "journalists in contested-press environments" than the population the brief implies. This is a small finding only because v1's narrow pilot scope (10–15 users known to the developer) likely renders it moot for v1 specifically, but the audience claim in §2.2 about the populations Cairn targets needs to acknowledge that v1's audience is also bounded by the language(s) the v1 UI ships in.
- **Recommendation:** Add a sentence to §2.2 noting language coverage as an audience constraint v1 does not address and v1.5+ partially addresses.

## Patterns

**P1: Architectural honesty extends to threats but not to user populations.** §2.1's threat description is properly hedged ("varies sharply by jurisdiction," "casual-privacy use cases where its operational discipline would be friction"). §2.2's audience description is not similarly hedged: it names five populations and asserts an intersection without acknowledging that the intersection is small and the populations are heterogeneous in ways that determine architectural fit. The honesty discipline applied to the threat tier should be applied to the audience taxonomy.

**P2: The user's intimate network is missing from §2.** §2 describes external adversaries, network adversaries, and the peer-recovery network as a designated trust circle. The user's family, household, employer, romantic relationships, and the social proximity of the developer-as-facilitator to the pilot population are absent. In casework these are first-order determinants of whether a tool can be safely adopted. Findings F2, F4, and (partially) F7 all manifest this pattern.

**P3: "Architectural commitment" language compresses a cost the user pays into a feature the project ships.** §2.1's "operational discipline" framing and §2.2's "architectural cost... justifiable for that population" framing both name real properties but in language that moves the cost into the architecture and out of the user's lived experience. Practitioners reading the brief will hear the unmoved cost. F3 makes this most concretely; the pattern recurs lightly through both §2.1 and §2.2.
