# Architecture Simplification — Comparable Products Lens

## Summary

Cairn's design brief positions itself in a landscape of seven products (Briar, SimpleX, Signal, Threema, Tails, SecureDrop, GrapheneOS, F-Droid) that each serve substantially overlapping audiences with materially simpler architectures. Section 4.3 argues that Cairn's four-commitment integration (three-tier identity, Sigsum-anchored social attestation graph, social recovery, multi-party release security) is the differentiator no comparator combines. This review tests that claim commitment-by-commitment against the comparators §2.3 names. Where Cairn's added complexity delivers an audience-serving property the comparator cannot — and where the v1 audience (§2.2) actually requires that property — the commitment is justified. Where a comparator already serves the same audience without the commitment, or where Cairn's complexity buys a property the v1 audience does not yet need, the commitment is defensible engineering rather than necessary engineering.

The headline finding: three of the four commitments survive comparable-product scrutiny for v1 (three-tier identity vs. SimpleX/Signal; social recovery vs. Briar/Threema; trust graph vs. Briar's introduction protocol). The fourth — the recruited 5+ reviewer pool with 3-of-5 Sigsum-anchored attestation threshold — is the weakest under this lens. GrapheneOS, F-Droid, and Signal Android each demonstrate that overlapping-audience products ship credible release-security postures with materially simpler distributed-trust models than what §5.5 commits Cairn to building from scratch at solo-developer scale.

## Critical findings (where comparable products' simplicity is correct)

### F1: F-Droid's rebuild pipeline already provides v1's distinct-from-developer attestation; recruiting a parallel reviewer pool duplicates effort F-Droid does at lower cost

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.5 commits to recruiting 5+ external reviewers with a 3-of-5 attestation threshold for every release, with attestations Sigsum-anchored, witness-cosigned, honoraria-funded (§10.3 Phase C dependency), conditional on Q3 funding and Q5 partner outreach (§6.1 release-security framing). §4.3 names this as a primary Cairn differentiator: "substitutes the developer's single signing identity with a distributed trust set."
- **Comparable product without it:** F-Droid. The F-Droid rebuild/resign pipeline — explicitly named in §4.3 as prior art — constitutes independent attestation: F-Droid fetches the source, rebuilds the APK with their toolchain, and resigns with F-Droid's key. Briar, Signal Android, and many other security-sensitive Android apps ride this pipeline as a second signing authority that operates outside the developer's control. F-Droid's rebuild posture is not a recruited pool of five named individuals — it is a single project-operated independent build pipeline whose output users can compare against the developer's signed artifact.
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** A recruited pool of five named, geographically- and institutionally-diverse reviewers delivers a structurally stronger property than F-Droid's single-pipeline rebuild: jurisdictional diversity at the attestation layer (one US-based pipeline can be served process; five reviewers in five jurisdictions cannot), institutional diversity (one F-Droid team is one organization), and the ability to issue source-review attestations in v1 before reproducible builds land in v1.5 (F-Droid's rebuild presupposes reproducibility, which §5.5 acknowledges is 4–8 weeks of toolchain work deferred to v1.5). The reviewer-pool model gives Cairn a v1 release-security story without the reproducible-build prerequisite.
- **What v1 loses if Cairn matches comparable simplicity:** Without the recruited pool, v1 ships with: (a) developer Sigstore signature + Rekor entry as primary attestation, (b) F-Droid rebuild as independent attestation once v1.5 reproducible builds land, (c) §5.5 multi-channel distribution unchanged, (d) Sigsum log of releases unchanged. v1 loses jurisdictional diversity at attestation and loses any independent attestation between v1 release and v1.5 reproducible builds — a gap of however long v1→v1.5 takes. That gap is the actual cost of matching F-Droid simplicity, and §6.1 already acknowledges v1 ships with the source-vs-binary supply-chain gap unaddressed.
- **Recommendation:** Defer reviewer-pool recruitment to v1.5, coupled with the reproducible-build transition F-Droid presupposes. v1 ships with developer Sigstore + Rekor + multi-channel + Sigsum release log. v1.5 lands reproducible builds + F-Droid pipeline as the v1.5 independent-attestation channel. v1.5+ optionally adds the 5-of-N recruited pool if Phase C honoraria funding closes (§10.3), as a strengthening layer rather than the primary v1 release-security story. This matches Briar's trajectory exactly — Briar shipped reproducible builds in 2017 and rides F-Droid; Briar does not maintain a recruited reviewer pool, yet is the comparator §2.3 acknowledges as serving the closest threat tier to Cairn's audience. If Briar's release-security posture serves Briar's audience (a substantial overlap with Cairn's), the marginal property Cairn's recruited pool adds over Briar's posture is what the v1 audience does not yet require.

### F2: GrapheneOS already runs a multi-signer release process; v1 can ride GrapheneOS's posture rather than building a parallel one for the application layer

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.5 builds an application-layer release security stack independent of GrapheneOS: two signing layers (long-lived APK key + per-release Sigstore identity), reviewer attestations, Sigsum anchoring, witness cosignature pool, rollback resistance, multi-channel distribution, signing-identity-compromise plan. §3.4 names GrapheneOS as a trust root; §6.1 names GrapheneOS-on-Pixel as the v1 platform.
- **Comparable product without it:** GrapheneOS itself. GrapheneOS ships a multi-signer release process with reproducible builds and a public verification posture; the device baseline Cairn already depends on. An application running on GrapheneOS inherits GrapheneOS's platform-integrity guarantees: verified boot, hardware attestation, the GrapheneOS update channel for OS updates.
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** Application-layer release security is a different problem from OS-layer release security. GrapheneOS's release process attests the OS; it cannot attest a third-party application's APK. Cairn's stack attests the Cairn application specifically. The §5.5 stack is therefore not duplicating GrapheneOS's work — it is addressing a surface GrapheneOS does not cover (application updates, §3.3 Update channel surface). This finding is structurally weaker than F1: there is no GrapheneOS feature Cairn can ride for application-layer attestation.
- **What v1 loses if Cairn matches comparable simplicity:** Cairn cannot match comparable simplicity here without abandoning the application-update threat the §3.3 Update channel surface names. The structural question is not whether to defer release security but whether to ship a simpler v1 release-security posture than §5.5 commits to.
- **Recommendation:** Keep release security in v1 with the simplification F1 proposes: developer Sigstore + Rekor + Sigsum release log + multi-channel distribution, deferring the recruited reviewer pool to v1.5 alongside reproducible builds and F-Droid integration. This finding's effect is to reinforce F1's recommendation rather than introduce a separate simplification. §5.5 should explicitly cite GrapheneOS's release process as the precedent for "multi-signer reproducible-build release security at GrapheneOS audience overlap," with the v1.5 transition aligning Cairn's posture with GrapheneOS's rather than building Cairn's own variant.

## Significant findings

### F3: Briar's introduction protocol + BQP delivers small-scale graph-shaped trust for Briar's audience without a transparency log; v1's audience overlaps Briar's substantially

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.2 commits to a nine-field signed-operation schema with prior-hash chain, five operation types (attestation, attestation withdrawal, key compromise revocation, introduction, key rotation), cascade quarantine semantics with hard- and soft-flag splits, 90-day stale-flag auto-quarantine, Sigsum commitment-only anchoring with witness cosignature, and trust evaluation against the live log on demand. §4.3 names the trust graph as a Cairn differentiator: "attests social structure" rather than just "key continuity."
- **Comparable product without it:** Briar. §2.3 acknowledges Briar's trust model: introduction protocol (mutual contacts cryptographically introducing two users), Bramble Question Protocol (BQP) for shared-secret verification, forums/private groups with membership cryptographically rooted in introducer signatures — "small-scale graph-shaped trust without a transparency log." Briar serves an audience §2.3 explicitly characterizes as "journalists and activists in hostile environments" — overlapping substantially with Cairn's §2.2 audience.
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** Cairn's trust graph provides three properties Briar's introduction protocol does not: (a) public auditability via Sigsum commitments — a user (or an external auditor) can detect equivocation across the user's view; Briar's introductions are pairwise and unauditable from outside the participants, (b) attestation withdrawal as a distinct operation from key-compromise revocation, with cascade semantics that prevent post-compromise laundering — Briar's model does not formalize this split, and (c) the nine-field schema includes context, strength, and expiry — calibration the user can act on, where Briar's binary "introduced or not" is coarser. These properties are real, but the audience-serving question is whether the v1 audience requires them or whether Briar's coarser model suffices for the v1 pilot.
- **What v1 loses if Cairn matches comparable simplicity:** If Cairn adopted Briar's introduction-protocol model with BQP-style shared-secret verification instead of the §5.2 trust graph, v1 would lose: public auditability of trust state (the user could not detect equivocation from outside their view), the formal attestation-withdrawal-vs-compromise-revocation split that closes the cascade-laundering attack §5.2 identifies, and the structured operation envelope with context/strength/expiry. v1 would gain: substantial simplification of the cryptographic engineering surface (§4.3 acknowledges the trust graph as "substantial original cryptographic engineering at the integration boundary"), removal of the Sigsum-commitment-only dependency from the v1 critical path, and alignment with a model already operating in the §2.3 comparator with the closest threat-tier overlap.
- **Recommendation:** Keep the trust graph but acknowledge in §4.3 that Briar's introduction protocol + BQP serves an overlapping audience at lower architectural cost. The cascade-quarantine semantics §5.2 develops (the hard/soft revocation split closing the laundering attack) are genuinely novel and audience-serving for users whose threat tier includes post-compromise attestation issuance — a real attack on Briar's model that §5.2 correctly identifies. The Sigsum-commitment-only anchoring is what makes the audit property external rather than internal to the user's view. Both properties are defensible at the v1 audience tier. The simplification this finding suggests is editorial rather than architectural: §4.3 currently positions the trust graph as a clean differentiator from a landscape that does not have one; the honest framing is that Briar has a smaller trust graph, Cairn has a larger one, and the v1 audience requires Cairn's larger one specifically because of the external-auditability and cascade-laundering-resistance properties.

### F4: Threema's centralized paid Swiss product serves an audience adjacent to Cairn's without any of Cairn's four commitments; the audience-overlap question deserves direct treatment

- **Cairn commitment:** §4.3 names four commitments (three-tier identity, trust graph, social recovery, multi-party release security) as compounding rather than additive. §2.3 names Threema as "the closest existing analogue to Cairn's identifier-less, jurisdiction-aware, paid-and-deliberate positioning."
- **Comparable product without it:** Threema. §2.3 acknowledges Threema is "identifier-less (ID-not-phone-number) paid product used by parts of the Swiss government and several European militaries for unclassified communications." Threema ships none of Cairn's four commitments: single signing identity for the company, centralized service operation, closed-source client and server, no integrated trust-graph or social-recovery. Threema's audience overlap with Cairn's: parts of European government, militaries, journalist/civil-society users in jurisdictions where Swiss centralization is acceptable.
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** §2.3 names Cairn's contribution above Threema as: "open-source-auditable release security, the social-attestation trust graph, and the integration with peer-to-peer-over-Tor protocols (SimpleX, Briar) Threema does not use." For the §2.2 audience subset whose threat tier includes Swiss-jurisdiction legal process as a concern (organizers in Myanmar, journalists in Iran, activists in jurisdictions where Swiss MLA treaties operate), Threema's centralized posture is disqualifying. For the audience subset whose threat tier accepts Swiss centralization (much of European civil society, partner-organization staff in friendly-jurisdiction operations), Threema is the lower-cost solution that already exists.
- **What v1 loses if Cairn matches comparable simplicity:** This is the wrong framing — Cairn cannot become Threema without abandoning the §2.2 audience whose threat tier is Threema's disqualifying gap. The honest question is: what fraction of Cairn's §2.2 audience could be served by Threema today, and what is the marginal Cairn-audience-not-served-by-Threema for which v1 is being built?
- **Recommendation:** §2.3's Threema treatment is correct; the comparison-against-Threema framing should escalate into §2.2 itself. §2.2 should name the Cairn-audience-not-served-by-Threema explicitly — users whose threat tier includes legal process the Swiss jurisdiction does not block, users whose economic position cannot absorb Threema's paid-product model, users whose operational posture cannot accept closed-source clients. The current §2.3 treatment is structurally sound; the editorial improvement is to surface in §2.2 that a meaningful slice of Cairn's claimed audience could in fact be served by Threema, and that Cairn v1 is being built for the residual slice. This sharpens the funding case and the audience-serving case simultaneously. No architectural change.

### F5: Tails serves session-bounded operations without persistent identity; the "persistent identity is essential" claim is implicit rather than argued

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.1 commits to three-tier persistent identity: master Ed25519 keypair anchoring trust over the project's lifetime, operational keypair rotatable but persistent across sessions, device tokens short-lived but issued from persistent operational identity. §4.3 implicitly assumes persistent identity is the right answer for §2.2 audiences — the multi-tier model is justified against "single keypair on-device" and "HD derivation" alternatives, not against "no persistent identity at all."
- **Comparable product without it:** Tails. §2.3 acknowledges Tails is "the canonical amnesic-Linux distribution for journalist and activist use, with overlap into Cairn's audience" and characterizes Tails as serving "session-bounded operations (no persistent identity across sessions; no integrated messaging beyond what the user brings)." Tails serves an audience that explicitly chose intermittent identity over persistent identity as the right tradeoff for their threat tier.
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** §2.3 names Cairn as "the inverse of Tails: persistent cryptographic identity with structural protection of that identity across sessions, calibrated to ongoing-messaging audiences." For ongoing-messaging audiences who maintain stable contacts and need attestation continuity, persistent identity is required by the use case. For users whose work fits Tails's session-bounded posture (a journalist doing single-investigation work, an activist whose operational window is bounded), Tails's no-persistent-identity posture is the correct tradeoff and Cairn's persistent identity is overhead.
- **What v1 loses if Cairn matches comparable simplicity:** Matching Tails simplicity (no persistent identity) means abandoning ongoing-messaging audiences entirely, which is the §2.2 core use case. The structural question this finding raises is not whether v1 should abandon persistent identity but whether the brief honestly engages with the alternative.
- **Recommendation:** No architectural change. §2.3's Tails framing already correctly characterizes Cairn as the inverse-of-Tails for an audience whose use case requires it. The editorial improvement is in §4.3 or §5.1: the brief should add a single sentence acknowledging that the persistent-identity commitment itself is a choice against the Tails-style intermittent-identity alternative, with the justification (ongoing-messaging audiences) made explicit rather than implicit. This is a one-sentence addition, not a structural change. The risk this finding does flag: §2.2 should distinguish between audiences whose threat tier requires persistent identity (organizing across months, journalist-source relationships maintained over years) and audiences whose threat tier could be served by intermittent identity (single-investigation work, bounded-campaign organizing). The current §2.2 treatment elides this distinction; the Cairn-vs-Tails comparison makes it visible.

## Minor findings

### F6: SimpleX's identifier-less queues serve audiences without integrated identity above the protocol; v1's three-tier identity adds value where SimpleX leaves a gap

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.1's three-tier identity sits explicitly above SimpleX (§6.1 messaging substrate). §4.3 acknowledges this is original construction at the integration layer.
- **Comparable product without it:** SimpleX. §2.3: "no persistent user identity at the protocol level in the v1 single-device configuration; ... each conversation requires out-of-band connection establishment with no overlay structure; no trust-graph for attestation propagation; no integrated social-recovery flow."
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** SimpleX's identifier-less queues are the metadata-protection property §3.3 requires; the SimpleX team's roadmap aligns with what the audience needs at the protocol layer. What SimpleX does not provide is identity continuity across conversations or trust propagation across the social network — both required by §2.2's "ongoing-messaging" framing. Cairn's three-tier identity is what makes SimpleX queues operationally usable as a relationship substrate rather than a per-conversation transport.
- **Recommendation:** Keep the three-tier identity. §2.3 and §4.3 already correctly frame this as Cairn's integration value above SimpleX. No simplification available; the comparison is properly handled.

### F7: Signal's single signing identity and central service represent the posture Cairn's release-security stack explicitly does not adopt; the question is what audience needs the difference at v1

- **Cairn commitment:** §5.5 release-security stack with 5-of-N recruited reviewers, Sigsum, witness pool, multi-channel distribution. §4.3: "substitutes the developer's single signing identity with a distributed trust set."
- **Comparable product without it:** Signal. §2.3 acknowledges Signal Foundation operates a single foundation with centralized service and a single signing identity in the traditional sense (Signal does publish reproducible builds for Android per §4.3, and rides F-Droid where users choose).
- **What Cairn's added complexity delivers:** §4.3's framing is structurally honest — Cairn's release-security posture is what Signal explicitly does not commit to. The Signal Foundation's approach is "we are the trust placement and we operate it well"; Cairn's approach is "no single party is the trust placement." For the §2.2 audience whose threat tier includes adversaries who can compel a US-based foundation under legal process, Signal Foundation's posture is the disqualifying gap §2.3 names. For the audience whose threat tier accepts Signal's posture, Signal is the larger-adoption tool.
- **Recommendation:** F1's recommendation already addresses the release-security simplification path. The Signal comparison reinforces that Cairn's distinct-from-Signal release-security commitment is structurally meaningful for the §2.2 audience, while F1's recommendation lets v1 ship a simpler version of that distinct-from-Signal posture (developer Sigstore + Rekor + Sigsum + multi-channel + F-Droid as v1.5 second-attestation channel) without the recruited reviewer pool as v1 critical path.

## Patterns

**Pattern 1: Three of four §4.3 commitments survive comparable-product scrutiny for v1; one does not.** The three-tier identity (F6 vs. SimpleX), the social-recovery commitment (vs. Threema-style centralization, see F4), and the trust graph (F3 vs. Briar's introduction protocol) each deliver an audience-serving property the comparable products in their respective lanes cannot provide for the §2.2 audience. The fourth commitment — the recruited 5+ reviewer pool with 3-of-5 Sigsum-anchored attestation as v1 critical path — is duplicative of release-security postures GrapheneOS, F-Droid, and Briar each operate at lower cost for overlapping audiences (F1, F2, F7). The pattern: Cairn's identity-and-trust integration is its distinctive contribution; Cairn's release-security stack at v1 critical path is defensive engineering that comparable products already provide variants of.

**Pattern 2: The brief's comparison treatments in §2.3 are structurally honest; the §4.3 differentiation argument is where simplification pressure lives.** §2.3 correctly characterizes each comparator's audience-serving property and where it falls short for §2.2. The §4.3 framing of Cairn as a four-commitment integration that no comparator combines is true but obscures that v1 does not need all four commitments to ship — three are integration value, one is a release-security commitment that could ride v1.5 reproducible builds + F-Droid + GrapheneOS-precedent rather than recruiting from zero.

**Pattern 3: Audience-overlap is sharper than §4.3 acknowledges; the Cairn-audience-not-served-by-comparators is a meaningful slice but not the totality of §2.2.** F4 (Threema) and F5 (Tails) each name a real audience subset whose threat tier is served by the comparator at lower cost. §2.2 currently elides these subsets; the comparison framing this lens applies would tighten the §2.2 audience description by naming the residual audience explicitly (users whose threat tier is not served by Threema's Swiss centralization or by Tails's session-boundedness). This sharpens both the funding case and the audience-serving case without architectural change.

**Pattern 4: The "compound rather than add" framing in §4.3 is the structural defense against simplification critiques, and it holds for three of four commitments.** §4.3 argues that removing any of the four layers does not produce a degraded Cairn but a Cairn whose security properties do not hold against §3's threat tier. This is true for identity + trust graph + recovery (removing any one breaks the others — the trust graph depends on persistent identity, recovery depends on the master identity tier, identity rotation depends on trust-graph cascade). It is structurally weaker for the release-security commitment: the 5-of-N recruited reviewer pool is not a precondition for the identity/trust/recovery layers to function; it is a separate trust placement that exists in parallel. F1's recommendation severs this commitment from v1 critical path without breaking the compound-property argument for the other three.

## Summary (150 words)

Three of Cairn's four §4.3 architectural commitments survive comparable-product scrutiny for v1. The three-tier identity (vs. SimpleX's identifier-less queues, F6), social recovery (vs. Threema's centralization, F4), and the Sigsum-anchored trust graph (vs. Briar's introduction-protocol + BQP, F3) each deliver an audience-serving property the closest comparator cannot provide at §2.2's threat tier. The fourth commitment — recruiting a 5-of-N reviewer pool with Sigsum-anchored attestation as v1 critical path — is the weak link. F-Droid's rebuild pipeline (F1), GrapheneOS's multi-signer release process (F2), and Signal Android's reproducible builds + F-Droid posture (F7) demonstrate that overlapping-audience products ship credible release-security postures without recruiting a parallel pool. Recommend deferring the recruited pool to v1.5 alongside reproducible builds + F-Droid integration; v1 ships developer Sigstore + Rekor + Sigsum release log + multi-channel distribution. Editorial sharpening of §2.2 around Threema-served and Tails-served audience subsets would tighten the funding case.
