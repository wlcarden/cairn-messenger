# Civil-society partner review findings

**Review date:** 2026-05-28
**Brief version reviewed:** v0.7
**Time spent:** ~75 minutes
**Your operational context:** Five years running a digital-safety helpline at a civil-society organization in the Access Now / EFF Threat Lab / Tactical Tech mold. 2,000+ intake calls from journalists, organizers, defenders, dissidents, and front-line workers facing mercenary-spyware, forensic-extraction, and HUMINT threats. Dozens of walk-throughs of Pegasus IoC checks and post-seizure key rotation. Direct experience with Signal-in-newsrooms, Briar in protest contexts, SecureDrop integration into editorial workflows, and the deployment-vs-design gap that recurs in every secure-tool project this space has shipped.

## Summary

My organization could not honestly recommend Cairn to our network on the basis of this brief. The brief is internally consistent and unusually honest about what it does not serve, but its own compounding exclusions — D0014 (no peer network), §2.2 (no co-located adversary, no English non-fluent, no missing-Pixel-channel jurisdiction), §3.3 (no tool-use-as-pretext jurisdiction), §3.5 (no users who need messages within 48 hours), §6.3 (no users outside the developer's 1–2 local groups) — leave an addressable v1 population so small (Q17: "low hundreds globally," unvalidated) that the partner-mediation operational cost D0013 / D0015 / D0009 require may not be worth what an organization like mine would be asked to absorb.

The deployment plan depends entirely on partner-organization roles that have not been recruited, with the partner asks (mediated consent channel per D0013; permanent advisory authority per D0009; reviewer pool per D0015; facilitator network per §8.6) materially understated against what civil-society organizations our size can realistically commit. The brief's own §10.4 names "structural collapse into the D0009 sudden-unavailability contingency" as the most likely Phase D outcome — meaning a partner that vouches for Cairn to its network is vouching with a five-year horizon for a project whose maintainer expects to be unable to sustain operations on a five-year horizon. The brief should not be sent to partner organizations for outreach without addressing the partnership-realism and sustainability-fiction findings below; doing so risks the first-conversation "go back and rewrite this" rejection the maintainer has signaled they are trying to avoid.

There is real honesty work in the brief (D0014, D0013, §3.4 trust-root naming) that should not be retreated from. The problems below are not arguments to soften that honesty; they are arguments to extend it further and to redesign the deployment plan around what the honest analysis already says.

## Audience and threat-model calibration

**PROBLEM: The audience description (§1.2, §2.2) names populations the architecture does not serve, then acknowledges the exclusion later in the same paragraph.**

EVIDENCE: §1.2 names "journalists, lawyers, organizers, dual-use technology workers, civil-society researchers, dissidents, undocumented organizers, abuse survivors with severed networks, queer and religious minorities in criminalizing jurisdictions, and others" as the threat-tier audience. §2.2 repeats this list verbatim, then lists exclusions: co-located adversary, no peer network (D0014), English non-fluent, no GrapheneOS-channel access. The combination of named exclusions strikes most of the named populations: undocumented organizers and queer people in criminalizing jurisdictions overlap heavily with "no peer network" per D0014; abuse survivors are explicitly named in D0014; journalists in many target jurisdictions face tool-use-as-pretext per §3.3 (Iran, GCC, parts of SE Asia). §2.2's "low hundreds globally" estimate at the four-precondition intersection (Q17 acknowledges this is unvalidated) is the honest number; §1.2's threat-tier population list is the dishonest one.

IMPACT: A helpline operator reading §1.2 cannot tell which of their callers the tool serves. The named populations are the populations my casework actually involves; the four-precondition intersection is not. When a partner organization considers whether to facilitate Cairn pilots, the gap between marketing-language audience (§1.2) and deployable-population audience (Q17, §2.2 exclusions) is the first thing their program leadership will notice. The brief's own honesty work in §2.2 / D0014 makes the contradiction explicit, which is worse than leaving it implicit: a partner reads §1.2, then reads §2.2 and concludes the §1.2 framing is marketing copy, then declines to be associated.

RECOMMENDATION: Rewrite §1.2 to lead with the four-precondition intersection ("low hundreds globally, drawn from the threat tier below subject to the architectural commitments in §2.2") and demote the threat-tier population list to one sentence with the named exclusions next to it. §2.2's audience subsection must come first, not the threat-tier sentence. The brief should name two populations: who v1 actually serves (post-D0014-exclusion intersection); who v1 does not serve but the architecture is designed against (the broader threat tier). The latter cannot be marketed as the former.

**PROBLEM: The threat-model adversary list (§3.2) overweights technically-glamorous threats and underweights threats that dominate actual helpline casework.**

EVIDENCE: §3.2 names mercenary spyware (Pegasus, Predator), forensic-extraction operators (Cellebrite, MSAB, GrayKey), and traditional SIGINT-capable state actors. HUMINT is included but listed last; §3.3 HUMINT surface gets one paragraph noting it is "the dominant risk category at this threat tier" per Access Now Helpline and Front Line Defenders casework. The brief's own §3.3 acknowledges HUMINT dominance, but §3.2's adversary-landscape framing leads with the spyware vendors, which is the framing that gets cited in §1, §2.2, and §4.3 differentiation. Account takeover, SIM swap, lawful-intercept on signaling (the dominant compromise pattern in mid-tier jurisdictions), and device confiscation at non-border checkpoints (the dominant compromise pattern in the cases my organization sees) appear nowhere or only implicitly. The brief's §4.3 differentiation argument is structured against the spyware-and-extraction tier; my casework distribution is roughly 60% HUMINT or account-takeover, 25% lawful-intercept or telecom-mediated, 10% forensic-extraction, 5% mercenary-spyware.

IMPACT: A partner-organization helpline reading the brief would conclude Cairn is designed for the 5–15% of our cases where mercenary spyware is the proximate threat, not the 60% of cases where HUMINT or account takeover dominates. Cairn's identifier-less posture is genuinely useful for those 60%, but the brief does not lead with the threats those users actually face. The §4.3 "integration plus three architectural commitments" differentiation is sold against a threat profile that is not the one most helpline callers present.

RECOMMENDATION: Restructure §3.2 to lead with HUMINT and the account-and-identifier surface (the surfaces where Cairn's identifier-less posture provides material defense), and demote the mercenary-spyware paragraph to one of several capability tiers rather than the headline. Cite Access Now Helpline incident data and Front Line Defenders casework with case-distribution numbers if partners will share them. The "ability to defend against a Pegasus zero-click" framing is something my organization cannot honestly tell a caller Cairn provides — the §3.5 indefinitely-out-of-scope list correctly says "compromise of GrapheneOS itself, compromise of Pixel hardware" are out of scope, which is most of what a zero-click delivers. Don't market the threat the architecture cannot defend.

**PROBLEM: BYOD-with-GrapheneOS-on-Pixel (§10.1, §6.3) is operationally incompatible with most of the named threat-tier population.**

EVIDENCE: §10.1 commits the pilot to BYOD: pilot users source their own GrapheneOS-capable Pixel devices through standard commercial channels. The project retains a 2–4 device loaner pool. §2.2 acknowledges "users who do not have or cannot acquire GrapheneOS-capable Pixel hardware" are out of scope until v1.5+ hardware partnerships (per §10.5, "deferred indefinitely under current funding posture") or v2's USB form factor. A Pixel through standard channels in the US costs $499–999; in jurisdictions where Pixels are not officially sold (most of Africa, much of South and Southeast Asia, parts of Latin America, all of the GCC, Iran, Belarus, Russia), the import cost plus customs is $700–1,500. My typical caller from a target jurisdiction makes $200–800/month. The Pixel is not a second device for them; it is a half-year of income.

IMPACT: The BYOD posture quietly selects for users who (a) live in jurisdictions where Pixels are commercially available, (b) can afford to buy one, (c) can afford to use it as a second device alongside the phone they need for normal life, (d) can switch to GrapheneOS without losing the day-to-day apps their employer or family requires. This is the upper-quartile of the threat-tier population by income, dominantly Western or Western-diaspora, dominantly English-fluent. It is precisely the population §2.2 says the architecture is not specifically calibrated to. The brief's "Distribution and supply-chain surface" in §3.3 names this — but the 2–4 device loaner pool is acknowledged as insufficient for even pilot scale, and §10.5's hardware partnerships are "deferred indefinitely."

RECOMMENDATION: Either commit (with budget) to a meaningful loaner-pool scale that the actual pilot cohort can draw on, or rewrite §2.2 to name "Pixel-affording, GrapheneOS-tolerant, second-device-capable" as part of the v1 audience precondition. Q26 (CalyxOS inclusion) should be resolved before pilot recruitment, not after — the CalyxOS option might double the addressable population at v1.x, and partners cannot evaluate facilitation feasibility without knowing which device baseline the broader-release plan will require. The "iOS in v3" framing means iOS-using populations (the majority of journalists in much of Latin America, the GCC, and the Mediterranean) are out of scope for years, not months. Name this explicitly in §1.2 as a v1-audience exclusion.

**PROBLEM: §3.1's "facilitators understand the threat model" framing is structurally correct but contradicts the §2.2 audience naming.**

EVIDENCE: §3.1 explicitly states: "This threat model is written for the people who deploy and support the product on behalf of users — pilot administrators, NGO security trainers, journalists' digital-security staff — and not, in most cases, for the end users themselves." This is correct deployment-history practice (Signal-in-newsrooms works this way; SecureDrop is built around it). But §1.2 and §2.2 name individuals — journalists, lawyers, organizers — as the audience, as if those individuals will read the brief and adopt the product. They will not. The actual user-facing layer is partner-organization staff: the digital-safety officer at the news org, the security trainer at the human-rights NGO, the helpline operator. The brief does not acknowledge that those staff are the actual user.

IMPACT: The brief is written as if its audience model is "individuals at the threat tier." Its actual deployment model is "individuals at the threat tier, mediated by partner-organization staff who understand the architecture." This is fine — it is how protective-tech deployment works at this threat tier — but the brief does not say so. As a result: the audience-section asks (Q17 population estimate, Q4 pilot identification) are framed against the wrong population. The correct question is not "how many individuals at the threat tier are GrapheneOS-Pixel-capable" but "how many partner-organization staff are willing to mediate Cairn deployment to their users." That is a much smaller number, and it is the number the brief actually depends on.

RECOMMENDATION: Restructure §2.2 around three-tier audience naming: (1) partner-organization staff who mediate deployment; (2) end users those staff deploy to; (3) the broader threat-tier population the architecture is designed against but does not directly reach. Each tier has different evaluation criteria and different recruitment pathways. The brief currently conflates them.

## Deployment-reality gaps

**PROBLEM: D0013's partner-mediated consent channel is a precondition for pilot enrollment, but the partner has not been recruited, the protocol has not been written, and the partner-side workload has not been estimated.**

EVIDENCE: D0013:30 commits the brief to "negotiating partner-organization mediation as a pilot precondition: a partner organization (candidate organizations per §8.6: Front Line Defenders, Tactical Tech, Access Now Digital Security Helpline) operates a contact point pilot users can use for any of: reporting negative outcomes; refusing a pilot element; requesting exit; reporting tool-mediated harm." D0013:40: "Pilot enrollment cannot begin until the partner-mediation arrangement is in place." D0013 then defers framework selection to Q22, partner identity to Q5, and modifications to "partner-organization conversations" that have not happened. No estimate is provided of the partner-side staff hours required to operate the channel for the pilot's six-month duration; no escalation path is specified if a tool-mediated harm report comes in at 02:00 on a weekend; no liability framing is specified for the partner organization if a pilot user's compromise can be partly attributed to Cairn use.

IMPACT: My organization's intake-and-escalation infrastructure runs at about 4–6 full-time-equivalent across a global helpline serving thousands of cases per year. Operating a dedicated mediation channel for a 10–15-user pilot is conservatively 2–4 staff hours per week (intake, triage, weekly check-ins, monthly reporting to project, quarterly partner debrief, plus on-call escalation) — call it 50–100 hours over the six-month pilot. This is not a small ask for a 10–15-user pilot. The partner's program leadership will weigh that cost against (a) the population the pilot reaches (10–15 users in 1–2 local groups not in our network), (b) the post-pilot trajectory (broader release deferred to v1.5 which is gated on Phase C funding which is not secured), (c) the reputational risk of being publicly associated with an unaudited-at-broader-release tool. Without those three answered, my organization would decline.

The harder problem: D0013 is currently written as if the protocol is project-co-developed-with-partner. It is not. The protocol is project-drafted with the assumption that some partner will agree to the role as drafted. Front Line Defenders' or Tactical Tech's protection-work guidance would substantially modify what D0013 currently specifies. The deferral to Q22 "framework selection" makes this worse, not better — it pushes a substantive protocol-co-development conversation into a Q-tracker entry as if it were a small open item.

RECOMMENDATION: Do not approach partner organizations with D0013 in its current form. First, commission ~2–4 weeks of unpaid protocol-drafting consultation with one or two partner-organization protection-program staff (Front Line Defenders' protection desk; Tactical Tech's training program; Access Now Helpline's case-management lead) to co-draft what the consent and exit protocol actually contains. Second, present the co-drafted protocol to other candidate partners as the basis for facilitation discussions, not as a project-drafted document for approval. Third, the partner-side workload and the escalation path must be specified in the protocol — partner organizations evaluate asks by total cost, not by drafted-policy elegance.

The brief's current sequence is "draft protocol → recruit partner → run pilot." The right sequence is "recruit two protection-program consultants → co-draft protocol → recruit pilot-facilitation partner → recruit consent-mediation partner → run pilot." This adds 2–3 months to pre-pilot work but converts the first partner conversation from "review what we drafted" to "agree to a role we drafted with people like you."

**PROBLEM: §6.3 and D0013 do not specify what happens to a partner-mediated exit at 02:00 on a weekend.**

EVIDENCE: D0013:30 lists four categories of partner-mediated reports: negative outcomes, refusing a pilot element, requesting exit, tool-mediated harm. D0013:32 commits to "honor exit without follow-up pressure from the developer." Neither D0013 nor §6.3 names what the partner does between the time a pilot user reports tool-mediated harm and the time the developer can be reached. If the user reports a key-compromise scenario, the operational response (revoke operational identity per §5.1; initiate fresh-identity path per §5.3) requires either the user's own action (which the user may not be able to take if they are in active crisis) or the developer's intervention (which the partner has explicitly been set up to bypass).

IMPACT: At my organization, every case has an on-call escalation path. The most common request from a caller in crisis is "I think my phone is compromised, what do I do right now." The partner who has agreed to operate D0013's mediation channel will receive that call. They will not have the tools, the access, or the authority to walk a pilot user through a revoke-and-recover under crisis. Either the partner ends up routing back to the developer (which D0013 explicitly disallows), or the partner ends up absorbing a kind of casework they were not budgeted for. Either way, the partner organization's reputation is on the line for an outcome the brief did not give them tools to control.

RECOMMENDATION: D0013 must specify the partner-side escalation path for crisis cases that the partner cannot resolve without developer involvement. The "honor exit without follow-up pressure" commitment is correct in principle but cannot be operationalized without an exception for "user is in active crisis and needs the project's incident-response capability." Specify what that exception looks like, who decides when it applies, and how the partner documents that they invoked it without violating the consent-protocol's structural commitment.

**PROBLEM: The pilot facilitator is the developer in §6.3; this concentrates trust, HUMINT exposure, and travel-pattern visibility in one person across all pilot users.**

EVIDENCE: §6.3: "Conducted in person by the developer for each pilot user." §3.4 names "the in-person facilitator (v1 pilot model)" as a trust root: "A compromised or coerced facilitator gains visibility into multiple users' identity material at the moment of provisioning and influences the structure of their early trust graph." §6.3 names pilot scope as "10-15 users in one or two local groups." The "one or two local groups" framing means at most two cities. Pilot users in different cities require the developer to travel to them. The developer's travel pattern is itself observable to the same adversaries §3 describes (border records, hotel records, telecom geolocation).

IMPACT: For pilot users in jurisdictions where the threat tier in §3 is actually present (this would justify the architecture's cost), the developer's arrival becomes a forensic-triage event for adversaries. For pilot users in the developer's own jurisdiction (which is the practical implication of "local groups"), the geographic concentration biases the pilot toward whatever city the developer lives in, which is unlikely to be the threat-tier-deployment context the brief actually describes. Either way, the developer's pilot-facilitation role compounds HUMINT exposure (per §3.3 HUMINT surface): an adversary who recruits or compromises the developer compromises the provisioning ceremony for every pilot user.

RECOMMENDATION: Specify the developer's pilot-facilitation security posture explicitly. What does the developer do to reduce the observability of pilot-user provisioning ceremonies (counter-surveillance practice, ceremony location selection, ceremony scheduling)? What is the response plan if the developer becomes a HUMINT target during the pilot? If the answer is "the developer's security posture is whatever the developer's security posture is," then the pilot is unsuitable for users at the §3 threat tier — only for lower-threat users for whom the developer's casual security posture is acceptable. Name this. The most honest framing is that v1 pilot users are at the threat tier the developer's own security posture can survive in, not at the threat tier §3 describes.

**PROBLEM: The 48-hour cooling-off window in D0005 / §5.3 (and the 48–96 hour total recovery time) is incompatible with the operational tempo of users in active crisis.**

EVIDENCE: §5.3: "From recovery initiation to operational identity available: typically 48–96 hours. Peer response time is the variable component — peers verifying through their chosen out-of-band channel and starting the cooling-off window may take hours to a day; the 48-hour delay is then mandatory." D0005 establishes the 48-hour cooling-off as the principal defense against the unlock-enumerate-impersonate attack chain. §5.3 also lists the "fresh-identity path" as the alternative when recovery is not viable, with the consequence "the user is, cryptographically, a new participant whose first attestation is the re-introducing contact's signature" — no message history, no historical attestations.

IMPACT: In my casework, the most common post-seizure request is "I need to reach my contacts now to tell them my device is compromised and they should stop using the old channel." 48 hours is the minimum window. 96 hours is the realistic window. During that window, the user has no operational identity, no signed traffic, no way to alert their contacts through Cairn. They will use Signal, WhatsApp, or whatever channel works — which is exactly what the §3 threat model says they shouldn't do. The "out-of-band alerting to known contacts that the prior identity is compromised is the user's responsibility during this window" line in §5.3 means the user is using out-of-band channels (which are the channels the architecture was supposed to replace) during the crisis window the architecture was supposed to address. This is the friction-vs-survival gap and the brief acknowledges it only implicitly.

A second pattern: in mass-arrest, evacuation, or infrastructure-shutdown scenarios (Hong Kong 2019–2020; Iran 2022–2023; Russia 2022; Gaza 2023–2025; many others), users lose multiple peer connections simultaneously. D0014 names the architecture's response to peer compromise as "a model that requires the existence of a non-compromised peer network — exactly what these populations lack." The brief is honest about excluding these populations from v1 recovery; but in deployment the brief does not control which crisis its users will face. A pilot user whose recovery network is intact at provisioning but compromised by the time recovery is needed (the mass-arrest case) is structurally indistinguishable, at the moment of recovery, from a user D0014 excludes.

RECOMMENDATION: Name the friction-vs-survival tradeoff explicitly in §1.2 and §2.2 as a user-borne cost, not architecturally hidden. Specify what a partner organization tells a user who needs their messages in less than 48 hours: that they must use Signal, that Cairn is not appropriate for crisis-window communication, that recovery is a multi-day ceremony not an emergency procedure. Until this is named in user-facing language, partners cannot deploy Cairn without setting expectations that the architecture cannot meet.

**PROBLEM: The "in-app support channel" in §6.3 is the only documented support path; there is no helpline-equivalent layer for the day-to-day "I don't understand what happened" volume.**

EVIDENCE: §6.3: "A dedicated in-app support channel (cross-ref Section 5.7 acknowledgments on crash reporting and feedback) gives pilot users a direct channel to report operational issues." That, plus 3-month and 6-month partner debriefs, is the entire support infrastructure described. §8.6 lists "user training and onboarding" as a partner role category: "Front Line Defenders, Access Now Digital Security Helpline, regional civil-society security organizations." But that role is not committed to and not staffed.

IMPACT: For the audience the brief names (§3 threat tier), the support volume is not "crash reports and feedback." It is "I think I just leaked something, what do I do," "my peer didn't respond to the recovery challenge, can you help me find out why," "I lost my phone, my pre-shared challenges are on a sticky note in the phone, what now," "I think my colleague's device was confiscated at the border last week and I need to know if my recovery share with them is still safe." This is helpline work. It is sustained, high-skill, multi-language work. At pilot scale (10–15 users) the developer can absorb it. Post-pilot, no plan exists. The brief implicitly externalizes this load onto partner organizations (Access Now Helpline, Front Line Defenders) without naming the cost or committing to fund it. My organization would absorb roughly 2–8 hours per active Cairn user per year in steady-state support load if we integrated it into our intake. The brief does not budget for this anywhere.

RECOMMENDATION: §10.4 Phase D must add a line item for partner-organization helpline support load, even if the project does not directly fund it. At minimum, name it as an externalized cost partner organizations would absorb. Better: budget a partner-organization support honorarium as a Phase C / Phase D line item with the explicit framing that helpline integration is a multi-year operational commitment, not a one-time partnership. Until this is named, partner organizations evaluating facilitation are reading the brief as "the project does not see the operational cost it is asking us to absorb," and that reading is correct.

## Adoption-friction analysis

**PROBLEM: The Signal-equivalent moment that breaks adoption in my deployment experience is the provisioning ceremony itself.**

EVIDENCE: §6.3 commits to an in-person provisioning ceremony per user covering "identity generation, recovery peer designation, pre-shared challenge establishment with each peer, initial trust-graph seeding, and walkthrough of the post-coercion recovery guidance." §5.3 commits to peer-designation as a meaningful per-peer conversation involving "geographic distribution, social distance, demonstrated ability to refuse coercion" guidance. §5.6 (not read in detail) and the §6.3 ceremony together describe an onboarding that is operationally equivalent to a 90–180 minute session per user.

Signal onboarding: download app, enter phone number, verify SMS code, done. ~5 minutes. Briar onboarding: install app, generate identity, accept contact requests as they arrive. ~10 minutes plus per-contact verification. SecureDrop onboarding (closest analogue to Cairn's deployment-mediation pattern): mediated by FPF support with paid integration, 1–3 days of newsroom-side setup, paid commitment from the news org. Cairn's onboarding is closer to SecureDrop than to Signal — but without FPF's institutional infrastructure or paid-integration model.

IMPACT: Users abandon at the provisioning conversation. Specifically, at the recovery-peer-designation step. The brief assumes users have 5 contacts who satisfy the §5.3 selection guidance (geographic distribution, social distance, ability to refuse coercion). In my casework, most callers in the threat tier do not. Their network is clustered geographically (same city, same building, same prison), their social distance is small (everyone knows everyone — that's how organizing works), and "ability to refuse coercion" is a question the user cannot answer about their own peers without putting those peers at risk. The provisioning conversation will surface this gap, and at that point the user has spent 30 minutes of onboarding to discover that the architecture is not designed for them. The facilitator either fudges the selection guidance (which compromises the security property the architecture promises) or sends the user away (which terminates onboarding).

RECOMMENDATION: Run a 5–10 user pre-pilot onboarding exercise specifically to surface where the provisioning ceremony fails. Do this before the partner-mediated pilot consent protocol is locked in, because the protocol's content depends on what the ceremony actually requires. The pre-pilot exercise can be with developer-known users at lower-than-target threat tier; the point is to test the ceremony's structure, not to test the architecture under threat. The brief currently treats the ceremony as a solved design problem; it is not.

**PROBLEM: The brief does not account for users running Cairn alongside Signal/WhatsApp/iMessage because they cannot migrate their network.**

EVIDENCE: §2.2 names the audience as "users whose adversaries treat their communications as worth substantial resources to compromise." The architecture is a complete alternative to consumer messengers. But the brief does not specify what happens when a Cairn user's contacts are on Signal or WhatsApp and will not migrate. The §3 threat model assumes Cairn carries the user's high-sensitivity traffic. It does not address what happens to the rest of the user's traffic.

IMPACT: In every deployment I have seen, the user runs the secure tool alongside the consumer tool. Signal-in-newsrooms users still have WhatsApp for family. Briar users in protest contexts still use Signal for casual organizing. Cairn users will have Cairn for sensitive traffic and WhatsApp for everything else. The threat-model gap from this is: an adversary who cannot read Cairn traffic reads WhatsApp traffic. An adversary who cannot compel Cairn metadata compels WhatsApp metadata. The §3 trust roots (GrapheneOS, Pixel hardware, Tor) apply only to Cairn traffic; the user's daily traffic mostly evades them. This is fine — it is how secure-tool deployment actually works — but the brief does not name it, and the partner organization that recommends Cairn alongside Signal has to manage the concurrent-use threat model the brief does not address.

RECOMMENDATION: Add a §3.6 (or extend §3.5) explicitly addressing the concurrent-use case. What threats does Cairn defend against when the user also uses Signal/WhatsApp/iMessage for non-Cairn traffic? What is a partner organization supposed to tell users about which conversations move to Cairn and which stay on consumer tools? The "all or nothing" framing implicit in §3 is not what deployment looks like.

**PROBLEM: The reviewer-pool deferral to v1.5 (D0015) leaves v1 pilot users on a tool whose application-supply-chain integrity rests on a single developer.**

EVIDENCE: D0015 defers the recruited 5+/3-of-5 reviewer pool from v1 critical path to v1.5. D0015's argument: the v1 supply-chain gap (a compromised build pipeline producing a malicious binary from clean source not detected by developer source review alone) is named in §5.5 and closed at v1.5. v1 release-security stack: developer-signed APK + Sigstore + Rekor + Sigsum-anchored release log + multi-channel distribution. Pilot users in the v1 cohort are at the §3 threat tier; they specifically benefit from multi-party verification. D0015 itself acknowledges this: "Pilot users are at the §3 threat tier. They specifically benefit from multi-party verification of source; concentrating release attestation in the developer at v1 leaves the developer's single-signing-identity surface uncovered until v1.5." D0015 rejects this argument anyway, on the basis that v1 pilot is 10–15 users and the multi-party verification's primary value lands at broader release.

IMPACT: D0013's pilot consent protocol asks the partner organization to mediate consent and exit for users who are running a tool with an acknowledged supply-chain gap. When my organization recommends a tool to a network, the recommendation is implicitly "we vouch for the tool's integrity." For v1 Cairn, we cannot — D0015 explicitly says we cannot, until v1.5 ships. The brief asks partners to facilitate pilot deployment against a population for which the v1 stack is named-as-insufficient. The defensible v1 pilot population is "users at lower than §3 threat tier where the developer's source-review attestation is sufficient." That is not the population §6.3 commits the pilot to.

RECOMMENDATION: Either (a) re-scope v1 pilot to a lower-threat-tier population for which D0015's v1 stack is acknowledged-sufficient (which means rewriting §6.3 and §1.2), or (b) ship a v1 reviewer-pool at smaller scale (the rejected Option B in D0015) so that v1 pilot users have multi-party attestation. The current decision works only if the partner-mediated consent protocol explicitly names the v1 supply-chain gap and asks pilot users for consent against that gap. D0013 must include this disclosure; currently it does not.

## Partnership-realism analysis

**PROBLEM: The brief asks partner organizations for permanent, multi-year operational roles before it has held any partner-organization conversations.**

EVIDENCE: Q5 ("NGO partner outreach: roles and sequencing"): "Outreach not begun." Q4 ("Pilot user community identification"): "Developer has groups in mind; not documented or committed." §8.6: "The candidate organizations listed above are organizations whose mission and capacity match the role category; assignment to a specific role at a specific organization happens through Q5 outreach, not through the design brief." D0013 partner-mediated consent: candidate organizations named, not recruited. D0009 partner advisory authority (permanent governance scaffold under D0016 deferral per §8.4): candidate organizations named, partner unselected (Q14). D0015 reviewer pool (v1.5 commitment): Q5 outreach drives recruitment. §10.4 unbudgeted developer time of 40–80 hours/year on "partner-organization coordination" — at steady state, this is one full-time-equivalent week per year just on coordination, not on the work being coordinated. The brief structures partner relationships as substrate of the project's commitments; partners have not been asked whether they want the role.

IMPACT: In my experience evaluating asks from secure-tool projects, the first partner conversation is the diagnostic moment. If a project arrives with a design brief that names my organization in five places — pilot mediation, advisory authority, reviewer pool, helpline support, witness pool — and has not previously contacted us, the program-leadership response is usually "this is presumptuous, please come back when you have engaged us on what we'd actually contribute." This is the "go back and rewrite this" response the maintainer has signaled they are trying to avoid. The brief currently invites it.

The deeper problem is that the brief's sequence — design first, recruit partners second, run pilot third — is backwards for protective-tech work at this threat tier. ## What works well

Listing only items whose specific prevented-failure-mode I can articulate against deployment-history precedent.

**D0014's explicit naming of populations the v1 architecture does not serve.** This prevents the failure mode where (Telegram's "Secret Chats" branding precedent; CounterMail precedent; multiple lesser-known secure-tool examples) users in marginal positions adopt a tool built for a different population and discover the mismatch only at compromise. The protective-tech deployment literature is full of cases where projects claim "human rights defenders" as audience and serve "Western privacy enthusiasts" in practice. D0014's explicit, named exclusion list — sex workers under criminalization, abuse survivors with severed networks, isolated dissidents, undocumented organizers, queer people in criminalizing jurisdictions, religious minorities under family surveillance, prisoners' families — is the only example I can recall in this space of a project naming the populations it does not serve with that specificity. This honesty must not be retreated from under grant-reviewer pressure for broader "addressable market" language. The prevented failure mode is the partner organization deploying the tool to a population it cannot serve and absorbing the consequences when the population discovers it. Names like Tactical Tech, Front Line Defenders, and Access Now will sign onto a tool that names the populations it doesn't serve more readily than a tool that pretends it serves everyone.

**D0013's explicit disclosure of the developer-as-recruiter-facilitator-social-contact triple role.** This prevents the failure mode where (Open Whisper Systems' early pilot precedent; multiple academic protective-tech pilots that violated their own IRB guidance after the fact) pilot feedback is filtered through social cost and reported as validation. Front Line Defenders' own protection-work guidance specifically flags this conflict; my organization's own pilot-program guidance also flags it. D0013's recognition of the structural power asymmetry in pilot recruitment is necessary precondition for a defensible pilot. It is not sufficient (the partner-mediation channel still has to exist), but it is the necessary precondition that most projects skip.

**§3.4's explicit naming of trust roots including the OIDC provider jurisdiction, in-person facilitator, witness pool concentration, and partner advisory authority.** This prevents the failure mode where (Signal Foundation's relationship with US legal process, which was implicit for years and surfaced as surprise to deployment partners during the 2016 grand jury case; ProtonMail's metadata-handling surprise to partners during the 2021 Swiss court order) the trust placement is implicit in the design and emerges only when something fails. Naming the trust roots up front, including the uncomfortable ones (US OIDC jurisdiction in v1; facilitator-as-trust-root; witness-pool concentration), gives a partner organization the information needed to make an informed decision about whether to be associated. The honest framing of §3.4 is the substrate any partner conversation should start from.

**D0009's dead-man's-switch architecture as a mechanism, separately from the partner-recruitment gap.** This prevents the failure mode where (Cryptocat 2013–2016 precedent; multiple smaller secure-tool projects) the project disappears silently and pilot users continue using a tool whose maintainer is gone, the signing infrastructure is unattended, and incident response is dead. The monthly check-in + 60-day trigger + pre-staged successor documentation is the right architecture for this risk; the partner-recruitment problem is separate and should not be conflated with the architecture's correctness. This must be retained even if the rest of the foundation-incorporation framing is restructured.
