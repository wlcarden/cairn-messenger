# §10 — Audit Budget Defensibility Lens

## Summary

The §10 audit budget numbers are mostly anchored to defensible references, but several specific figures and assumptions would not survive scrutiny from a partner who has actually engaged Trail of Bits, NCC Group, Cure53, or Quarkslab. The most material problems are (1) the pre-pilot subsidized lower bound ($15K) and the 1-2 person-week scope estimate are inconsistent with each other given Cure53/OTF effective day rates as commonly summarized in public engagement reports, (2) the unsubsidized pre-beta upper bound ($150K) is light for a true 4-6 week engagement at Trail of Bits or NCC Group rates when scope expands to Sigstore/Sigsum/UnifiedPush integration as §8.5 names, and (3) subsidy-program assumptions are presented with a confidence that the underlying program criteria, acceptance rates, and cycle frequencies do not justify — particularly OTF Secure Audit and Mozilla OSAA.

The reviewer-honoraria, foundation-incorporation, and rolling-consulting figures are within defensible ranges but contain internal arithmetic gaps and a jurisdictional assumption (UK CIC at lower end) that does not align with how the named candidates actually price. The cumulative effect is that the Phase B floor and Phase C floor figures understate the realistic minimum capital required to execute the §8.5/D0011 commitments, which is precisely the figure that funders evaluating "smallest grant that materially advances the project" will check first.

## Critical findings

### F1: Pre-pilot $15K subsidized lower bound is inconsistent with the 1-2 person-week scope estimate at every named subsidy program's effective rate

- **Evidence:** §10.2 line 1063 states "$15-30K at subsidy-tier rates (Open Tech Fund Secure Audit program, Cure53 mission-org rates)"; §8.5 line 808 and D0011 line 27 state "Realistically 1-2 person-weeks of senior auditor time." D0011 line 10 itself acknowledges "industry rates at the named candidate firms — Trail of Bits, NCC Group, Cure53, Quarkslab — are $15-40K per week of engagement." Cure53's published mission-rate discount in public engagement reports (e.g., the Mullvad, Mailvelope, SecureDrop summaries) typically lands around 30-50% off commercial — bringing one week to roughly $10-20K and two weeks to $20-40K. OTF Secure Audit historical award sizes for similar primitive-only scopes have clustered in the $20-40K range, not $15K. A $15K floor implies either ~0.75 person-week of senior auditor time (insufficient for the four scope items D0011 names) or a subsidy depth that no public Cure53 or OTF engagement summary corroborates.
- **Impact:** The Phase B floor of $17K (line 1068) is the figure funders will cite as the "smallest grant that materially advances the project." If the realistic subsidized floor for the stated 1-2 person-week scope is closer to $22-25K, the Phase B floor is understated by 30-50% and the "single subsidy-program close at the lower end of its typical range covers Phase B" claim at line 1071 collapses for any program except possibly OTF at its larger award tier.
- **Recommendation:** Either tighten the lower bound to $20-25K and re-derive the Phase B floor, or narrow the scope at the lower bound to a single primitive (e.g., COSE_Sign1 envelope only, deferring SSS and trust-graph envelope to pre-beta) and document that scope reduction explicitly. The current presentation reads as "1-2 weeks at $15K" which no named subsidy program delivers.

### F2: Pre-beta $150K unsubsidized upper bound is light for the stated scope at Trail of Bits or NCC Group market rates

- **Evidence:** §8.5 line 809 lists the pre-beta scope as "full cryptographic-primitive review (re-confirming against v1.5 changes); full trust-graph operation handling; full recovery flow; capability-token construction; release-security stack including Sigstore and Sigsum integrations; UnifiedPush implementation if push is enabled in v1.5." D0011 line 10 states $15-40K per week at named firms; line 38 states "4-6 week engagement" for the upper bound. Six weeks at Trail of Bits' upper rate ($40K/week) is $240K, and the stated scope (six distinct review areas, three of which touch external supply-chain integrations) is realistically 6-8 person-weeks rather than 4-6 when broken into the actual review threads. Public Trail of Bits engagement summaries for comparable scope (e.g., zcash, libsignal-related work) typically land in the $200-400K range; NCC Group cryptography practice engagement letters in the same band. Cure53 at extended mission rates is the only firm where $150K plausibly closes a 6-week engagement of this scope.
- **Impact:** The Phase C ceiling of $335K (line 1094) understates realistic exposure if the project lands at Trail of Bits or NCC Group rather than Cure53. A funder reading the "we will engage one of [Trail of Bits, NCC Group, Cure53, Quarkslab]" framing in §8.5/D0011 will note that the upper-bound budget is sized only for the Cure53 case.
- **Recommendation:** Either (a) raise the unsubsidized upper bound to $180-220K to reflect Trail of Bits/NCC Group market rates honestly, (b) explicitly state that the $150K ceiling assumes Cure53 or Quarkslab as the firm and that engaging Trail of Bits or NCC Group requires additional funding, or (c) narrow the stated scope to drop Sigstore/Sigsum integration review (which has upstream audit history per the §8.5 line "explicitly does not duplicate upstream-project audits") and document the scope reduction.

### F3: Subsidy-program acceptance assumptions are stated as plans without acknowledging program-specific eligibility gaps

- **Evidence:** §10.5 lines 1124-1128 name OTF Secure Audit, Cure53 mission-org rates, Mozilla OSAA, NLnet NGI Zero Trust. The §10.6 strategy at line 1147 says "the project applies to multiple programs in parallel." But:
  - **OTF Secure Audit** historically requires the applicant to be a fiscal-sponsored or incorporated entity (per OTF's published program criteria); Phase A operates as a natural person, and §10.2 lists fiscal-sponsor setup as a Phase B item. There is a chicken-and-egg dependency: the pre-pilot audit subsidy needs fiscal sponsorship, but the fiscal sponsorship arrangement is itself a Phase B item. §10 does not call this out.
  - **Mozilla OSAA** has been on irregular cycles since 2021 (the program's website lists no fixed cadence); naming it as a route without acknowledging that the program may not run during Cairn's Phase B window is optimistic. As of the brief's date the most recent Mozilla open-source security funding has been MOSS-routed rather than OSAA-named.
  - **Cure53 mission-org rates** are not a grant program — they are a discount Cure53 applies at their discretion to engagements they accept. There is no application cycle; the project still needs the engagement budget on its own side, the subsidy just changes the size of the engagement budget required. Naming Cure53 as a "subsidy program" alongside OTF and NLnet, which are grant-makers, conflates two different mechanisms.
  - **NLnet NGI Zero Trust** has audit allocations only when an audit is part of a development grant the project has already received; NLnet does not typically fund standalone audits. The brief frames it as audit-specific subsidy in §10.5 and D0011 line 49, which is not how NLnet operates.
- **Impact:** Funders or partners familiar with these programs will note that the brief lists four "subsidy programs" of which one (Cure53) is a discount mechanism not a program, one (NLnet) does not fund standalone audits, one (Mozilla OSAA) may not have an active cycle, and one (OTF Secure Audit) has an entity-eligibility prerequisite the project's own Phase B sequencing creates a dependency for. The "apply to multiple in parallel" strategy is weaker than presented.
- **Recommendation:** Distinguish in §10.5 between (a) grant programs with cycles (OTF Secure Audit, Mozilla OSAA when active, NLnet when an integrated dev grant is in play) and (b) vendor discount mechanisms (Cure53 mission rates, Trail of Bits civic-tech rates). Acknowledge the OTF entity-eligibility prerequisite and how it interacts with the Phase B fiscal-sponsor sequencing. Note Mozilla OSAA cycle uncertainty rather than naming it as an assumed route.

## Significant findings

### F4: Reviewer honoraria arithmetic does not match the stated parameters

- **Evidence:** §10.3 line 1086 states "$2,000-5,000 per reviewer per release at 8-20 hours of review per release, multiplied across the five-reviewer pool and the release cadence the funded operational model supports (quarterly target per D0008)." Doing the arithmetic: 5 reviewers × 4 releases × $2,000 = $40,000 (floor) and 5 × 4 × $5,000 = $100,000 (ceiling). That matches the stated $40,000-100,000 range. But the per-hour implied rate at the floor is $2,000 / 20 hours = $100/hour, and at the ceiling is $5,000 / 8 hours = $625/hour. The brief acknowledges senior security-engineering market rates as $150-300/hour. The $100/hour floor is below the stated market range; the $625/hour ceiling is above the stated range. The honoraria are therefore "fair relative to security-engineering market rates" only in the mid-band of the stated range, not at the floor or ceiling.
- **Impact:** A reviewer evaluating the honoraria offer at the low end will see an effective rate below the §8.2 "fair-relative-to-market" commitment. A funder reviewing the ceiling will see the project paying $625/hour which is above the stated market reference.
- **Recommendation:** Tighten the honoraria range so the implied per-hour rate stays within the stated $150-300/hour market band. For 8-20 hours per release at $150-300/hour, the realistic per-reviewer-per-release figure is $1,200-6,000; the $2,000-5,000 range used in the brief is defensible mid-band but the arithmetic should be shown rather than left for funders to do.

### F5: Foundation incorporation lower bound at $5K assumes UK CIC is genuinely cheapest, which has not been validated against current Companies House and counsel fees

- **Evidence:** §10.3 line 1087 states "$5,000-25,000 initial depending on jurisdiction (US 501(c)(3) at the higher end, UK CIC at the lower end, Dutch Stichting and Swiss Verein in the middle)." Companies House CIC registration fee is approximately £35; the actual cost driver is counsel fees for the CIC36 form, asset lock provisions, board governance documents, and the IP-assignment transfer mechanics §10 itself names. UK non-profit counsel rates for a clean CIC incorporation with these elements have historically been £4,000-8,000 ($5,000-10,000 USD), but the brief at line 1087 includes "IP-assignment transfer mechanics" which for a natural-person-to-entity transfer typically adds 5-10 hours of IP-specialist time on top, pushing realistic UK CIC total to $8-15K rather than $5K. Dutch Stichting incorporation is typically cheaper than UK CIC by raw fees (notary plus chamber of commerce) but counsel for the asset-lock-equivalent structure narrows the gap.
- **Impact:** The $5K floor is plausibly achievable only for a UK CIC where the developer absorbs the IP-assignment counsel cost personally or uses a low-cost legal aid scheme. The Phase C floor of $105K (line 1093) assumes this lower bound.
- **Recommendation:** Either raise the incorporation lower bound to $8-10K to reflect realistic counsel fees including IP-assignment mechanics, or document explicitly which sub-items the $5K figure does and does not include and what gets absorbed personally.

### F6: Rolling cryptographic consulting at $5-10K/month implies a rate band that should be stated

- **Evidence:** §10.2 line 1073 states "$5-10K/month for 3-6 months of design-and-implementation phase ($15,000-60,000 total)" for part-time rolling cryptographic consulting. Independent senior cryptographic consultants (the realistic candidate pool for the named scope — design review of capability tokens, Shamir, trust-graph CRDT semantics) typically charge $250-400/hour. $5K/month at $300/hour is 16-17 hours/month or roughly 4 hours/week; $10K/month is 8-9 hours/week. The brief does not state the implied engagement intensity, which is the figure a consulting candidate evaluates when deciding whether to accept the engagement at the stated rate.
- **Impact:** Without the implied intensity stated, a consulting candidate may either decline (interpreting $5K/month as below their minimum engagement) or accept and underdeliver (interpreting it as half-time rather than quarter-time). The brief leaves the project exposed on the consulting-recruitment side.
- **Recommendation:** State the implied engagement intensity (e.g., "4-8 hours/week at $250-400/hour") so the consulting offer is unambiguous to the candidate pool.

## Minor findings

### F7: Recurring audit at $40-100K/year amortization assumes 18-24 month cadence holds, but cryptographic-relevant changes accumulate non-linearly

- **Evidence:** §10.4 line 1106 amortizes a recurring audit to "$40,000-100,000 per year depending on the rate of cryptographic-relevant changes." The $40K/year floor implies a $60-80K audit every 18-24 months at the subsidized rate, which is the lower end of D0011's $60-150K pre-beta range. This assumes the project sustains Cure53 mission rates or OTF Secure Audit awards repeatedly. Subsidy programs typically do not fund the same project's recurring audits at the same rate indefinitely — OTF's program criteria favor projects that have not yet been audited, and Cure53 mission rates are at their discretion per engagement.
- **Impact:** The Phase D floor figure of $90-100K/year depends on continued subsidy access that is not as durable as a one-time subsidy.
- **Recommendation:** Note in §10.4 that the recurring-audit floor assumes continued subsidy access and that Phase D sustainability depends on either subsidy continuity or direct-grant operational funding scaled to cover the unsubsidized recurring audit ($60-100K every 18-24 months → $30-65K/year amortized at the upper end of the recurring band).

### F8: "Trail of Bits civic-tech rates" framing in §8.5 and D0011 may overstate the program's depth

- **Evidence:** §8.5 line 809 and D0011 line 38 reference "Trail of Bits civic-tech engagement rates" as a lower-bound subsidy. Trail of Bits has done public civic-tech and open-source security work (e.g., osquery, Solidity audits), and they have engaged with civil-society projects at varying terms, but they do not operate a named "civic-tech rate" program comparable to OTF Secure Audit or Cure53 mission rates. The discount they extend is per-engagement at their discretion, not a published program.
- **Impact:** Naming Trail of Bits as a subsidy route at the same structural level as OTF or Cure53 mission rates overstates Trail of Bits' subsidy posture. Funders may interpret this as a more bankable route than it is.
- **Recommendation:** Reframe as "Trail of Bits engagements at the firm's discretionary reduced rate for civic-tech projects" rather than naming it as a comparable subsidy program.

## Patterns

Two patterns recur across the findings:

1. **Subsidy program conflation.** Cure53 mission rates and Trail of Bits civic-tech rates are vendor discount mechanisms; OTF Secure Audit and (when active) Mozilla OSAA are grant programs with cycles; NLnet NGI Zero Trust funds audits only when integrated with development grants. §10 treats all four as substitutable subsidy routes, which obscures the actual probability and timing distribution across them.

2. **Floors and ceilings derived from a single firm's pricing model.** Both the pre-pilot lower bound ($15K) and the pre-beta upper bound ($150K) make sense at Cure53 mission rates but are inconsistent with Trail of Bits or NCC Group pricing. The brief names four firms in §8.5 and D0011 as auditor candidates, but the budget figures are sized only for one (Cure53). The "we will engage whichever firm closes" framing is at structural tension with budget ranges that only one firm's pricing makes coherent.

---

## 150-word summary

§10's audit budgets are anchored to plausible references but contain three critical defensibility gaps. First, the pre-pilot $15K subsidized floor is inconsistent with the stated 1-2 person-week scope at any named subsidy program's actual effective rate — Cure53 mission rates and OTF Secure Audit historical awards land closer to $20-25K for the same scope. Second, the pre-beta $150K unsubsidized ceiling is sized only for Cure53 or Quarkslab pricing; Trail of Bits or NCC Group at the stated 4-6 week scope realistically lands at $180-240K. Third, the four named "subsidy programs" conflate grant programs (OTF, Mozilla OSAA) with vendor discount mechanisms (Cure53, Trail of Bits) and ignore OTF's entity-eligibility prerequisite that interacts with the Phase B fiscal-sponsor sequencing. Reviewer-honoraria arithmetic, UK CIC incorporation floor, and recurring-audit subsidy continuity are all defensible mid-band but understated at the floors used to derive Phase B and Phase C minima.
