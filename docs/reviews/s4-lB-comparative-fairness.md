# §4 — Comparative Fairness Lens

## Summary

§4 is the brief's "what makes us different" section. Unlike §2.3, which surveys alternatives one at a time and assigns each its own paragraph, §4's comparisons are compressed into one-line characterizations inside §4.3 ("Most existing tools either trust a single party (Signal Foundation; Wickr's AWS deployment; a Matrix homeserver), trust a peer-to-peer flood (PGP-style web of trust without a transparency log), or trust nothing (Briar's pure peer-to-peer model)"). At that compression rate, characterizations have to be careful. Several are not: the Signal/Wickr/Matrix trichotomy is presented as exhaustive in a landscape that includes Threema, Wire, Element-as-software-distinct-from-homeservers, and Tails/Qubes for OS-tier comparisons that §4.1 invites by introducing a "GrapheneOS endpoint" layer. The "no current product makes these four commitments together" framing in §4.3 (line 279) does most of the differentiation work, but several of the four commitments (multi-party release attestation; transparency-logged trust state) have closer prior art than §4 acknowledges.

The unfairness is mostly framing-by-omission rather than outright misrepresentation: alternatives' deliberate design choices are described as gaps without naming them as choices, and tools whose existence weakens Cairn's "no one has done this" claim (Cwtch's group-trust experiments, Briar's Mailbox, Status, Berty, Tails/Qubes, GrapheneOS's own Auditor) are absent.

## Critical findings

### F1: "Most consumer messaging tools rely on a single signing identity" misrepresents the current state of release security

- **Evidence:** §4.3 line 287: "Most consumer messaging tools rely on a single signing identity (the developer's private key) whose compromise affects every release indefinitely. Cairn's release stack — Sigstore identity-based per-release signing on top of the long-lived APK key, an external reviewer pool of 5+ with 3-of-5 attestation threshold, Sigsum-anchored attestations, multi-channel distribution — exposes provenance to multiple independent verifiers." The framing implies Cairn is approximately alone in addressing this. In practice: (1) Signal Android publishes reproducible builds and the APK signing certificate is verifiable against multiple distribution channels; (2) Briar has documented reproducible builds since 2017; (3) GrapheneOS itself — which Cairn _runs on_ — uses a multi-signer release process with public verification and is arguably the closest comparator for "multi-party publicly-verifiable Android release security"; (4) F-Droid (named as a v1 distribution channel in §4.2 line 263) is structurally a third-party rebuild/resign pipeline that is itself a form of independent attestation; (5) Sigstore-style transparency-logged signing is increasingly standard in open-source supply chains. The "external reviewer pool of 5+ with 3-of-5 attestation threshold" is genuinely novel; the rest of the stack is well-traveled.
- **Impact:** §4.3's strongest "no one else does this" claim rests on a sentence that is partially inaccurate. Reviewers from GrapheneOS, Briar, or supply-chain-security communities will read line 287 as the brief either not knowing the comparator landscape or selectively presenting it. Either reading damages credibility on the other three differentiators, which are stronger.
- **Recommendation:** Narrow the claim to what is actually distinct: the 5-of-N external reviewer attestation threshold (where N=5+) on top of, not as a replacement for, the standard reproducible-build/transparency-log practices. Acknowledge GrapheneOS, Briar reproducible builds, and Sigstore prior art explicitly. Strengthens rather than weakens the differentiation by ceding the parts that are not novel.

### F2: The "trust a single party / trust a flood / trust nothing" trichotomy is presented as exhaustive but omits the relevant comparator

- **Evidence:** §4.3 line 283: "Most existing tools either trust a single party (Signal Foundation; Wickr's AWS deployment; a Matrix homeserver), trust a peer-to-peer flood (PGP-style web of trust without a transparency log), or trust nothing (Briar's pure peer-to-peer model, where each pair of users handles verification themselves)." This omits the most architecturally similar prior art: (1) Keybase's signed-statement-chain model with public auditability — defunct as a product but the pattern is well-known and was specifically a transparency-logged social attestation graph; (2) CONIKS / Key Transparency (used by WhatsApp's Auditable Key Directory since 2023 and by iMessage Contact Key Verification since 2023) — transparency-logged key state with user-visible auditability; (3) Cwtch's group-trust mechanisms and trust-decision UI work, which are the closest peer-to-peer comparator to a "graph of attestations" model.
- **Impact:** WhatsApp's AKD and iMessage's CKV both ship today to billions of users and both use transparency logs to make key state publicly auditable. They are not Cairn (no social attestation graph, no master-tier separation, different threat tier) but they are in the same architectural family and a comparative-fairness reviewer will notice their absence. The claim "transparency-logged trust state is what makes Cairn structurally different" reads as weaker once those exist.
- **Recommendation:** Either widen the comparator set to include AKD/CKV/Keybase/CONIKS and distinguish Cairn within that family (Cairn attests _social structure_, AKD attests _key continuity_; Cairn anchors in Sigsum + witness cosignatures rather than a vendor-operated log), or narrow the claim to "a transparency-logged _social_ trust graph available to clients facing this threat tier" — qualifications that are accurate without inviting "Keybase already did this" pushback.

## Significant findings

### S1: §4 omits Tails and Qubes despite introducing an OS-tier comparison

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 246 establishes the endpoint layer as "GrapheneOS-on-Pixel device running the Cairn application" and treats the OS as part of Cairn's trust placement, not just an environment. §2.3 surveys messaging products but not OS-tier comparators. §4.3's "no current product in the landscape makes these four commitments together" thus implicitly defines "the landscape" as messaging-products-only, even though the brief's own architecture is a phone-OS-plus-messaging integration that overlaps materially with what Tails and Qubes do for the same audience.
- **Impact:** Tails is the canonical "operational-discipline-as-a-product" tool for the journalist/activist audience §2.2 names. Qubes is the canonical "compartmentation as a security property" tool. Both are integrated products combining standard primitives with operational discipline for a state-actor-adversary threat tier — which is exactly Cairn's pitch. A reviewer asking "how is this different from a Tails-equivalent for messaging on a phone" is asking a reasonable question that §4 does not answer.
- **Recommendation:** Add one paragraph in §4.3 explicitly distinguishing Cairn from Tails/Qubes: different threat surface (always-on messaging device vs. session-bounded amnesia OS vs. compartmented workstation), different operational unit (facilitator-supported individual vs. trained user vs. technical user), different trust placement (GrapheneOS + hardware vs. Debian + verified boot media vs. Xen + AEM). The comparison strengthens Cairn rather than weakening it but only if made explicitly.

### S2: "Briar's pure peer-to-peer model, where each pair of users handles verification themselves" understates Briar's actual trust model

- **Evidence:** §4.3 line 283 characterizes Briar as "trust nothing... each pair of users handles verification themselves." Briar's actual trust model includes (1) introduction protocol where a mutual contact can cryptographically introduce two users, (2) BQP (Bramble Question Protocol) for shared-secret verification, (3) forums and private groups with membership cryptographically rooted in introducer signatures. This is not "trust nothing"; it is small-scale graph-shaped trust without a transparency log. The architectural distance from Cairn's model is smaller than the line implies — Cairn adds transparency-log anchoring and a master/operational tier separation, but the graph-shape itself is not a Cairn invention relative to Briar.
- **Impact:** §4.3's strongest comparative claim — "Cairn's trust graph is signed-operation-based with public auditability via Sigsum commitments and witness cosignatures" — is genuinely differentiated from Briar's model. But the line that frames Briar as "trust nothing" overstates the gap and invites Briar maintainers reading the brief to push back on the characterization. §2.3 line 85 is more careful ("the protocol is correctly chosen for what it does"); §4.3 is not.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "trust nothing (Briar's pure peer-to-peer model, where each pair of users handles verification themselves)" with "trust a locally-rooted introduction graph without public auditability (Briar)" — accurate, preserves the differentiation (Sigsum anchoring + witness cosignatures), and is consistent with §2.3's framing.

### S3: Session, Cwtch, and Status are named or implied but Berty is absent

- **Evidence:** §2.3 covers Session (line 87) and Cwtch (line 89) but not Berty (peer-to-peer messaging with libp2p, IPFS-based content addressing, designed for offline-first / mesh use). §4 inherits §2.3's comparator set. Berty's relevance: it is the closest current product to a "messaging stack designed to survive infrastructure disruption" — which is §2.2's v3 audience for mesh integration. A reviewer doing comparator research will find Berty.
- **Impact:** Mid-tier. Berty is smaller-footprint than the others and a fair characterization would say "smaller deployed footprint, not load-bearing as a comparator" — but absence reads as not-having-looked rather than having-considered-and-set-aside.
- **Recommendation:** Either add Berty to §2.3 with a one-sentence characterization (and inherit it in §4), or add a footnote in §4.3 acknowledging the broader comparator set (Berty, Status, Tox, Ricochet Refresh) was considered and set aside as smaller-deployment or earlier-stage.

### S4: "Federation pushes the metadata-disclosure problem to the per-user homeserver" understates the Matrix project's active work on this

- **Evidence:** §2.3 line 79 characterizes Matrix this way; §4.3 line 283 inherits "a Matrix homeserver" as the single-trust-point example. Matrix has shipped multiple protocol-level mitigations (Pinecone overlay, dendrite/conduit lower-metadata homeservers, MSC for low-bandwidth and decentralized account portability) and the broader project explicitly treats homeserver-metadata as a problem to solve. The characterization is accurate as a v1-current-deployment description but elides that Matrix-the-project treats this as a known design problem, not a feature.
- **Impact:** Smaller than F1 or F2. The functional point — that Matrix-as-currently-deployed exposes metadata to homeservers — is correct. The framing implies design oversight where the actual situation is "known limitation of the current federation model, with active mitigation work in progress." Reviewers from the Matrix community will read this as uncharitable.
- **Recommendation:** No change required in §4 itself; if §2.3 is revised, add "Matrix's ongoing protocol work explicitly addresses metadata exposure" so §4's compressed reference inherits a fairer framing.

## Minor findings

### M1: "Cairn does not federate" is true but glosses Matrix's threat-tier rationale

- **Evidence:** §2.3 line 79 ends with "Cairn does not federate." The brief does not say _why_ federation is wrong for this threat tier — federation's argument is censorship-resistance and provider-choice, both of which are properties Cairn's audience also values. The omission is a small one but means the brief does not explicitly carry the argument it implicitly makes.
- **Impact:** Minor. Affects readability for reviewers who expect to see federation's rationale acknowledged before being declined.
- **Recommendation:** Either add a half-sentence in §2.3 ("federation's rationale — censorship-resistance, provider-choice — is met for Cairn's audience through Tor + self-hostable SimpleX relays rather than federated identity") or leave as-is; the §4 compression is not where to fix this.

### M2: Threema and Wire absent from §2.3 and thus from §4

- **Evidence:** Neither §2.3 nor §4 mentions Threema (Swiss-jurisdiction, identifier-less by design via 8-character Threema ID, paid app, audited) or Wire (open-source, MLS-based, Swiss/German jurisdiction). Both are reasonable comparators for "identity not tied to phone number" and "non-US jurisdiction matters" — both of which are Cairn arguments.
- **Impact:** Lower. Neither tool targets Cairn's threat tier; both target enterprise/privacy-conscious consumer. The omission is defensible but a comparator-completeness reviewer will note it.
- **Recommendation:** Add brief mentions in §2.3 (one sentence each) with the actual reason for setting aside (Threema: closed-source app, Swiss jurisdiction is favorable but the product is single-vendor-trust; Wire: MLS adoption is forward-looking but the threat tier is consumer/enterprise). No §4 change needed if §2.3 is updated.

### M3: GrapheneOS's own Auditor and remote attestation are not referenced

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 246 says "the user trusts the GrapheneOS image, the Pixel hardware, and that the Cairn application itself was installed through a verified provenance chain (Section 5.5)." GrapheneOS ships an Auditor app that provides hardware-attested device integrity verification — a tool that overlaps with Cairn's "verify the client is genuine" goal at the OS layer.
- **Impact:** Low. The Auditor verifies the OS, not the application; Cairn's release-security stack verifies the application. The integration question (does Cairn surface Auditor's attestation to peers? does the trust graph incorporate device-attestation?) is a Section 5 detail, not §4. Worth noting for the §4 → §5 handoff.
- **Recommendation:** No §4 change. Flag for §5.1/5.5 to address whether Cairn integrates with or duplicates Auditor's role.

## Patterns

1. **§4 compresses §2.3's careful per-product framing into one-line characterizations that lose nuance.** §2.3 says Briar "is correctly chosen for what it does"; §4.3 says Briar trusts nothing. §2.3 acknowledges Matrix's "strong ecosystem of clients and bridges" and treats federation as a deliberate tradeoff; §4.3 reduces Matrix to "a homeserver" as a single-trust-point caricature. The pattern: §4's differentiation argument leans harder than §2.3's characterizations support.

2. **"Most existing tools..." sentences in §4.3 do framing-by-exhaustion that the actual comparator set does not support.** Lines 283 and 287 both use "Most existing tools either X or Y or Z" framings where the three options are presented as covering the space. F1 and F2 show that AKD/CKV, Keybase, GrapheneOS's own release model, F-Droid's resign pipeline, Briar's reproducible builds, and Sigstore-the-standard exist in the gap between the named options. The fix is uniform: replace "most existing tools either X or Y or Z" with "no current product combines [specific Cairn-distinctive properties]" — which is what §4.3 line 279's framing was trying to do before line 283 and line 287 narrowed it.

3. **OS-tier comparators are introduced architecturally but not acknowledged comparatively.** §4.1 makes GrapheneOS load-bearing for the threat model; §4.3 does not engage with Tails or Qubes despite both being the closest "integration of standard primitives plus operational discipline for state-actor-adversary audience" prior art. S1's recommendation closes this.

4. **The four §4.3 commitments are individually strong but the framing claims more novelty than the field supports.** "The protocol layer is delegated; the discipline layer is built" (line 281) is genuinely differentiated within messaging-products. "Trust is graph-shaped and publicly auditable" is differentiated against Signal/Wickr/Matrix-as-deployed but less so against AKD/CKV/Keybase. "Recovery is social without being centralized" is the strongest of the four and faces the least prior art. "Release security is multi-party and publicly verifiable" is overstated relative to GrapheneOS, Sigstore-the-pattern, and F-Droid. Narrowing the second and fourth to what is actually distinct strengthens all four.
