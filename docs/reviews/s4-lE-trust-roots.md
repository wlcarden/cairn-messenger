# §4 — Trust Roots & Dependency Visibility Lens

## Summary

§4 reads as a clean architectural narrative, but the price of that cleanliness is dependency invisibility. The section names SimpleX, Briar, Tor, Sigstore, Sigsum, and GrapheneOS-on-Pixel — yet for a first-time reader §4 frames them almost exclusively as Cairn's chosen substrates rather than as trust placements whose upstream maintainer competence, jurisdictional exposure, and operational health Cairn's threat-tier resistance depends on. Several v1-central dependencies that §3.4 either names (OIDC provider, build supply chain) or that surface concretely elsewhere in the brief (UnifiedPush, F-Droid/Accrescent, Rekor) are absent from §4 entirely. The §4.3 line "the components are standard" (line 291) reinforces a reader impression that the dependencies are unremarkable infrastructure.

The most consequential gap is the §4.2 "Minimal project-operated infrastructure" principle (line 263), which tells the reader that "a Cairn deployment continues to function for its existing users if the project disappears." That statement is structurally true of the messaging plane but understates Cairn's dependence on a network of upstream projects (Tor, GrapheneOS, SimpleX, Briar, Sigstore/Rekor, Sigsum) whose disappearance or capture would degrade Cairn's threat-tier resistance just as surely as the project's own disappearance. §4 should make the reader feel the dependency surface that §3.4 catalogues, not file it away.

## Critical findings

### F1: §4.1 Endpoint layer's trust placement omits GrapheneOS-Pixel availability and upstream maintenance

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 246: "The trust placement: the user trusts the GrapheneOS image, the Pixel hardware, and that the Cairn application itself was installed through a verified provenance chain (Section 5.5)." §3.4 names GrapheneOS (line 178) and Pixel hardware (line 180) as trust roots. Neither §4 nor §3.4 surfaces what §3.5 confirms (line 224): the product is "anchored to the GrapheneOS-on-Pixel security baseline" with no fallback. GrapheneOS is a small-team upstream whose continued maintenance against Pixel firmware/driver changes is itself a project the brief depends on, and Google's continued shipment of unlocked-bootloader Pixels with Titan M2 is a vendor decision Cairn does not control.
- **Impact:** A reader of §4.1 forms the impression that "trust the GrapheneOS image and Pixel hardware" is a static cryptographic assumption (like trusting Ed25519). It is actually a dynamic dependency on an active upstream project's maintainer bandwidth and a single vendor's continued production of a specific hardware line. The brief's threat-tier claims collapse if GrapheneOS loses its maintainer pool or if Google ships a Pixel generation without an unlockable bootloader / verified-boot user-keys path.
- **Recommendation:** §4.1 should name GrapheneOS upstream maintenance and Pixel-line availability as continuity dependencies, with a cross-reference to a §3.4 trust-root entry that explicitly covers them. Currently §3.4's GrapheneOS entry treats backdoor / supply-chain compromise but not project-discontinuation or hardware-line-discontinuation risk.

### F2: §4.1 Transport layer frames Tor's properties as Cairn's properties

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 248: "Tor's role is to complicate traffic analysis and network-level surveillance for the threat tier without claiming to eliminate them; the limits are named explicitly in Section 3.4. v1 ships SimpleX messaging over Tor… Pluggable-transport selection is an ongoing engineering commitment rather than a one-time decision — DPI evasion is a continuously-being-solved problem." The framing positions Cairn as committing engineering effort to "pluggable-transport selection," but the actual pluggable transports (obfs4, meek, Snowflake, webtunnel) are Tor Project deliverables. Cairn's "ongoing engineering commitment" is dependency tracking, not protocol development.
- **Impact:** A reader is led to believe Cairn's DPI-evasion posture is a Cairn engineering property. It is in fact a transitive consumption of the Tor Project's anti-censorship roadmap. If the Tor Project's anti-censorship team loses funding (a recurring real risk), Cairn's threat-tier coverage against state-level network blocking degrades without any Cairn-side change. §3.4 line 184 names Tor as a trust root for anonymity properties but does not name dependency on the Tor Project's ongoing anti-censorship maintenance.
- **Recommendation:** §4.1 should distinguish "Cairn integrates pluggable transports" from "Cairn depends on the Tor Project's continued delivery of pluggable transports." A §3.4 cross-reference to a Tor Project organizational-health trust placement (paralleling the OIDC-provider jurisdiction placement at line 196) would correctly frame what the reader is being asked to trust.

### F3: §4.1 Communications layer hides that SimpleX/Briar metadata-resistance is upstream-maintainer-dependent

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 250: "SimpleX is the v1 messaging substrate — identifier-less, queue-based, with no persistent user identity at the protocol level (Section 5.4). Briar joins as the highest-sensitivity tier in v1.5." §3.4 line 186: "The SimpleX and Briar protocols. Their core cryptographic constructions… are assumed correct. Both projects have undergone external review and both remain subject to ongoing scrutiny." Neither passage acknowledges that SimpleX's metadata-resistance properties (queue rotation, server-pool diversity, future post-quantum work) are an evolving roadmap maintained by a small upstream team, and Briar's BTP/BSP work has historically had narrow contributor depth.
- **Impact:** §4.1 presents "identifier-less, queue-based, no persistent user identity" as a static architectural property of SimpleX that Cairn inherits. The reader does not learn that the metadata-resistance properties Cairn's threat tier needs are a roadmap, not a finished artifact, and that an upstream project decision (or compromise, or maintainer attrition, or funder capture) could shift those properties in ways that Cairn would have to either follow, fork, or replace. The §4.3 line 281 framing "projects with explicit missions to build and maintain those tools" understates how dependent Cairn's claimed properties are on those missions continuing to be funded and staffed.
- **Recommendation:** §4.1 (or a sub-bullet of the §4.2 "minimal project-operated infrastructure" principle) should acknowledge that the metadata-resistance properties Cairn derives from SimpleX and Briar are upstream-roadmap-dependent, not finalized; and that the project has no current capacity to fork either if upstream direction diverges from Cairn's threat tier.

### F4: §4 does not mention UnifiedPush or Rekor at all, despite both being v1 dependencies §3.4 and §5 acknowledge

- **Evidence:** Search of §4 (lines 240–294) returns zero hits for "UnifiedPush," "Rekor," "F-Droid" (one hit at line 263 in passing), or "Accrescent" (one hit at line 263 in passing). Meanwhile §3.4 line 196 names the OIDC provider as a trust root (which implies Rekor); §5.4 line 456 establishes UnifiedPush as Cairn's push protocol; §5.5 line 480 establishes Rekor as the Sigstore transparency log Cairn depends on; §5.5 line 494 establishes F-Droid and Accrescent as primary distribution channels. §6 line 899 names the UnifiedPush distributor as a metadata-channel surface.
- **Impact:** A reader who reads §4 and stops (which the brief invites: §4 is the "orientation level," line 244) leaves with no awareness that v1 depends on (a) UnifiedPush distributor availability and the UnifiedPush protocol team's continued maintenance, (b) Rekor's single-operator transparency log model (named only at §6 line 957 as a "Sigstore-specific operational risk"), (c) F-Droid's app-review pipeline and signing process, or (d) Accrescent's continued development as a distribution channel. All four are v1-central and at least three add jurisdictional or maintainer-health exposure that the §3 threat model touches.
- **Recommendation:** §4 needs at least one paragraph (most naturally in §4.1 or §4.2) that enumerates the v1 external dependency surface beyond Tor/SimpleX/Briar/Sigstore/Sigsum/GrapheneOS — specifically: UnifiedPush (protocol + distributor), Rekor (Sigstore's log infrastructure), F-Droid and Accrescent (distribution channels). Without this the reader leaves §4 with an incomplete dependency model.

## Significant findings

### F5: §4.1 "release-security stack" reference to Sigstore omits the OIDC-jurisdiction trust placement §3.4 names

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 255 lists "two-layer signing (long-lived APK key plus per-release Sigstore attestation), external reviewer pool of 5+ with 3-of-5 attestation threshold, multi-channel distribution." §3.4 line 196 names the OIDC identity provider as a trust root and explicitly notes its jurisdiction enters Cairn's effective trust surface. §4 references Sigstore three times (lines 255, 281, 287) and never cross-references the OIDC-jurisdiction trust placement.
- **Impact:** §4.3 line 287's "publicly verifiable" framing of release security reads to a first-time reader as if the only trust placement is "the public log." The actual v1 architecture places trust in a specific OIDC provider (currently U.S.-based per §5.5 line 488) whose jurisdiction is a property the partner organizations the project is courting may consider disqualifying. A reader of §4 will not surface this question.
- **Recommendation:** §4.1 or §4.3's Sigstore mentions should carry a short parenthetical cross-reference to §3.4's OIDC trust-root entry. The current §4.3 line 287 framing is technically accurate but operationally misleading.

### F6: §4.2 "Minimal project-operated infrastructure" obscures the dependency on third-party-operated infrastructure

- **Evidence:** §4.2 line 263: "The trust-graph propagation path and the user's daily messaging do not depend on project infrastructure; a Cairn deployment continues to function for its existing users if the project disappears." The statement is bounded to "project infrastructure" but a reader will hear "infrastructure." The principle's actual semantic content is: Cairn's runtime substitutes third-party-operated infrastructure (Tor relays, SimpleX servers, Sigsum witnesses, Sigstore/Rekor, UnifiedPush distributors, F-Droid mirrors) for project-operated infrastructure.
- **Impact:** "Continues to function if the project disappears" is true only if the third-party infrastructure continues to function. The §3.4 trust roots make this clear; §4.2 does not. A funder or partner reading §4.2 in isolation may conclude that Cairn is more sustainability-robust than it actually is.
- **Recommendation:** §4.2 should reframe the principle as "trust distributed across multiple third-party-operated infrastructures rather than concentrated in project-operated infrastructure" and explicitly name the third-party infrastructures the user depends on instead. The current framing is sustainability-optimistic in a way that the rest of the brief does not support.

### F7: §4.3 "the components are standard" understates the upstream-maintainer concentration

- **Evidence:** §4.3 line 291: "Cairn's claim is not that it has invented a new approach to secure messaging — the components are standard." §4.3 line 281 also: "SimpleX, Briar, Tor, Sigstore, and Sigsum are the cryptographic and protocol substrates — projects with explicit missions to build and maintain those tools."
- **Impact:** "Standard" suggests substitutable, broadly maintained, low-risk components. The actual upstream picture: SimpleX has a small core team; Briar's protocol work has narrow contributor depth; Sigsum is a small Swedish project; Tor's anti-censorship work is grant-funded with periodic continuity scares; GrapheneOS is a small team. The "components are standard" framing carries a sustainability and substitutability connotation that does not match the upstream reality. A funder is precisely the audience for whom this overstatement matters.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "the components are standard" with framing that names what Cairn is actually claiming — that the cryptographic primitives are standard and that the protocol substrates are externally maintained projects whose continued operation Cairn depends on. The §4.3 "delegated" framing at line 281 is closer to honest but needs to land in the closing paragraph too.

### F8: §4 does not surface dependency-compromise failure modes that §3.3 and §6 catalogue

- **Evidence:** §3.3 line 142 names the Update channel surface. §6 line 957 names "Sigstore-specific operational risks" including "Rekor's single-operator log model." §6 line 899 names the UnifiedPush distributor metadata channel. §5.5 line 498 specifies the Sigstore-identity-compromise and long-lived APK key compromise response plans. None of these failure modes is surfaced in §4.
- **Impact:** §4 is the section that orients a reviewer to "what does this product look like and what could go wrong with it." The current §4 surfaces what the product looks like (architecturally clean) but not what could go wrong with it at the dependency level (Sigstore-identity compromise, Rekor operator capture, OIDC-jurisdiction legal process, GrapheneOS-Pixel discontinuation, UnifiedPush distributor compromise, SimpleX-team capture). A first-time reader leaves §4 underestimating the dependency-failure surface relative to what §5 and §6 will later expose. The lens-relevant consequence: dependency compromise risk is structurally invisible in §4.
- **Recommendation:** §4 should add a short paragraph (most naturally as the closing of §4.2 "Honest about limits" at line 275, or as an explicit new principle) enumerating the dependency-compromise failure modes the architecture inherits, with cross-references to §3.3 and §3.4. The §4.2 "honest about limits" paragraph currently names HUMINT, Tool-use, evil-maid, and Recovery network surfaces — but conspicuously omits the dependency-compromise surfaces.

## Minor findings

### F9: §4.1 line 248 SimpleX/Briar ordering is misleading in dependency-visibility terms

- **Evidence:** §4.1 line 248 says "v1 ships SimpleX messaging over Tor; v1.5 adds Briar (peer-to-peer over Tor) as the highest-sensitivity tier."
- **Impact:** The reader is led to think SimpleX is the lower-risk, more-mature dependency and Briar is the higher-risk specialty addition. The actual upstream-maintenance picture is closer to inverse: Briar is older and has had broader academic review (BTP/BSP), while SimpleX's metadata-resistance posture is younger and more roadmap-dependent. The framing — accurate as a sensitivity-tier statement — obscures the dependency-maturity question.
- **Recommendation:** No change required if §3.4 line 186 is read alongside §4.1; but a short note that SimpleX is the younger of the two upstream projects would correctly calibrate the reader's dependency-maturity impression.

### F10: §4.3 differentiation framing makes Cairn sound infrastructure-light vs. competitors

- **Evidence:** §4.3 line 281: "most are 'a protocol with a UI'; Cairn is 'an operational discipline with an integration of protocols.'"
- **Impact:** The framing is rhetorically clean but suggests Cairn carries less infrastructure dependency than e.g. Signal (which depends on Signal Foundation infrastructure). Cairn does not carry less dependency — it carries differently-shaped dependency. Signal places trust in one centralized operator; Cairn places trust in a distributed set of upstream projects and third-party operators. A funder may infer "fewer moving parts to fail" when the accurate framing is "more moving parts, but failure of any single one is bounded."
- **Recommendation:** Calibrate §4.3 line 281 to name the dependency shape Cairn actually adopts rather than implying dependency lightness.

## Patterns

1. **§4 surfaces dependencies the way an architect would write them — by name, in role — and not the way a reviewer needs to see them — as trust placements with upstream-maintenance, jurisdictional, and operational properties.** §3.4 already does the trust-placement framing; §4 needs to systematically cross-reference §3.4 where it names a dependency. Currently §4 cross-references §3.4 only at line 248 (Tor limits) and obliquely at line 263 (consequences acknowledged in §3.4).

2. **The dependencies §4 omits are the ones that don't fit the architectural-layer model.** UnifiedPush, F-Droid, Accrescent, Rekor, and the OIDC provider are operational infrastructure, not architectural layers. §4's layer-shaped narrative has no obvious slot for them, so they fall out. A short "v1 external dependency surface" paragraph in §4.1 or §4.2 would fix this without disrupting the layer model.

3. **§4 framings consistently shade toward "Cairn property" rather than "inherited property."** Tor's DPI-evasion roadmap, SimpleX's metadata-resistance roadmap, GrapheneOS's continued maintenance, Sigstore's OIDC chain — each is presented as a Cairn-stack feature rather than as an inherited dependency whose continued health Cairn cannot independently guarantee. A small linguistic shift (from "Cairn does X" to "Cairn integrates X's X") in §4.1 and §4.3 would close most of this gap without restructuring.

4. **The §3.4 → §4 inconsistency is asymmetric: §3.4 is more honest than §4.** Where §3.4 names the OIDC provider's jurisdiction as a trust placement, §4 mentions Sigstore three times without cross-referencing it. Where §3.4 names the reviewer pool and the witness pool's correlated-trust note, §4 names the reviewer pool but not the witness-pool correlation. The reader who reads only §4 forms a less-honest picture of Cairn's trust placements than the reader who reads §3.4. §4 should not regress §3.4's honesty.
