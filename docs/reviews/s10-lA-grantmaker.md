# §10 — Grantmaker Actionability Lens

## Summary

§10 is structured for narrative honesty rather than for grant-administration consumption. Read as a programme officer evaluating whether to route Cairn into a docket, the section is legible enough to extract phase ranges (Phase B floor ~$17K, Phase C ceiling ~$335K, Phase D ~$90-250K/yr) but actively withholds the structural elements a committee needs to fund: a named ask, a deliverable schedule tied to a tranche, a marginal-impact statement against a stated baseline, and a reporting cadence. The brief explicitly disclaims most of these in §10.8 ("the brief is not a fundraising target with a deadline attached"), which is honest but transfers all conversion work — turning the phased model into a grant application — to the developer, post-hoc, per-funder. For a self-funded solo developer with limited grant-writing bandwidth, this is the inverse of leverage: a single well-structured "what we'd ask of OTF / what we'd ask of NLnet" appendix in §10 would let conversations begin from a shared artifact.

The deeper grant-administration problem is fit-to-instrument. §10.5 lists eleven funder categories without sizing any of them against the named programme's actual grant envelope, and §10.6 sequences pursuit without naming a target-range per funder. OTF Secure Audit awards typically sit in the $50-150K range; NLnet NGI Zero Trust calls cap at €50K for small grants and €300K for larger ones; Ford and OSF run substantially larger but more complex multi-year operational grants. A funder reading §10.5 cannot tell which item maps to their instrument; a developer pursuing §10.6 in parallel cannot tell what a "successful subsidy close" looks like without doing per-programme research the brief defers entirely. Both readers are doing work §10 could amortize once.

## Critical findings

### F1: No named ask. The brief presents costs but never an ask.

- **Evidence:** §10 (lines 1016-1172) characterizes phases, totals, and contingencies but never states a request. The closest is §10.2:1071 ("a single subsidy-program close [...] at the lower end of its typical range covers Phase B and unlocks the pilot"), which describes a sufficiency condition, not an ask. §10.8:1172 explicitly disclaims: "it is not a fundraising target with a deadline attached." No section frames a sentence of the form "the project is requesting $X from a funder in category Y to fund deliverables Z by date W."
- **Impact:** Programme officers read briefs to determine whether to invite an application. Without a named ask, the reader cannot tell whether Cairn is currently fundraising, in what amount, against what instrument, or whether the document is the application itself, a precursor, or context for a future application. Committee paperwork requires a request line. A docket reviewer who must summarize this for a committee in two sentences cannot do it from §10 as written without inventing the ask, which is exactly the work the brief should do once for all readers. The honest-disclosure framing (§10.8) does not preclude a structured ask; it precludes a fake one.
- **Recommendation:** Add §10.9 "Current funding requests" enumerating the project's actual pursuit posture at brief publication. Example: "The project is currently approaching: (a) OTF Secure Audit programme for $25-30K covering the pre-pilot audit (Phase B item); (b) NLnet NGI Zero Trust call for €50K covering pre-incorporation legal consultation plus initial honoraria; (c) deferring direct large-grant applications until partner-organization outreach (§8.6) produces co-applicants." If the project is not yet ready to ask, state that explicitly with a target date.

### F2: §10.5 funder roster is not mapped to phase items or instrument size.

- **Evidence:** §10.5:1119-1140 lists eleven funder categories (OTF Secure Audit, Cure53 mission rates, Mozilla MOSS, NLnet NGI Zero Trust, OTF main grants, Ford, OSF, MOSS successors, European democracy instruments, NLnet main, Knight) without naming any programme's typical grant envelope, cycle length, or which §10.1-§10.4 line item each funder fits. §10.6:1147 says "multiple programs in parallel; grant cycles vary from 3-9 months" but does not break this down per programme.
- **Impact:** A programme officer evaluating fit against their own instrument cannot tell from §10.5 whether the project is a candidate for them specifically. OTF Secure Audit subsidizes Phase B audit; OTF main grants are sized for Phase C operational support; NLnet NGI Zero Trust at the €50K small-grant tier matches different items than NGI Zero Trust at the €300K large tier; Ford and OSF run multi-year operational grants that fit Phase D, not Phase B. Lumping these in a flat list forces every reader to redo the mapping. A developer using §10.5 as a pursuit map cannot prioritize without doing the same per-programme research per cycle.
- **Recommendation:** Restructure §10.5 as a phase-to-funder matrix. For each named funder, state: typical grant envelope range, typical cycle length, which §10.1-§10.4 line items it fits, and whether the project considers it a primary or secondary route for that item. Example row: "OTF Secure Audit — $30-150K per award, ~6 month cycle, fits Phase B pre-pilot audit (primary route) and Phase C pre-beta audit (primary route)." If a funder's instrument doesn't fit any line item cleanly, say so.

### F3: Deliverables are not tied to funding tranches in a way that supports grant-agreement drafting.

- **Evidence:** §10.1-§10.4 list deliverables and costs separately within each phase. §10.2:1075 ("What Phase B unlocks. Pilot deployment with the cryptographic core externally audited") is the closest the brief comes to tying funding to outcome, but it does not state milestones, reporting points, or what the project would commit to deliver per dollar tranche. §10.7:1156-1160 enumerates failure modes but not success deliverables.
- **Impact:** Grant agreements require a deliverables schedule with reporting milestones. Programme officers and grant-administration staff draft these from the applicant's proposal; if the proposal does not contain them, the staff either request them (slowing the cycle) or generate them and ask the applicant to confirm (introducing miscommunication risk). For OTF Secure Audit and NLnet NGI Zero Trust specifically, both programmes require milestone-based deliverables in their standard contracts. The phase-gated model is well-suited to milestone structure — Phase B has natural milestones (audit firm engaged, audit kickoff, audit report received, audit findings remediated) — but §10 does not surface them.
- **Recommendation:** For each phase, add a "Deliverables and reporting" subsection enumerating the milestones a funder would receive against the phase's funding. Phase B example: M1 audit firm engagement (within 60 days of award), M2 audit kickoff (within 90 days), M3 draft report (within 180 days), M4 remediation status report (within 240 days), M5 pilot kickoff. This also disambiguates what "subsidy program closes" means operationally (§10.2:1071) — funders fund deliverables, not "Phase B."

## Significant findings

### F4: No marginal-impact statement against existing absorbed funding.

- **Evidence:** §10.1:1043 states Phase A absorbed costs are "approximately $1,500-3,500 across the duration, primarily hardware" and that developer time is unvalued. §10 does not state cumulative developer hours invested to date, equivalent dollar value at market rate, or what fraction of total project value is already in-kind. §10.5:1123 ("Self/personal") notes the absorbed posture without quantifying it.
- **Impact:** Foundations evaluating marginal impact ("what does our dollar produce that wouldn't otherwise happen?") need a baseline. Without a stated in-kind contribution figure, the brief reads as "a project that costs $17-60K to advance to pilot" rather than "a project where ~$X of in-kind work has been invested and $17-60K of grant funding converts that into a piloted product." The latter framing is materially stronger for foundations whose programme criteria reward leverage. Briar, SimpleX, and Signal Foundation grant histories all surface in-kind valuation prominently. §10's silence on this is a missed leverage signal — and one funders will themselves estimate, possibly less generously than the developer would.
- **Recommendation:** Add a sentence to §10.1 stating cumulative developer hours to date (or a defensible estimate) and the equivalent at the security-engineering market rate the brief uses elsewhere ($150-250K/FTE/yr per §10.4:1117). Frame it as "in-kind contribution that grants leverage rather than substitute for." This is honest disclosure of effort, not a fake-FTE conversion.

### F5: No statement of what the project would report to funders at each phase.

- **Evidence:** §10 does not specify reporting cadence, format, or content. §8.5:817 covers audit-report publication and §8.4:798 covers board composition once incorporated, but §10 itself is silent on what a funded project reports back. §10.6:1141-1149 sequences pursuit but does not address post-award reporting.
- **Impact:** Grant agreements include reporting requirements; programme officers want to know what they'll see and at what cadence before recommending an award. For pre-incorporation fiscal-sponsor grants, the reporting boundary is especially important because the developer-as-natural-person and the fiscal sponsor have a shared reporting obligation. A funder reading §10 cannot tell whether the project will report quarterly, semi-annually, against milestones, against the transparency log only, or some combination. The phase-gated model could naturally support phase-completion reports; §10 does not articulate this.
- **Recommendation:** Add a "Reporting commitments" paragraph to §10 (or as §10.9). State: reporting cadence (e.g., quarterly progress notes, milestone-triggered reports, annual financial-and-impact summary post-incorporation), reporting format (public transparency log entries vs. funder-private reports), and what kind of evidence each report contains. Tie it to the transparency-log architecture in §5.5 where possible, which is a credibility-strengthening detail for security-tooling funders.

### F6: Fiscal-sponsor cycle timing is not realistic about grant-administration friction.

- **Evidence:** §10.2:1062 describes fiscal-sponsor setup as "typically free-to-low at lower-overhead sponsors" with fees "typically 5-15% of routed grants." §10.6:1146 commits the project to engaging the fiscal-sponsor question before first grant intake. Neither addresses the calendar reality that fiscal-sponsor application-to-onboarding cycles at SFC, OCF, and CS&S typically run 2-6 months (SFC's published process; OCF's project-onboarding timeline; CS&S's typical 3-month evaluation cycle), often with a queue. The 5-15% fee range is also lower than current OSC/SFC published rates, which sit at 10-15% standard with reduced rates negotiable for established projects.
- **Impact:** A programme officer who is willing to fund Cairn at Phase B may discover that the project cannot accept funds for 3-6 months after award notification because fiscal-sponsor onboarding has not started. This is a recoverable problem for the funder but signals operational immaturity in the proposal. NLnet handles this internally (it sponsors its own grantees), so NLnet routes avoid the issue; OTF, Ford, OSF do not. The brief's optimistic "free-to-low" framing understates the calendar friction.
- **Recommendation:** Update §10.2:1062 to state typical fiscal-sponsor onboarding cycle length (2-6 months) and recommend the developer initiate sponsor onboarding before first grant award, not after. Tighten the fee range to "5-15% typical, often 10-15% at established sponsors." Note explicitly that NLnet's grantee-sponsorship model avoids this gap and is therefore the lowest-friction first route.

### F7: Phase B "$17K floor" obscures the structural minimum a funder is actually being asked for.

- **Evidence:** §10.2:1068 states the Phase B floor as "approximately $17,000 (legal consultation lower bound + subsidized pre-pilot audit lower bound + ongoing infrastructure)." This sums (a) $1,500 legal consultation, (b) $15,000 subsidized audit, (c) $500 infrastructure. The audit lower bound is conditional on subsidy-program close (§10.2:1063, D0011:51); the legal consultation lower bound is "the developer may absorb it personally if grant timing requires" (§10.2:1061). The infrastructure cost is in Phase A absorption.
- **Impact:** A programme officer reading "$17K floor" infers that a $17K grant would unlock Phase B. In fact, no $17K grant exists in the named instrument set that would accomplish this — OTF Secure Audit awards do not size at $17K, NLnet NGI Zero Trust small grants are €50K, Cure53 mission engagements are scoped per audit not per dollar. The $17K figure is an arithmetic floor of independently-funded items, not an addressable grant request. A funder cannot write a $17K check that does what §10.2 implies. The structural-minimum addressable grant is closer to $30K (Cure53 mission-rate small engagement, fully covering audit + absorbed-by-developer legal).
- **Recommendation:** Reframe §10.2 totals as "addressable grant ranges by typical instrument" rather than arithmetic floor/ceiling. Example: "OTF Secure Audit award typically $30-60K, covers full Phase B excluding legal consultation. NLnet NGI Zero Trust small grant at €50K covers full Phase B including legal consultation. Cure53 mission-rate audit engagement at $20-30K covers audit only; legal consultation absorbed or separately funded." This matches how funders think about their own instruments.

## Minor findings

### F8: Funder list omits Internews and Digital Defenders Partnership.

- **Evidence:** §10.5:1119-1140 names eleven funder categories but does not include Internews (digital-rights infrastructure funding, active programme for civil-society security tooling) or Digital Defenders Partnership (rapid-response and capacity-building grants for at-risk-population tooling, run by Hivos in The Hague). Both are programmatically relevant for Cairn's audience.
- **Impact:** Mild. The roster is reasonably complete and the omission does not damage the brief's credibility. But for a developer using §10.5 as a pursuit map, the omission is a missed application opportunity.
- **Recommendation:** Add both to the §10.5 direct-grant list with a brief programme-fit note.

### F9: §10.4 maintainer-comp "$150-250K/yr per FTE" range is U.S.-equivalent and should be stated as such for non-U.S. readers.

- **Evidence:** §10.4:1117 states "approximately $150,000-250,000 per year per FTE (US-equivalent rates; jurisdiction-dependent)." The jurisdiction-dependence is acknowledged but not characterized. European, UK, and Dutch security-engineering rates differ materially; given §10.4:1108 contemplates non-U.S. incorporation (D0010), readers from European funders may misread the range as the project's actual aspiration rather than a U.S.-benchmark anchor.
- **Impact:** Minor framing issue. European funders may evaluate the maintainer-comp aspiration as out-of-line with EU non-profit norms when the developer might actually target EU-equivalent rates if EU-incorporated.
- **Recommendation:** Briefly note the EU-equivalent range (~€80-130K/yr at senior security-engineering rates) alongside the U.S. range, or state explicitly that the project will target jurisdiction-appropriate rates at incorporation.

### F10: "Subsidy program close" terminology is used without definition.

- **Evidence:** §10.2:1063, §10.2:1071, §10.3:1085, §10.5:1124, §10.7:1155 use "subsidy programs close" or "subsidy close" repeatedly. The phrase appears to mean "an application to a subsidy programme is awarded."
- **Impact:** "Close" is M&A/sales vocabulary that does not match grant-administration vocabulary. Programme officers say "awarded," "approved," or "funded"; "close" reads slightly off-domain. Minor credibility detail.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "subsidy programs close" with "subsidy program award" or "grant award" throughout §10.

## Patterns

Three patterns underlie the findings. First, **§10 optimizes for narrative integrity over administrative actionability** — the phase-gated structure is intellectually honest but does not produce the artifacts (named ask, deliverable schedule, reporting plan, instrument-fit mapping) that committee paperwork requires. The §10.8 disclaimer makes this explicit, which is admirable but transfers conversion work to the developer post-hoc per funder. Second, **the funder-to-item mapping is one-to-many in both directions and §10 leaves the mapping implicit** — eleven funders, four phases, ~20 line items, and no matrix. A reader doing the mapping does it once per reader; the brief could do it once for all readers. Third, **arithmetic-floor framing (Phase B $17K, Phase C $105K, Phase D $90K) does not align with how funders size instruments** — addressable grant ranges by named instrument would be more useful than independent-item sums, even though the latter is mathematically tidier.

The structural fix in all three cases is the same: an "addressable asks" appendix to §10 that maps each phase to specific named-funder asks with deliverable schedules and reporting commitments. The phase-gated model is the right substrate; it just needs a complementary surface that speaks to grant-administration directly.
