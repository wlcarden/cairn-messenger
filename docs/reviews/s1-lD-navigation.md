# §1 — Navigation / What-to-Do-Next Lens

## Summary

§1's "Where to read next" paragraph (line 34) is a section enumeration, not a navigation aid. It walks §2 through §10 in serial order with a one-clause descriptor per section — the same information a reader gets from the table of contents — and then appends an abstract pointer to the decisions directory and open-questions tracker. No reader intent is mapped to a section. A funder evaluating fit, a partner evaluating facilitation, a researcher evaluating cryptography, and a pilot candidate evaluating audience fit are all left to read top-to-bottom or guess. Given that §1 itself runs roughly 16 dense paragraphs of body prose and the brief totals ~1,650 lines, the cost of forcing every reader through the full structure is high.

§1 also under-surfaces the two cross-document instruments it depends on. Decision documents D0001–D0014 are cited inline at point-of-claim (good) but the navigation paragraph treats `docs/decisions/` as an undifferentiated bucket; no reader can tell from §1 which decisions are load-bearing for which argument. The open-questions tracker (Q1–Q26) is mentioned once at the end of the navigation paragraph as a generic pointer, despite §1 itself naming Q17, Q18, Q19, Q3, and Q5 inline as the questions on which audience size, differentiation, localization, and partner outreach actually turn. A sophisticated reader who knows they want to engage at Q5 (NGO partner outreach) cannot tell from §1 that Q5 is the partner-engagement entry point — they would have to read §8.6 first.

## Critical findings

### F1: "Where to read next" enumerates the TOC instead of mapping reader intent to sections

- **Evidence:** Line 34: "Section 2 establishes the problem, audience, and landscape. Section 3 specifies the threat model. Sections 4 and 5 cover the architecture at the orientation and detailed levels respectively. Sections 6 and 7 cover v1 scope and the roadmap. Section 8 covers operations and governance. Section 9 covers risks and limitations. Section 10 covers funding." This is the table of contents in prose form. The brief's own framing at line 14 names three reader audiences (technical reviewers, operational reviewers, funders); §2.2 (line 58) names a fourth (pilot candidates); §1 closes without telling any of them which section to open first.
- **Impact:** A funder evaluating Cairn's grant case has to read through §§2–7 to reach §10's phase model and §8.5's audit-budget commitment — both of which are what they actually need. A partner organization evaluating whether to facilitate has to navigate to §8.6 and Q5 without §1 indicating those exist as the partner-engagement entry points. A pilot candidate has to find §2.2's exclusions and §6.3's pilot scope. The result: §1 fails at the job it explicitly sets itself in line 20 ("a single-section orientation that lets a busy reader decide whether to engage with the rest of the brief"). Busy readers either give up or read linearly and lose the orientation §1 was meant to provide.
- **Recommendation:** Replace the serial enumeration with a reader-intent map. Concretely: a short paragraph or bulleted block that says, for each of the four named reader types, which 2–3 sections answer their question and which open-questions and decisions are most relevant. Example structure:
  - Funders evaluating fit: §10 (phase-gated model), §8.5 (audit budget), §2.3 (differentiation caveat and Q18), §9 (risks).
  - Partner organizations: §2.2 (audience and exclusions), §8.6 (partnership approach), §6.3 (pilot deployment), Q5 (partner outreach status).
  - Technical reviewers and cryptographers: §3 (threat model), §4.3 (differentiation at the integration boundary), §5 (architecture detail), D0006 (cryptographic envelope), §8.5 (audit scope).
  - Pilot candidates and facilitators: §2.2 (audience and exclusions), §6.3 (pilot plan), §8.6 (facilitator model), D0013 (consent and exit).

### F2: Decision documents D0001–D0014 are invisible at the navigation level

- **Evidence:** §1 cites 11 of the 14 decisions inline at point-of-claim (D0001, D0003, D0004, D0005, D0006, D0008, D0009, D0010, D0011, D0012, D0013, D0014) — see lines 22, 26, 28, 30. But the navigation paragraph on line 34 treats them as a single undifferentiated set: "Decision documents in `docs/decisions/` (D0001–D0014) record specific architectural and operational decisions with their alternatives and rationale." Appendix A.2 (line 1305) groups decisions thematically (architecture, operations, audience, governance, funding) but §1 does not point to that grouping. A reader who wants to engage with the cryptographic-envelope rationale cannot tell from §1 that D0006 is the central document; a reader evaluating the volunteer cadence claim cannot tell D0008 is the load-bearing one.
- **Impact:** The brief's intellectual rigor — that every load-bearing commitment in §1 has a decision document behind it — is invisible to a reader who only reads §1. The decision documents do their work locally at each citation, but as a navigable asset they are flattened. A sophisticated funder or researcher who is conditioned to read decision logs (architecture decision records are common in well-run technical projects) is not told the decision register exists as a coherent artifact worth engaging with on its own.
- **Recommendation:** Add a sentence to §1 pointing to Appendix A.2's thematic grouping and naming 2–3 of the most load-bearing decisions by ID — for example: "The most load-bearing decisions are D0006 (cryptographic envelope), D0008 (volunteer-baseline cadence), D0011 (audit budget and timing), and D0014 (non-peer recovery); Appendix A.2 organizes the full register by theme." This costs one sentence and gives a reader an entry point into a body of work §1 currently treats as a footnote pool.

### F3: Open-questions tracker is misrepresented as a generic pointer despite §1 naming five specific questions

- **Evidence:** §1 inline-cites Q3 (line 30, funding for honoraria), Q5 (line 30, NGO partner outreach), Q17 (line 24, audience-size estimate), Q18 (line 32, differentiation evidence basis), and Q19 (line 24, localization). These are not minor open items — Q18 is explicitly described as "the central grant-case question" (line 32) and Q5 is the partner-engagement dependency for half the operational commitments in §8. But the navigation paragraph on line 34 says only: "open questions in [open-questions.md](open-questions.md) (Q1–Q26) track unresolved items, with resolved questions moving to decision documents." A reader who skims §1 and wants to understand what the project is actively engaging on cannot tell from this sentence that Q18 is central, that Q5 gates partner outreach, or that Q17 gates the audience-size claim.
- **Impact:** Two reader populations are specifically failed. (1) Funders who want to know what the project is still working out before grant submission cannot tell which questions are central versus housekeeping — Q24 (line 380, reserved/duplicate) is in the same flat list as Q18 ("central grant-case question"). (2) Potential partner organizations cannot see that Q5 is the explicit hook for their engagement; they would have to read §8.6 to discover this.
- **Recommendation:** Either (a) extend the navigation paragraph to name the 3–5 most consequential open questions and their associated section, or (b) add a short "Where the project is actively engaging" paragraph after the navigation paragraph that surfaces Q18 (differentiation evidence), Q5 (partner outreach), Q3 (funding), Q17 (audience size), and Q20 (self-funding runway) as the open items with the highest grant-and-partner relevance. Option (b) also addresses the question of what a reader can do now (read §X) versus what depends on the project's outreach (engage at Q5) — see F5 below.

## Significant findings

### F4: §1 does not distinguish actionable-now from outreach-dependent

- **Evidence:** §1 line 30 describes the funding posture as "deliberately conditional" and names which items are unilateral commitments versus conditional. The taxonomy is good. But it never crosses over to navigation: a partner who has read §1 and decided they want to engage cannot tell from §1 whether their entry point is reading a section (e.g., §8.6 partnership approach) or engaging in the open question (Q5 NGO partner outreach). The same is true for funders: §1 does not tell them whether Phase B funding pursuit is described in a section they can read (§10.5, §10.9) or whether it is conditional on outreach (Q3, Q21).
- **Impact:** §1 establishes that the project is honest about what is conditional, but does not give a conditional-aware reader an action. The reader who wants to act is told to read more sections, not told where their engagement plugs in.
- **Recommendation:** In the F1 reader-intent map (or its equivalent), distinguish "to read" from "to engage." Example: "Partners evaluating facilitation: read §2.2 (audience), §8.6 (partnership approach), D0013 (consent and exit); engage at Q5 (partner outreach is open and the project is actively seeking conversations)."

### F5: Some sections are over-emphasized and others under-emphasized at navigation

- **Evidence:** §1's navigation paragraph gives Section 2 and Section 3 the first two slots and one clause each. Section 4 and Section 5 share a clause ("the architecture at the orientation and detailed levels respectively") despite §5 being the brief's longest section (lines 317–584, roughly 268 lines covering identity model, trust graph, recovery model, communications protocols, release security, UX principles, implementation language) and the load-bearing technical content. Section 8 ("operations and governance," 8 subsections, ~135 lines) gets the same single clause as Section 9 ("risks and limitations," 4 subsections, ~170 lines). Section 10 ("funding") gets one clause despite its phase-gate model being a major contribution of the brief.
- **Impact:** A technical reviewer is told §5 exists but not that it is the largest and most cryptographically detailed section in the brief. A funder is told §10 exists but not that the phase-gate framing is unusual and worth engaging with on its own terms. The flat enumeration treats all sections as equivalent contributions when they are not.
- **Recommendation:** Either rebalance the enumeration to signal the relative weight (acceptable but adds prose), or — preferred — drop the enumeration entirely in favor of the reader-intent map proposed in F1, which sidesteps the need to rank-order sections.

### F6: §1's own internal navigability is degraded by long paragraph blocks

- **Evidence:** §1 runs eight body paragraphs (lines 22, 24, 26, 28, 30, 32, 34) plus the framing note on line 20. Several paragraphs are very long — paragraph 1 (line 22, "What this is") is roughly 240 words in one block; paragraph 3 (line 26, "Architectural commitments") runs to roughly 350 words with four numbered commitments inline; paragraph 4 (line 28, "v1 scope and roadmap") is roughly 280 words. A reader who wants to find §1's audience claim (paragraph 2, line 24) without re-reading from the top has to scan paragraph openers for the bold lead-in — which works, but only because the bold leads are consistent. There is no internal table-of-contents for §1, no whitespace separation between thematic blocks, and the bold lead-ins are buried inside the prose blocks rather than treated as scannable headers.
- **Impact:** §1 is itself hard to navigate. A reader who skims §1, decides to engage, and later returns to find a specific claim (e.g., "what was the audience size estimate?" or "what's the Phase B funding number?") has to scan ~13 lines of dense prose. The bold lead-ins are the only navigational scaffolding and they are not visually separated.
- **Recommendation:** Consider whether §1's bold lead-ins should be treated as subsection headers (e.g., 1.1 What this is, 1.2 Threat tier and audience, 1.3 Architectural commitments, 1.4 v1 scope and roadmap, 1.5 Operational and funding posture, 1.6 What this brief is and is not, 1.7 Where to read next). This would make §1 scannable from the table of contents alone and let a returning reader jump to the specific claim. Cost: minor — the bold lead-ins are already structurally section-like; promoting them to headers formalizes what is already there.

## Minor findings

### F7: The closing meta-sentence about §1's working posture is navigationally inert

- **Evidence:** Line 34's closing sentence: "A reader who skims §1 alone should treat what §1 frames as the project's working posture; a reader who reads §1 and disagrees with the posture should find their objection elaborated and addressed (or honestly acknowledged as a limit) in the sections §1 references." This is a meta-claim about §1's epistemics, not a navigation aid. A reader who disagrees with §1's posture is not told _which section_ their disagreement will be addressed in.
- **Impact:** The sentence consumes navigation real estate without giving the reader a destination. It performs honesty rather than enables action.
- **Recommendation:** Either drop the sentence (the honesty claim is already made elsewhere in §1, e.g., line 32) or convert it to an actionable form: "Common objections and their sections: 'too narrow audience' → §2.2 and Q17; 'differentiation argument is thin' → §2.3 and Q18; 'volunteer cadence is unrealistic' → §8.1, D0008, Q13; 'funding model is speculative' → §10.7 and Q21." This converts a meta-statement into a navigation aid.

### F8: No cross-reference to Appendix A's decision-document pattern from §1

- **Evidence:** Appendix A.0 (line 1270) explains how to read decision documents and Appendix A.2 (line 1305) groups them thematically. §1 does not point to either. A reader new to the architecture-decision-record convention may not realize the decision documents are designed to be read as a coherent register.
- **Impact:** Minor — most sophisticated readers will infer the convention from the inline citations — but the brief's investment in Appendix A.2's thematic grouping goes unused by readers who don't make it to the appendix.
- **Recommendation:** When implementing F2, point §1's mention of decision documents at Appendix A.2 specifically rather than at the directory.

### F9: Open-questions filename treated inconsistently across §1

- **Evidence:** §1 links to `open-questions.md` three times (lines 24, 30, 32, 34). Two of those mentions are inline at point-of-claim (Q17, Q18). The other two are generic navigation pointers. The genres are mixed without signaling, so a reader sees four links to the same file and does not know which is for active-engagement reference (Q5, Q18) versus catalog browsing.
- **Impact:** Minor — repeated identical-target links can look like cruft rather than load-bearing navigation.
- **Recommendation:** When converting Q5/Q18 references to active-engagement signaling per F3, distinguish those uses from the catalog-browsing pointer.

## Patterns

Three patterns explain the navigation gaps:

1. **The brief is navigationally citation-heavy but structurally TOC-light.** §1 carries 11 D0001–D0014 citations and 5 Q-number citations inline (very good for traceability at the point of claim) but its only structural navigation aid is a TOC-in-prose paragraph (line 34). The brief over-invests in citation density and under-invests in reader-intent mapping. The honest fix is not to add more citations but to convert §1's closing paragraph from enumeration to mapping.

2. **§1 treats decisions and open questions as homogeneous registers rather than as differentiable instruments.** The point-of-claim citations work because the local context distinguishes (D0006 for cryptographic envelope, Q18 for differentiation evidence). The navigation paragraph flattens this — "D0001–D0014" and "Q1–Q26" as ranges — losing the per-item load-bearing information §1 itself established inline. This is a missed opportunity: §1 has done the work of identifying which decisions and questions matter most; the navigation paragraph just doesn't surface it.

3. **§1 confuses orientation with completeness.** The brief's framing at line 20 says §1 is "a single-section orientation that lets a busy reader decide whether to engage." A busy reader does not need every section enumerated; they need to know which 1–3 sections answer their specific question. §1 currently does the orientation job (the bold lead-ins do this well) and then immediately undoes it by giving every reader the full structure. The fix is to commit to orientation as a job-to-be-done and let the table of contents at the top of the brief carry the structural-enumeration job.

## 150-word summary

§1's "Where to read next" paragraph is a serial table-of-contents recitation, not a navigation aid that maps reader intent to sections. Four named reader populations — funders, partners, researchers, pilot candidates — are left to read top-to-bottom. Decision documents D0001–D0014 are cited inline at point-of-claim (good) but flattened in navigation as an undifferentiated set; the brief does not surface which decisions are load-bearing for which argument or point to Appendix A.2's thematic grouping. The open-questions tracker is described as a generic Q1–Q26 catalog despite §1 itself naming Q18 as "the central grant-case question" and Q5 as the partner-engagement hook. §1 does not distinguish what readers can act on now (read §X) from what depends on outreach (engage at Q5). §5 and §10 are under-weighted relative to their load-bearing role. §1's own internal navigability suffers from long unstructured paragraph blocks; promoting the bold lead-ins to subsection headers would help.
